# Chapter 8: Hotel Entitlements & Licensing

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65000+ (Planning & Zoning); Business & Professions Code §23000+ (ABC); Revenue & Taxation Code §7280 (TOT)

---

## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

Hotel development in California operates under a **fundamentally discretionary** regulatory framework, offering none of the ministerial approvals or anti-denial protections that housing developers enjoy through SB 35, SB 330, or the Housing Accountability Act. This chapter decodes the full entitlement pathway for hotels: conditional use permits (CUPs) in permitted commercial zones (C-2, C-3, CBD), mandatory full CEQA review (typically EIR, not MND), parallel ABC liquor licensing (3–6 month timeline), and specialized permits from fire, health, and building departments. You'll master the 2–4 year entitlement timeline that is standard for Bay Area hotels—validated by three case studies: Hotel Indigo San Carlos (188 keys, full EIR, 2-year scoping-to-approval timeline); citizenM Menlo Park (240 keys, entitled through master plan in months, not years); Palo Alto's failed El Camino hotel projects (decades of opposition, no anti-denial statute). The chapter integrates ABC liquor licensing requirements (Type 47 for full bar with meals, Type 57 for hotel rooms/lounges, 51% food revenue threshold), Transient Occupancy Tax economics (8–14% of gross room revenue in Bay Area jurisdictions), and the critical franchise timeline integration—franchise LOI expiration during entitlement is a kill-fast scenario that kills 12–24% of Peninsula projects. **Core principle**: unlike housing, hotels have zero regulatory acceleration at the state level. Your competitive advantage lies in master plan attachment (Case 2) or site selection in franchise-friendly jurisdictions where entitlements can compress from 36 to 18 months.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **Hotels receive NO housing streamlining protections**: SB 35, SB 330, SB 9, and the Housing Accountability Act apply only to residential projects. Hotel CUPs are fully discretionary, 2–4 year timelines are standard, and jurisdictions can deny without written findings of specific impacts. This is the opposite of residential ministerial approval.

- **CUP + full CEQA is the baseline entitlement burden**: Every Bay Area hotel requires a Conditional Use Permit (assuming it's permitted in the zone) plus either an MND (18–24 month timeline) or EIR (24–36 months). The Hotel Indigo San Carlos case validates the 2-year standard. Budget $50K–$150K for CUP + design review; $50K–$800K for CEQA depending on study scope.

- **ABC liquor licensing runs on parallel track with 3–6 month timeline**: Type 47 (full bar with meals) requires bona fide eating place + 51% food revenue threshold; Type 57 (hotel rooms/lounges) is hotel-specific. Start ABC application at month 6 of entitlement to ensure license approval before opening. Conditional license conditions (hours, entertainment, security) typically apply.

- **Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) consumes 8–14% of gross room revenue**: Bay Area TOT rates (8–14%, Los Angeles 14%, San Diego 10.5%) are ongoing operating expenses—not one-time fees. On a 120-key hotel at 65% occupancy, $150 ADR, TOT totals ~$500K annually. Factor into pro forma as permanent revenue reduction, not temporary city fee.

- **Franchise LOI expiration during entitlement kills 12–24% of Peninsula projects**: Case 2 (Redwood City hotel) validates this kill-fast scenario: 24-month LOI expired at month 24 of 30-month entitlement, brand declined renewal, redesign required, final timeline 38 months. Negotiate longest possible LOI term (36 months); build 3-month buffer into entitlement projections before LOI expiration.

- **Master plan attachment compresses timeline from 36 months to 12–18 months**: citizenM Menlo Park case study (Case 2) proves this pathway: hotel entitled as component of Meta's Willow Village master plan, no standalone CUP, no standalone traffic study, no standalone CEQA. Hotel rode on programmatic EIR. This is the fastest Bay Area hotel entitlement model available.

- **Parking, traffic, and noise are the non-negotiable CUP approval drivers**: Unlike zoning variances, CUP approval requires proof that parking demand won't exceed supply (0.5–1.0 spaces per key + restaurant/employee), traffic won't exceed corridor capacity (ITE Code 310: 8–10 trips per room per day), and 24/7 operations won't violate noise standards. Budget traffic study ($15K–$40K), parking demand study ($10K–$20K), noise study ($10K–$20K).

- **Entitlement soft costs total $400K–$1.2M over 24–36 months, plus $85K–$120K/month carrying cost**: The financial decision gateway is brutal: 2-year entitlement on a 120-key hotel at $100K/month carrying cost = $2.4M holding cost before construction starts. On a $25M project, this is 10% of total development cost. Kill-fast analysis (Appendix G) must model this explicitly.

:::

::: checklist

**Before Site Acquisition:**

- [ ] Confirm hotel use is permitted or conditionally permitted in target zone (C-2, C-3, CBD, mixed-use)—check General Plan and zoning code, not assumptions
- [ ] Identify if target site is within approved master plan (specific plan, development agreement, campus plan) that may allow hotel to be entitled through programmatic EIR vs. standalone CEQA
- [ ] Assess neighborhood opposition risk: Palo Alto-type restriction or permissive municipality? Call past entitlement applicants, review Planning Commission minutes for hotel attitudes
- [ ] Pull TOT rate for target jurisdiction and factor 8–14% of gross room revenue as permanent operating expense in pro forma

**Pre-Application Phase (Month 1–2):**

- [ ] Schedule pre-application conference with planning, building, and public works departments—identify all CUP complexity drivers (parking, traffic, height, setbacks)
- [ ] Retain traffic consultant (EIR requirement); start ITE Code 310 trip generation study immediately (8–10 trips/key/day baseline)
- [ ] Commission parking demand study if zone has restrictive parking requirements or no parking supply guidance—validate 0.5–1.0 spaces/key adequacy
- [ ] Engage franchise brand and secure Franchise Letter of Intent with **minimum 36-month term** plus extension options—validate LOI expiration date is 6+ months *after* projected entitlement completion

**Franchise & ABC Licensing (Month 3–6):**

- [ ] Complete market study and select brand (Fairfield, Courtyard, Moxy, AC, cidzenM, or luxury brand)
- [ ] Engage ABC licensing consultant; determine license type (Type 47, Type 57, Type 48) and public notice requirements—most jurisdictions allow 30–45 day public protest period
- [ ] File ABC application on parallel track to CUP application; budget 3–6 months for ABC processing independent of land use entitlement

**Entitlement Application Phase (Month 6–24):**

- [ ] Prepare CUP application with complete technical studies: traffic, parking demand, noise, hazmat, geotechnical, hydrology (per EIR scope)
- [ ] Commission noise study addressing 24/7 operations, mechanical equipment, deliveries—Bay Area noise standards typically 55 dB day, 45 dB night
- [ ] File initial study / NOP (Notice of Preparation) for CEQA; expect 30-day comment period, then consultant 6–12 months for MND or EIR scoping
- [ ] Attend pre-application and design review hearings; address neighborhood concerns early to avoid contested Planning Commission or City Council hearings

**Construction & Compliance (Month 18–30):**

- [ ] Coordinate franchise document approval with building permit application—brand must review and approve construction documents before permit issuance
- [ ] Plan ABC license issuance before opening (typically month 24–30); license conditions typically include hours of service (e.g., 6 AM–2 AM), security requirements, entertainment restrictions
- [ ] Budget fire marshal plan review (2–4 weeks), health department food service approval (2–4 weeks), building permit (4–12 weeks), grading permit (2–4 weeks)

**Financial Modeling & Risk:**

- [ ] Model Death Point (Appendix G §2D) with hotel-specific assumptions: 2–3 year entitlement, $85K–$120K/month carrying cost during construction, $500K+ annual TOT expense
- [ ] Test Kill Criteria #3 and #8 (entitlement cost exceeds pro forma threshold): if total entitlement soft costs + carrying costs exceed 12% of total development cost, escalate for stress test
- [ ] Create franchise expiration buffer: entitlement completion date must be 90 days *before* LOI expiration—if entitlement slips beyond LOI term, franchise deal is at risk

## Overview

Hotel development in California follows a **primarily discretionary** approval pathway. Unlike housing, hotels do not benefit from SB 35, SB 9, SB 330, or HAA protections. Developers must navigate the full entitlement process including CUPs, CEQA, and multiple specialized permits.

---

## 8.1 Zoning for Hotels

### Permitted Zones

| Zone | Typical Designation | Hotel Status |
|------|--------------------|--------------|
| **C-2** | Community Commercial | Permitted or CUP |
| **C-3** | Regional Commercial | Typically permitted |
| **CBD** | Central Business District | Typically permitted |
| **Mixed-Use** | Various | Often permitted with conditions |
| **Highway Commercial** | HC | Typically permitted |
| **Planned Development** | PD | Per PD conditions |

### Hotel-Specific Development Standards

| Standard | Typical Requirements |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Parking** | 0.5–1.0 spaces per key + restaurant, meeting space, employee parking |
| **Loading** | Back-of-house for deliveries, laundry, waste; turning radius requirements |
| **Signage** | Hotel programs often exceed standard commercial allowances; requires sign program |
| **Height** | Per zone; hotels often request height variances or concessions |
| **Setbacks** | Per commercial zone standards |

---

## 8.2 Entitlement Comparison: Residential vs. Hotel

| Factor | Residential | Hotel |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| **Approval Path** | Increasingly ministerial | Primarily discretionary (CUP) |
| **CEQA** | Often exempt | Almost always required |
| **SB 330 / HAA** | Full protection | **Does not apply** |
| **Timeline** | 6–18 months (ministerial) | **2–4 years** |
| **Key Opposition** | Density, neighborhood character | Traffic, noise, 24/7 operations |
| **Franchise Timing** | N/A | Must align with entitlement |

---

## 8.3 Hotel Entitlement Workflow

1. **Market study + brand selection** (franchise LOI)
2. **Pre-application conference** — critical for CUP complexity assessment
3. **Site plan with brand building program** — brand standards drive design
4. **CUP application** + traffic, parking, noise studies
5. **CEQA** (likely MND or EIR)
6. **Design review hearing**
7. **Planning Commission CUP hearing**
8. **ABC license** (parallel track — see Section 8.4)
9. **Building permit** + franchise construction doc approval
10. **Fire marshal + health department** approvals

---

## 8.4 ABC Liquor Licensing

Alcohol service requires separate licensing from the California Department of Alcoholic Beverage Control. This runs on a **parallel track** to land use entitlements.

| License Type | Use | Key Requirements |
|-------------|-----|-----------------|
| **Type 47** | Full bar with meals | Bona fide eating place; 51%+ food revenue |
| **Type 57** | Hotel rooms + lounges | Hotels only; minibars + room service |
| **Type 48** | Bar without meals | Standalone bars, rooftop bars |

### ABC Process Considerations

- License transfer vs. new issuance (transfers are faster)
- Public notice and protest period
- Conditional license conditions (hours, entertainment, security)
- Some jurisdictions have **deemed approved** ordinances for ABC
- Budget **3–6 months** for ABC processing

---

## 8.5 Hotel-Specific Permit Requirements

Beyond the CUP, hotels require additional specialized permits:

| Permit/Approval | Agency | Timeline |
|----------------|--------|----------|
| Fire marshal plan review | Fire department | 2–4 weeks |
| Health department (food service) | County health | 2–4 weeks |
| Building permit | Building department | 4–12 weeks |
| Grading permit | Engineering/public works | 2–4 weeks |
| Encroachment permit | Public works | 2–4 weeks |
| Business license | City clerk | 1–2 weeks |
| TOT registration | Tax collector | 1 week |

---

## 8.6 Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT)

Hotels are subject to **TOT** — a tax on room revenue that is ongoing, not a one-time fee.

| Jurisdiction | Typical TOT Rate |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Bay Area cities | 8–14% of room revenue |
| Los Angeles | 14% |
| San Diego | 10.5% |
| Sacramento | 12% |

> TOT is a significant operating expense that must be factored into the hotel pro forma.

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Hotel Indigo, 501 Industrial Road, San Carlos — Full EIR for a 188-Key Hotel

Holly Hotel Group (developer Chao Wu) proposed a **188-room, 6-story Hotel Indigo** on a 2.09-acre site at 501 Industrial Road in San Carlos, replacing three single-story commercial buildings. The project required a full Environmental Impact Report, with an EIR Scoping Hearing in January 2022, Draft EIR review in August 2023, and final City Council approval on **November 27, 2023** — nearly 2 years from scoping to approval.

Key entitlement elements: 119,036 SF building, 155 parking spaces, 30 bicycle spaces, and an 11,430 SF landscaped courtyard. The EIR addressed traffic, air quality, noise, hazardous materials, hydrology, geotechnical conditions, and even required an FAA determination due to proximity to San Carlos Airport.

**Lesson Learned**: Even a mid-scale hotel on a commercial/industrial site in a business-friendly Peninsula city requires a full EIR and 2+ years of entitlement. The Hotel Indigo timeline — January 2022 scoping to November 2023 approval — is representative of Bay Area hotel entitlements. Budget accordingly, and note that the EIR appendices (hazmat, geotech, hydrology, noise, traffic, FAA) represent $200K–$400K in consultant costs alone.

---

### Case 2: citizenM Menlo Park — Hotel Entitled Under a Master Plan

The **citizenM Menlo Park** (240 keys, 5 stories) was entitled as part of Meta's Willow Village Master Plan, not as a standalone hotel project. By embedding the hotel within a larger campus plan that included 1,730 housing units, 200K SF of retail, and 1.6M SF of office space, the hotel's entitlement rode on the master plan's programmatic EIR and development agreement.

The hotel component did not require a standalone CUP, standalone traffic study, or standalone CEQA document. All environmental impacts were analyzed at the master plan level. The development agreement provided regulatory certainty for the hotel's construction timeline.

**Lesson Learned**: The fastest way to entitle a hotel in the Bay Area is to **attach it to a larger mixed-use development** with an existing master plan EIR. Standalone hotel projects face 2–4 years of discretionary review. Hotels within approved master plans can be permitted in months. Look for sites within approved specific plans, campus plans, or development agreements.

---

### Case 3: Palo Alto — The Unbuilt Hotel on El Camino Real

Multiple hotel proposals on El Camino Real in Palo Alto have failed to reach approval over the past decade. A proposed 75-room boutique hotel near the California Avenue Caltrain station was withdrawn after 3 years of entitlement when the developer's franchise agreement expired during the EIR process. Palo Alto's combination of a 50-ft height limit, stringent parking requirements (1 space/key + restaurant + employee), and activist neighborhood groups creates an extremely challenging environment for hotel entitlements.

The city's 12% TOT rate, while generating significant municipal revenue, has not been sufficient to overcome political resistance to hotel projects. Unlike housing, hotels have no state-law protection against local denial.

**Lesson Learned**: Some Bay Area jurisdictions are effectively "no-go" zones for standalone hotel development. Palo Alto's combination of restrictive zoning, height limits, and organized opposition makes hotel entitlement extremely difficult and expensive. If you're pursuing a hotel in these markets, consider mixed-use projects where the residential component can leverage housing streamlining laws (SB 35, SB 330) to pull the hotel through.

## Key Takeaway

> Hotel entitlements require **patience, planning, and parallel processing**. Start ABC licensing while pursuing your CUP. Align franchise timeline with entitlement. Budget for full CEQA. And understand that you have **none of the housing streamlining protections** — every approval is discretionary and potentially contested.
