## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

Entitlement is the highest-risk development phase because substantial capital is deployed before revenue certainty exists. This chapter provides end-to-end workflows for residential and hotel projects, plus the financial analysis framework that integrates with Appendix G's 3-level decision methodology. Residential pathways range from ministerial (SB 35 multifamily in RHNA-deficient cities = 3–6 months, no CEQA) to discretionary (EIR required = 24–36 months, significant carrying costs). Hotel entitlement is inherently discretionary, requiring Conditional Use Permit (CUP) with design review, traffic/parking/noise studies, and 20–28 month timeline. The Permit Streamlining Act (Government Code §65920–65964) imposes hard statutory deadlines that are your enforcement levers: completeness review (30 days), EIR project decision (180 days), SB 35 approval (90–180 days depending on unit count). If cities miss deadlines, projects are "deemed approved" by operation of law. SB 330 preliminary applications (filed at site control) lock in fees and development standards before mid-entitlement increases of $5K–$10K/unit—a single one-page filing can save $600K–$935K across multiple projects. Impact fees vary wildly by jurisdiction and project type ($30K–$100K/unit residential, $10K–$35K/key hotels); concurrent filing of building permits while entitlement proceeds saves 4–8 months on critical path, worth $340K–$600K in carrying cost reduction on typical Bay Area project. The Palo Alto/Redwood City case study demonstrates the $2.8M entitlement premium difference between discretionary (28 months) and ministerial (5 months) pathways on identical 80-unit buildings. **Core principle**: jurisdiction selection determines entitlement timeline and cost more than any other factor. Know your pathway before site acquisition.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **SB 35 ministerial pathway compresses entitlement from 28 months to 5–6 months**: Multifamily projects in RHNA-deficient jurisdictions (most Bay Area cities) bypass CEQA, public hearings, and discretionary denial in favor of objective standards compliance. Check HCD's annual determination letter before committing to jurisdiction. If eligible, timeline is 90–180 days vs. 24–36 months for discretionary—equivalent to $2.44M in carrying cost savings on typical Bay Area multifamily.

- **SB 330 preliminary application locks fees before mid-entitlement increases**: One-page filing (site address, unit count, footprint sketch) at site control freezes development fees, exactions, building standards for 3 years. Typical Bay Area cities increase fees $4K–$8K/unit during entitlement. Three projects filed across San Jose, Mountain View, Sunnyvale = $2.375M fee savings total. Highest-ROI activity in residential development—takes 1 hour, saves $600K–$935K per project.

- **Jurisdiction selection is the $2.8M question**: Identical 80-unit multifamily in Palo Alto (discretionary, 28 months, $3.42M entitlement cost) vs. Redwood City (SB 35 ministerial, 5 months, $615K entitlement cost). $2.8M difference is purely jurisdiction choice, not site quality or building type. Before site acquisition, map entitlement pathway by jurisdiction. "Cheaper" land in restrictive city costs more in total development cost.

- **Concurrent building permit filing saves 4–8 months on critical path**: File building permit plans while entitlement proceedings continue (not after approval). Risk: plan revisions if conditions change. Upside: $340K–$600K carrying cost savings. Expected-value calculation strongly favors concurrent filing in most scenarios. Requires close coordination between entitlement and design teams, but compresses Death Point (§Appendix G) significantly.

- **Permit Streamlining Act deadlines are enforcement levers**: Cities have 30 days completeness review, 180 days EIR project decision, 90–180 days SB 35 approval (by statute). If city misses deadline, project is "deemed approved" by operation of law. Track every deadline meticulously in project calendar; send formal notice if city misses. This is your leverage to accelerate sluggish reviews.

- **Impact fee variation across jurisdictions creates $1M+ swings**: Residential impact fees: $30K–$100K/unit depending on city, affordable component, density bonus eligibility. Hotel impact fees: $10K–$35K/key plus ongoing TOT (8–14% room revenue annually). Same building type in different cities has vastly different fee burden. Include fee estimates in Level 1 Napkin Test; lock in via SB 330 if residential.

- **Development agreements lock regulatory certainty for 10–25 years**: For large, phased projects where regulatory risk is high, negotiate development agreement with city (requires public hearing, discretionary, subject to CEQA). Developer provides enhanced public benefits (affordable housing, parks, infrastructure); city provides certainty that entitlement conditions won't change. Useful when current entitlement conditions are favorable and you want to lock them in before city planning changes.

- **Hotel entitlement requires 20–28 months and full discretionary process**: CUP application with traffic, parking, noise studies; design review hearing; planning commission CUP hearing; ABC liquor license (parallel track). No ministerial pathways exist for hotels. Budget full CEQA (MND or EIR), design review process, and political risk. Impact fees per key are lower than residential per unit, but TOT obligation ($1.4M/year on 188-key hotel) is ongoing burden residential doesn't face.

:::

::: checklist

**Residential Entitlement Pathway:**

- [ ] Determine ministerial vs. discretionary pathway before site acquisition
- [ ] If multifamily residential: check HCD SB 35 annual determination letter (confirms RHNA-deficient status)
- [ ] If eligible for SB 35: 90–180 day timeline, no CEQA, objective standards compliance only
- [ ] If discretionary: budget 24–36 months, CEQA (likely MND or EIR), design review process, political risk
- [ ] For SB 9 (single-family lot splits + ADUs): 60–90 day ministerial timeline, no CEQA

**Site Control & Preliminary Application (Week 1–2):**

- [ ] File SB 330 preliminary application immediately upon site control (day 1 if possible)
- [ ] Preliminary application requires only: site address, unit count, approximate footprint sketch
- [ ] SB 330 locks fees and development standards for 3 years
- [ ] Estimated fee savings: $600K–$935K depending on jurisdiction
- [ ] Estimated time to file: 30 minutes
- [ ] Have city planning staff confirm preliminary application receipt and vesting date

**Pre-Application Conference (Week 2–3):**

- [ ] Request pre-application conference with planning director or entitlement planner
- [ ] Presentation: project summary, land use, unit count, parking, height/massing, affordable component (if applicable)
- [ ] Deliverable: written summary from city confirming requirements, identified issues, recommended pathway
- [ ] For hotels: confirm CUP pathway and required studies (traffic, parking, noise, design review)
- [ ] Document any oral feedback in writing before proceeding with design

**Environmental & Technical Studies (Month 2–4):**

- [ ] Determine CEQA pathway: exempt, categorical exemption, ND, MND, or EIR
- [ ] If exempt or categorical exemption: budget 6–12 months
- [ ] If MND required: budget 12–18 months (environmental assessment + public review)
- [ ] If EIR required: budget 18–36 months (full environmental review + alternatives analysis)
- [ ] For hotels: order traffic impact study, parking study, noise study (required for CUP)

**Application Submittal (Month 4–6):**

- [ ] Submit complete application with all required studies, architectural drawings, technical documentation
- [ ] Permit Streamlining Act: city has 30-day completeness deadline
- [ ] Track completeness review deadline in project calendar
- [ ] If city requests additional information on day 25: deadline resets for another 30 days (respond quickly to avoid delays)

**Public Hearings (Month 8–18, depending on pathway):**

- [ ] Ministerial (SB 35): no public hearings required
- [ ] Discretionary residential: design review + planning commission hearing (1–3 hearings)
- [ ] Hotels: design review + planning commission CUP hearing (2–3 hearings minimum)
- [ ] SB 330 limits housing projects to maximum 5 hearings
- [ ] Budget contingency for appeal periods (typically 10 calendar days)

**Conditions of Approval Negotiation:**

- [ ] Conditions of approval are negotiated at hearing (sometimes before, if city willing)
- [ ] Typical residential conditions: design revisions, traffic calming, parking management, affordable housing monitoring
- [ ] Typical hotel conditions: operating hours, noise limits, delivery times, valet parking, community relations
- [ ] Red-flag conditions: those that change project economics (height reduction, unit loss, increased parking requirement)
- [ ] Request written conditions before leaving hearing; clarify any ambiguous language

**Permit Streamlining Act Calendar:**

- [ ] Day 0: Application deemed complete (or city requests additional information)
- [ ] Day 30: Completeness review deadline (PSA §65943)
- [ ] Day 60–180: Project decision deadline (varies by CEQA pathway, §65950):
  - Day 60 for categorical exemption or ND/MND projects
  - Day 180 for EIR projects
  - Day 90–180 for SB 35 (90 days for <150 units, 180 for 150+ units)
- [ ] If city misses deadline: project is "deemed approved" by operation of law
- [ ] Document all deadline misses in writing; escalate to city manager if pattern emerges

**Concurrent Building Permit Filing (Month 8–12 during entitlement):**

- [ ] Begin building permit design while entitlement still in progress (not after approval)
- [ ] File building permit simultaneously with entitlement approval (or 2–4 months before, if confident in entitlement outcome)
- [ ] Risk mitigation: coordinate with entitlement attorney regarding condition change risk
- [ ] Expected value: $340K–$600K carrying cost savings vs. $30K–$60K plan revision cost
- [ ] For projects with clear entitlement path: concurrent filing is nearly always ROI-positive

**Building Permit Coordination (Month 12–18):**

- [ ] Plan check with building department (4–8 weeks)
- [ ] Parallel submittals with fire marshal, public works, stormwater, water/sewer districts
- [ ] Use phased permits if available: grading/foundation permit before full building permit (start site work 2–3 months earlier)
- [ ] Track AB 1114 plan check deadlines (30 days for <25 units, 60 days for 26+ units)
- [ ] If city misses AB 1114 deadline: applicant can use third-party plan check at city expense

**Hotel Entitlement Specifics:**

- [ ] No ministerial pathways for hotels (full discretionary process required)
- [ ] Timeline: 20–28 months from application to building permit (vs. 5–6 months SB 35 residential)
- [ ] Required studies: market study, traffic impact, parking analysis, noise study, design review
- [ ] Parallel track: ABC liquor license application (month 6, concurrent with entitlement)
- [ ] Timeline for ABC license: 6–12 months (separate process, often faster than building permit)
- [ ] CUP conditions typically include: operating hours, delivery windows, valet parking requirements, community relations

**Fee & Financial Tracking:**

- [ ] Impact fees (residential): budget $40K–$100K/unit depending on jurisdiction and inclusionary ordinance
- [ ] Impact fees (hotel): budget $10K–$35K/key
- [ ] SB 330 preliminary application savings: document fees locked at application date
- [ ] TOT (hotels): budget 8–14% of gross room revenue as ongoing operating expense
- [ ] School fees: statutory cap ($4–$5/SF), no negotiation available
- [ ] Park/Quimby fees: potentially negotiable via land dedication alternative

**Financial Stress Testing (Integration with Appendix G):**

- [ ] Level 1 Napkin Test: use impact fee estimates from §16.3; confirm projects pencils at maximum zoning buildout
- [ ] Level 2 Development Pro Forma: include impact fees as line items in Sources & Uses; include SB 330 fee vesting date
- [ ] Level 3 Stress Test: model timeline delay scenarios (6, 12, 18 months); calculate carrying cost impact
- [ ] Sensitivity analysis: vary impact fees ±20%, entitlement timeline ±6 months, interest rates ±100 basis points
- [ ] If concurrent filing strategy used: stress test impact of plan revision costs if conditions change

## Part D: Development Agreements

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65864–65869.5

A development agreement is a **contract between a developer and a local government** that locks in the developer's right to develop under current regulations for a specified term (typically 10–25 years).

### When to Use

- Large, phased projects where regulatory certainty is essential
- Projects requiring significant infrastructure investment
- Situations where current entitlement conditions are favorable

### Structure

- Developer provides **enhanced public benefits** beyond normal conditions
- Government provides **regulatory certainty** for the agreement term
- Agreement is recorded against the property and runs with the land
- Requires public hearing, is discretionary, and subject to CEQA

## Part G: Concurrent Filing & Building Permit Coordination

### 16.5 Compressing the Critical Path

The entitlement-to-construction timeline has a hidden bottleneck: the **sequential handoff** between planning approval and building permit issuance. Most developers wait for final entitlement before submitting building permit plans. This creates a 4–8 month dead zone where the project generates zero progress but full carrying costs.

### Concurrent Filing Strategy

Filing building permit plans **while entitlement is still in progress** can save 3–6 months on the critical path. The risk is plan revision if conditions of approval change — but the expected-value calculation strongly favors concurrent filing in most scenarios.

| Phase | Sequential Approach | Concurrent Approach | Time Saved |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Entitlement review | Months 1–12 | Months 1–12 | — |
| Building permit design | Months 13–16 | Months 8–12 (overlap) | 4 months |
| Plan check cycle 1 | Months 17–19 | Months 13–15 | 2 months |
| Plan check corrections | Months 20–22 | Months 16–18 | 2 months |
| Permit issuance | Month 23 | Month 19 | **4 months** |

### Building Permit Coordination Checklist

The building permit phase involves multiple departments that must sign off before issuance. Coordinating these in parallel — not sequentially — is critical.

| Department | Submittal Requirements | Typical Review Time | Parallel? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Building Division** | Full plan set, Title 24 energy calcs, structural calcs | 4–8 weeks | Primary track |
| **Fire Marshal** | Fire sprinkler plans, fire access, alarm system | 3–6 weeks | Yes — file with building |
| **Public Works** | Grading plan, encroachment permit, utility connections | 4–8 weeks | Yes — file with building |
| **Stormwater (C.3)** | Stormwater management plan, BMP sizing | 3–6 weeks | Yes — file with building |
| **Water District** | Will-serve letter, connection application | 2–4 weeks | Yes — can file during entitlement |
| **Sanitary District** | Sewer connection permit, capacity verification | 2–4 weeks | Yes — can file during entitlement |
| **School District** | School fee calculation | 1–2 weeks | Yes — pay at permit issuance |
| **Green Building** | CalGreen compliance, energy modeling (if required) | 2–4 weeks | Yes — submit with plans |

### 16.6 Plan Check Acceleration Tactics

| Tactic | How It Works | Expected Savings |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-submittal meeting** | Meet with plan check supervisor before submitting; walk through complex items | Reduces correction cycles by 1 round |
| **Over-the-counter corrections** | For minor corrections, request OTC review instead of formal resubmittal | Saves 2–4 weeks per correction |
| **Expedited plan check** | Some jurisdictions offer paid expedited review (1.5–2x fees) | Cuts plan check time 50% |
| **Third-party plan check** | Use a city-approved third-party plan check firm | Saves 4–8 weeks vs. city review |
| **Phased permits** | Get grading/foundation permit before full building permit | Allows site work to begin 2–3 months earlier |

### AB 1114 Plan Check Deadlines (Effective 2025)

California now requires building departments to return **comprehensive plan check comments** within statutory deadlines:

| Project Size | First Review Deadline | Subsequent Reviews |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **25 units or fewer** | 30 business days | 30 business days |
| **26+ units** | 60 business days | 60 business days |

> **Enforcement**: If the jurisdiction misses the deadline, the applicant may use a third-party plan check firm at the jurisdiction's expense. Track these deadlines as aggressively as PSA deadlines.

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Palo Alto vs. Redwood City — Entitlement Timeline Comparison

Two identical projects — 80-unit multifamily buildings — were proposed simultaneously in Palo Alto and Redwood City by the same development team. Both projects were on similarly zoned parcels (multi-family residential).

#### Side-by-Side Comparison

| Factor | Palo Alto (Discretionary) | Redwood City (SB 35 Ministerial) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Entitlement pathway** | Discretionary — full design review | SB 35 ministerial — objective standards only |
| **Pre-application** | Neighborhood concerns flagged; architectural review required | Confirmed SB 35 eligibility (city behind RHNA) |
| **Environmental review** | MND challenged by neighbors → upgraded to focused EIR (traffic, aesthetics) | None — CEQA exempt under SB 35 |
| **Public hearings** | 3 hearings before ARB/Planning Commission | None — no public hearings |
| **Conditions of approval** | 14 conditions imposed | Objective standards compliance only |
| **Affordable component** | Per city inclusionary ordinance | 10% affordable (SB 35 minimum) |
| **Total timeline** | **28 months** (application → building permit) | **5 months** (application → building permit) |
| **Total soft costs** | **$450K** (environmental, legal, design revisions, consultants) | **$85K** (design, application fees) |

#### Financial Impact: The $2.8M Question

| Cost Component | Palo Alto | Redwood City | Difference |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Land acquisition | $13M | $13M | — |
| Monthly carrying cost | ~$106K/mo (loan interest + taxes + insurance) | ~$106K/mo | — |
| Entitlement timeline | 28 months | 5 months | **23 months** |
| **Carrying cost during entitlement** | **$2.97M** | **$530K** | **$2.44M** |
| **Soft costs** | **$450K** | **$85K** | **$365K** |
| **Total entitlement cost** | **$3.42M** | **$615K** | **$2.8M** |

Same developer, same building type, same land cost — **$2.8M entitlement premium** for choosing the wrong jurisdiction (see Introduction: The $2.8M Question).

**Lesson Learned**: Jurisdiction selection is the single highest-impact decision in Bay Area development. A project that takes 5 months in Redwood City takes 28 months in Palo Alto — a $2.8M entitlement premium on the same building type. Before committing to a site, map out the entitlement pathway by jurisdiction. The "cheaper" land in a restrictive city may cost far more in total development cost than "expensive" land in a streamlined city.

### Case 2: Hotel Indigo San Carlos — Impact Fee Analysis

The Hotel Indigo (188 keys) at 501 Industrial Road illustrates Bay Area hotel fee structures. Based on San Carlos's fee schedules and the project's 119,036 SF building:

| Fee Category | Estimated Amount | Basis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| School impact fees | ~$78K | $0.66/SF x 119,036 SF |
| Traffic impact fees | ~$564K | ~$3K/key x 188 keys |
| Sewer/water connection | ~$940K | ~$5K/key x 188 keys |
| Building permit fees | ~$350K | Estimated at plan check |
| **Total one-time fees** | **~$1.93M** | **~$10.3K/key** |
| TOT (ongoing) | ~$1.4M/year | 12% x estimated $12M gross room revenue |

By comparison, a 188-unit multifamily residential project on the same site would face approximately $40K–$60K/unit in impact fees ($7.5M–$11.3M total) — significantly higher per-unit costs. However, the residential project would benefit from SB 330 fee vesting and potential density bonus concessions.

**Lesson Learned**: Hotel impact fees per key ($10K–$25K) are typically much lower than residential fees per unit ($40K–$100K) in Bay Area jurisdictions. However, hotels face ongoing TOT obligations (8–14% of room revenue annually) that residential projects do not. When comparing hotel vs. residential on the same site, model the total tax/fee burden over 10 years, including TOT, to get an accurate comparison.

### Case 3: Bay Area — SB 330 Preliminary Application Fee Savings Across Jurisdictions

A developer filed SB 330 preliminary applications for three projects simultaneously across Bay Area jurisdictions:

| Jurisdiction | Units | Fee Increase During Entitlement | Savings from SB 330 Vesting |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **San Jose** | 120 units | Parks: +$4K/unit; traffic: +$2K/unit | **$720K saved** |
| **Mountain View** | 85 units | Parks: +$8K/unit; sewer: +$3K/unit | **$935K saved** |
| **Sunnyvale** | 60 units | Below-market-rate in-lieu: +$12K/unit | **$720K saved** |

Total savings across three projects: **$2.375M** — from filing three one-page preliminary applications.

**Lesson Learned**: The SB 330 preliminary application is the highest-ROI activity in California residential development. Bay Area cities increase fees regularly and often dramatically. Filing a preliminary application costs essentially nothing and can save six or seven figures. Develop a practice of filing SB 330 preliminary applications within the first week of site control on every residential project.
