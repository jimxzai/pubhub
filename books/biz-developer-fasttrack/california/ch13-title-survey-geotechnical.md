# Chapter 13: Title, Survey & Geotechnical Engineering

**Statutory Reference**: Insurance Code §12340+ (Title Insurance); Public Resources Code §2690+ (Seismic Hazards Mapping Act); California Building Code §1613 (Earthquake Loads)

---

## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

Title, survey, and geotechnical investigations form the deal-evaluation trifecta that separates competent developers from those blindsided by hidden liabilities. This chapter teaches you to read and interpret each discipline critically, catching site constraints before capital is committed. Title reports reveal encumbrances and CC&Rs that may fundamentally constrain development potential; surveys physically map those constraints onto your site plan; geotechnical reports quantify subsurface conditions and foundation costs. The Bay Area presents specific challenges: alluvial soils in Santa Clara Valley with liquefaction hazards requiring $8K–$15K/unit for ground improvement and enhanced foundations; high water tables near creeks triggering $200K–$500K in dewatering and waterproofing; expansive clays in South Bay requiring post-tensioned slabs and $6K–$15K/unit foundation premiums. Real case studies—Menlo Park's $1.4M liquefaction cost shock, Palo Alto's 14-month easement negotiation, San Jose's expansive clay surprises—demonstrate that skipping due diligence costs exponentially more than the $3K–$6K Phase I ESA. ALTA surveys with appropriate Table A items catch easement conflicts that title reports miss. Geotechnical investigations revealing liquefaction risk, high water tables, or expansive soils should directly drive design decisions (elevated podiums, ground improvement, alternative foundations), not be discovered during plan check. **Core principle**: treat due diligence findings as design inputs, not obstacles to navigate around.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **Read title reports and surveys together, never separately**: Title reports describe easements by document reference; ALTA surveys show where they actually sit on the ground. Palo Alto case study: 10-ft PG&E easement in title became 15-ft diagonal easement on survey, cutting buildable envelope 18%. Always cross-reference with Table A Items 6, 11, 17 before closing.

- **Bay Area liquefaction risk is non-negotiable in Menlo Park and San Jose**: CGS Seismic Hazard Zone maps identify mapped liquefaction zones in Santa Clara Valley alluvial soils. Budget $8K–$15K/unit for ground improvement (stone columns, jet grouting) and enhanced foundations (mat slabs, lateral spreading design). Menlo Park case: $1.4M additional cost (~$12K/unit) discovered post-closing due to skipped GIS screening.

- **Geotechnical bearing capacity directly controls foundation economics**: Allowable bearing pressure of 1,500+ psf allows spread footings; less than 1,500 psf or highly variable conditions trigger deep foundations (piles/piers), adding $3K–$8K per unit. Liquefaction factor of safety (FOS) less than 1.2 requires mitigation. Settle these issues in geotech phase, not at foundation design.

- **High water table demands waterproofing and dewatering planning**: Santa Clara Valley near creeks and wetlands may have seasonal high water tables within 10 feet of grade. Budget $3K–$8K/unit for waterproofing; $100K–$300K for temporary dewatering systems if basements required. ICF basement projects especially vulnerable—plan dewatering permits 6 months in advance.

- **Expansive clays require post-tensioned slabs and overexcavation**: South Bay (Willow Glen, etc.) frequently has CH-classification expansive clays (PI > 40). Budget post-tensioned slab foundations ($4K–$6K/unit premium) plus 18-inch overexcavation with imported non-expansive backfill (adds $5K–$10K/unit site work). San Jose case: $6K/unit foundation cost + 25% grading cost increase from expansive soil.

- **Phase I ESA is mandatory before closing; Phase II cost is controllable after REC identification**: Phase I costs $3K–$6K and is non-negotiable—a $5K Phase I can save $5M remediation liability. If Phase I identifies RECs, budget Phase II at $15K–$100K for soil/groundwater sampling and testing. Check DTSC EnviroStor and SWRCB GeoTracker for adjacent contamination within 1,000 ft (not just on-site).

- **CREC (Controlled REC) or HREC (Historical REC) with engineering controls may still allow development**: Former gas station with institutional controls (deed notice, engineering assessment) may proceed; requires documenting that controls remain effective and factoring ongoing monitoring into operating budget. Distinguish from new REC requiring Phase II investigation and possible remediation.

- **Geotechnical findings drive site plan design decisions**: Liquefaction + flood zone in Menlo Park resulted in elevated podium design—a direct design consequence of geotechnical + survey findings. Don't treat geotech as a constraint to overcome; treat it as a design parameter that shapes your building footprint, foundation type, and cost structure.

:::

::: checklist

**Pre-Acquisition Due Diligence:**

- [ ] Run GIS site analysis (§15.5–15.6 protocol): Check for liquefaction zones, flood zones, fault zones, contamination
- [ ] If in Bay Area liquefaction zone or near creek: budget $8K–$15K/unit for ground improvement + enhanced foundations
- [ ] Check CGS Seismic Hazard Zone maps (Alquist-Priolo, liquefaction, landslide) before making offer
- [ ] Review DTSC EnviroStor and SWRCB GeoTracker for contamination within 1,000 ft radius
- [ ] For South Bay sites: research soil types (alluvial, clay, fill) and anticipate potential expansive soil conditions

**Title Analysis:**

- [ ] Request and review preliminary title report; categorize every Schedule B exception
- [ ] Flag CC&Rs that restrict density, height, use type, or commercial operations
- [ ] Identify all easements (utility, access, drainage, conservation); note their location on property
- [ ] For hotels: verify no restrictions on commercial use, 24/7 operations, or alcohol service
- [ ] Confirm all liens are disclosed and plan to clear or subordinate before close (request payoff amounts)

**Survey Ordering & Verification:**

- [ ] Order ALTA/NSPS survey with Table A Items: 1 (monuments), 3 (flood zone), 4 (area), 6 (setback lines), 8 (substantial features), 11 (utilities), 13 (adjoining owners), 16 (access), 17 (off-site easements), 19 (topo)
- [ ] Require surveyor to plot all title exceptions onto the survey map
- [ ] Cross-reference survey with assessor records: verify parcel boundaries match and lot size matches title
- [ ] Verify setback lines and buildable envelope against zoning requirements
- [ ] Confirm utility easement locations and clearances to planned building footprint

**Geotechnical Investigation:**

- [ ] Engage geotechnical engineer before finalizing pro forma assumptions
- [ ] Require soil profile analysis to 8 ft minimum depth (boring or test pits); 12 ft if deep foundations likely
- [ ] For Bay Area sites: specifically request liquefaction evaluation with factor of safety calculations
- [ ] Assess groundwater depth and seasonal variation; request monitoring well data if available
- [ ] For sites with basements or underbuilding: request waterproofing and dewatering recommendations
- [ ] Request bearing capacity and foundation type recommendations (spread footings vs. deep foundations)
- [ ] For modular projects: confirm bearing capacity supports concentrated loads at module bearing points

**Phase I ESA and Environmental Screening:**

- [ ] Complete Phase I ESA before closing (cost: $3K–$6K); non-negotiable
- [ ] Review Phase I findings for RECs, CRECs, and HRECs; understand remediation timeline for each
- [ ] If REC identified: budget Phase II ESA at $15K–$100K depending on scope
- [ ] Check for vapor intrusion risk (especially near VOC contamination); budget mitigation ($5K–$50K) if flagged
- [ ] For former commercial/industrial sites: budget asbestos and lead paint surveys before demolition

**Cost Impact Assessment:**

- [ ] Liquefaction or high water table identified: add $8K–$15K/unit to pro forma hard costs
- [ ] Expansive clay: add $5K–$10K/unit for enhanced foundations + grading impact
- [ ] REC with Phase II needed: add $15K–$100K soft costs + potential remediation (verify with engineer)
- [ ] Easement conflicts: model redline costs or negotiation timeline (could delay approval 6–18 months)
- [ ] Flood zone or seismic constraints: incorporate into site plan and verify design accommodates

**Title Clearance & Closing Coordination:**

- [ ] Ensure all liens are cleared or subordinated before closing
- [ ] Obtain title insurance commitment; verify exceptions are acceptable
- [ ] For easements that may impact development: consider easement relocation or recordation agreement
- [ ] Confirm access is legally recorded and not subject to termination by property owner
- [ ] Record all relevant survey information and geotechnical limitations in project file

## Overview

Technical due diligence separates experienced developers from novices. The ability to fluently read title reports, surveys, and geotechnical reports — and know what to look for — directly impacts your ability to accurately underwrite a deal and avoid costly surprises.

---

## Part A: Title Report Analysis

### 13.1 Preliminary Title Report

The preliminary title report is your **first look at what encumbers a property**. Every item in Schedule B (exceptions from coverage) must be reviewed and categorized.

| Exception Category | Typical Items | Risk Level | Action Required |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **CC&Rs** | Use restrictions, architectural controls, HOA requirements | Medium–High | Review for conflicts with proposed development |
| **Easements** | Utility, access, drainage, view, conservation | High | Plot on site plan; check for building envelope conflicts |
| **Liens** | Tax liens, mechanic's liens, judgment liens | Critical | Must be cleared or subordinated before close |
| **Deed Restrictions** | Building height limits, use restrictions, setback requirements | High | May require quiet title action or modification |
| **Mineral Rights** | Subsurface rights reservations | Low–Medium | Evaluate impact on foundation and underground work |

### Red Flags in Title Reports

- Unrecorded easements discovered during survey
- Access easements that don't align with planned site circulation
- CC&Rs that restrict density, height, or use type
- Outstanding liens from prior owners
- For hotels: restrictions on commercial use, alcohol sales, or 24/7 operations

---

## Part B: ALTA/NSPS Land Title Survey

### 13.2 The ALTA Survey

The ALTA survey is the **gold standard** for development sites. It combines a boundary survey, topographic information, and title exception mapping into a single document.

### Essential Table A Optional Items

For development projects, specify these Table A items:

| Item | Content | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Item 1** | Monuments | Confirms boundary markers exist |
| **Item 2** | Address and parcel ID | Cross-reference with assessor records |
| **Item 3** | Flood zone | FEMA determination; insurance requirements |
| **Item 4** | Gross land area | Confirms lot size for density calculations |
| **Item 6** | Setback/building lines | Defines buildable envelope |
| **Item 8** | Substantial features | Trees, walls, structures near boundaries |
| **Item 11** | Utilities | Location of all utility connections and services |
| **Item 13** | Names of adjoining owners | Identify neighbors for outreach |
| **Item 16** | Evidence of access | Confirms legal and physical access |
| **Item 17** | Off-site easements | Easements benefiting the property |
| **Item 19** | Topo contours | Grading design basis; drainage analysis |

## Part C: Geotechnical Engineering

### 13.3 Reading a Geotechnical Report

A geotechnical report translates subsurface conditions into engineering recommendations that directly impact your **foundation design, construction method, and project cost**.

| Section | What to Look For | Red Flags |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Soil Profile** | USCS classification, depth to bedrock, consistency | Expansive clays (CH), organic soils (OH/OL), fill soils |
| **Groundwater** | Seasonal high water table depth | Water table within 10 ft of foundation; perched water |
| **Bearing Capacity** | Allowable bearing pressure (psf) | Less than 1,500 psf; significant variation across site |
| **Liquefaction** | Factor of safety, settlement estimates | FOS less than 1.2; settlement greater than 1 inch; lateral spreading |
| **Seismic Design** | Site class, spectral acceleration values | Site Class E or F; near-fault effects |
| **Foundation Recommendations** | Type, depth, reinforcement, drainage | Deep foundations required (piles/piers); extensive soil improvement |
| **Slope Stability** | Factor of safety for cut/fill slopes | FOS less than 1.5 for static; less than 1.0 for seismic |

### Hotel-Specific Geotechnical Concerns

| Concern | Why It Matters for Hotels |
| --- | --- |
| **Larger footprint** | More soil variability across site |
| **Underground parking** | Dewatering, lateral earth pressure, waterproofing |
| **Modular stacking loads** | Higher point loads at module bearing points |
| **Full-block sites** | May need ground improvement program |
| **Module connections** | Must transfer seismic loads through connections |

### 13.4 Bay Area Geotechnical Considerations

The San Francisco Bay Area presents specific challenges:

- **Alluvial soils** in the Santa Clara Valley are susceptible to liquefaction
- **High water tables** near creeks and former marshlands
- **Mapped liquefaction hazard zones** under the Seismic Hazards Mapping Act
- Portions of **Menlo Park and San Jose** have documented liquefaction risk

### Foundation Implications

For projects with basements (e.g., ICF basement construction):
- **Waterproofing requirements** based on groundwater depth
- **Dewatering during construction** — permits and temporary systems
- **Lateral earth pressure** design for basement walls
- **Hydrostatic uplift** potential on mat foundations

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Menlo Park — Liquefaction Risk on Former Marshland

A developer acquired a 1.5-acre parcel near Bayfront Expressway in Menlo Park for a 120-unit multifamily project. The preliminary title report was clean, and the ALTA survey showed no unusual conditions. However, the geotechnical investigation revealed the site was on **former marshland with a seasonal high water table at 4 feet below grade** and a liquefaction factor of safety below 1.0 for the design earthquake.

The geotechnical engineer recommended: (1) ground improvement using stone columns ($800K), (2) a mat foundation instead of spread footings ($400K premium), (3) a structural slab designed for lateral spreading forces ($200K premium). Total geotechnical-driven cost increase: **$1.4M** — approximately $12K per unit that was not in the original underwriting.

**Lesson Learned**: In Menlo Park and other Bay-adjacent areas, always check the CGS Seismic Hazard Zone maps for liquefaction before putting a site under contract. If the site is in a mapped liquefaction zone, budget $8K–$15K per unit for ground improvement and enhanced foundations. This cost should be reflected in your offer price, not discovered after closing.

---

### Case 2: Palo Alto — Utility Easement Kills Building Envelope

A developer purchased a 12,000 SF lot on University Avenue in Palo Alto for a mixed-use project. The preliminary title report showed a 10-ft PG&E gas easement along the rear property line — seemingly routine. However, when the ALTA survey plotted the easement, it was discovered that the easement was actually a **15-ft easement** (the title report referenced the wrong recorded document) and it ran diagonally, not parallel to the rear lot line. The easement cut into the buildable envelope, reducing the building footprint by 18% and making the planned underground parking layout infeasible.

The developer spent 14 months negotiating an easement relocation with PG&E, ultimately paying $180K for PG&E to relocate the gas main and rerecord the easement.

**Lesson Learned**: Never trust the title report alone — always cross-reference with the ALTA survey. Title reports describe easements by document reference; surveys show where they actually are on the ground. Order your ALTA survey with Table A Items 6 (setback lines), 11 (utilities), and 17 (off-site easements) to catch these conflicts before closing.

---

### Case 3: San Jose — Expansive Clay Surprises

A residential developer in the Willow Glen area of San Jose encountered **highly expansive clays (CH classification, PI > 40)** at shallow depth across the entire site. The geotechnical report recommended post-tensioned slab foundations with a minimum 30-inch moisture barrier on all sides — adding $6K per unit to the foundation cost.

More critically, the expansive soil conditions required deeper utility trenches with imported non-expansive backfill, increasing site work costs by 25%. The grading plan had to be revised to accommodate 18 inches of overexcavation and replacement across the pad area.

**Lesson Learned**: Santa Clara Valley soils are notoriously variable. Even within a single neighborhood, soil conditions can range from well-graded gravel to highly expansive clay. Always order a geotechnical investigation before finalizing your pro forma — foundation costs in the South Bay can vary by $5K–$15K per unit based on soil conditions alone.

## Practitioner Checklist

### Title
- [ ] Review every Schedule B exception in the preliminary title report
- [ ] Plot all easements on the site plan
- [ ] Identify and clear all liens before closing
- [ ] Check CC&Rs for development restrictions
- [ ] For hotels: verify no restrictions on commercial use or alcohol

### Survey
- [ ] Order ALTA survey with appropriate Table A items
- [ ] Cross-reference survey with title exceptions
- [ ] Verify lot size matches assessor records
- [ ] Confirm setback lines and buildable envelope
- [ ] Check flood zone determination

### Geotechnical
- [ ] Review soil profile for expansive clays, fill, or organic soils
- [ ] Check groundwater depth and seasonal variation
- [ ] Evaluate bearing capacity for proposed structure type
- [ ] Assess liquefaction risk (especially in Bay Area)
- [ ] Review foundation recommendations and cost implications
- [ ] For modular: confirm bearing capacity supports stacking loads
- [ ] For basements: evaluate waterproofing and dewatering requirements

## Key Takeaway

> **Title, survey, and geotech are your deal-evaluation trifecta.** Title tells you what encumbers the property. Survey tells you the physical truth of the site. Geotech tells you what's underground and what it costs to build on. Read all three before committing capital.
