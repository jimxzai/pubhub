# Chapter 5: CEQA Decision Framework

**Statutory Reference**: Public Resources Code §21000+; CEQA Guidelines 14 CCR §15000+

---

## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

CEQA (California Environmental Quality Act) is the single most consequential regulatory process in California development—controlling whether a housing project gets to market in 4-6 months (ministerial, no CEQA) or 18-36 months (EIR pathway). The decision tree that determines CEQA exposure is mechanical and binary: (1) Is discretionary approval required? (2) Is an exemption available? (3) Is there substantial evidence of a significant effect? The 2025 reforms (AB 130 and SB 131, signed June 30, 2025) have fundamentally shifted the landscape for urban infill housing. AB 130 creates a sweeping statutory exemption for infill projects ≤20 acres with no unit cap, no traffic/noise/air studies required—only a Phase I environmental site assessment. SB 131 eliminates the "cliff effect" where missing one exemption criterion by a small margin triggers a full EIR; instead, "near-miss" projects receive limited focused CEQA review. Categorical exemptions (Class 3 for small structures, Class 32 for urban infill, Class 15 for parcel maps) remain available for projects that don't qualify for ministerial pathways. The "fair argument" standard is the critical vulnerability: if a project opponent can present substantial evidence (expert opinion, agency comment, even lay testimony) that a significant impact may occur, an EIR becomes mandatory—even if the lead agency disagrees. The practical implication: assume controversial projects will trigger EIR cost ($200K–$2M) and timeline (18–24+ months) estimates and budget accordingly. Bay Area case studies (Castilleja 7+ years, San Jose downtown tower biologist letter triggering $350K supplemental EIR) demonstrate that the "fair argument" standard is extraordinarily low—a single expert letter can force environmental review costing hundreds of thousands and consuming years.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **AB 130 Statutory Exemption (Effective 2025) Eliminates CEQA for ~70–85% of Urban Infill Housing**: Projects ≤20 acres in urban infill with GP consistency, no environmental constraints, and no special habitat/farmland require only a Phase I ESA—no traffic study, no air quality analysis, no biological survey, no full CEQA. This is the broadest housing exemption in California history. Qualifying projects bypass 12–24 months and $200K–$2M in CEQA costs entirely. The AB 130 checklist is your first analysis step: locate site in urban area, confirm ≤20 acres, verify Mullin residential density threshold met, confirm no wetlands/habitat/farmland, verify not on DTSC hazard sites, confirm not in hazardous waste zone. If all boxes check, file the project ministerially and avoid CEQA entirely.

- **SB 131 "Near-Miss" Limited Review Eliminates the Cliff Effect**: Prior to 2025, missing one exemption criterion by a small margin triggered a full EIR costing $200K–$2M and consuming 18–24+ months. SB 131 creates a "near-miss" pathway where projects that miss one criterion receive limited focused CEQA review on that single issue only—typically an MND with targeted analysis. For example, a project that exceeds the 20-acre AB 130 threshold by 5 acres but otherwise qualifies now gets a streamlined MND on the acreage impact rather than a full EIR. This compresses CEQA costs from $200K–$2M to $50K–$150K and timelines from 18–24 months to 6–12 months.

- **Ministerial Approval = Zero CEQA**: SB 35/423 (multifamily in RHNA-deficient cities), SB 9 (duplexes/lot splits), SB 684 (small-lot subdivisions), AB 2011 (commercial-to-housing) all explicitly exempt CEQA from the approval process. No environmental documentation required. No discretionary review. No CEQA litigation window. If you qualify for a ministerial pathway, use it—the cost and timeline advantage is transformative.

- **Categorical Exemptions (Class 3, 32, 15) Are Your Strongest Non-Ministerial Protection**: Class 3 (small structures up to 6 units in urbanized areas), Class 32 (urban infill ≤5 acres GP-consistent), and Class 15 (minor land divisions ≤4 parcels, urbanized, GP-consistent) are categorical exemptions requiring only initial study confirming no exceptions apply. Proactively document why the six exceptions to categorical exemptions do not apply (cumulative impact, significant effect, scenic highway, hazardous waste, historical resource, sensitive environment) in a preliminary environmental screening ($10K–$15K investment) that creates an administrative record defensible in court.

- **"Fair Argument" Standard Is an Extraordinarily Low Evidentiary Bar**: If a project opponent can present substantial evidence (expert opinion, agency comment, lay testimony with relevant personal observations) that a significant effect *may* occur, an EIR must be prepared—*even if* the lead agency and preparer disagree with the opponent's argument. A single biologist's letter claiming peregrine falcon impacts from vibration/light forced a $350K supplemental EIR in San Jose downtown. Leave no impact category unanalyzed in your initial study; opponents will attack gaps as evidence that impacts were not considered.

- **MND (Mitigated Negative Declaration) Costs $50K–$150K and Takes 12–18 Months; EIR Costs $200K–$2M and Takes 18–36+ Months**: The cost and timeline gap between MND and EIR is 4x. Model CEQA costs explicitly in Appendix G Level 3 Stress Test. For every month of CEQA review, budget $85K in typical Bay Area carrying costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance). A 12-month MND vs. 24-month EIR represents a $1M+ cumulative holding cost difference—often the kill decision criterion for marginal projects.

- **Class 32 Infill Exemption Requires All 5 Criteria Met; Conditions Can Be Imposed During Development**: Class 32 qualifying projects (GP-consistent infill ≤5 acres, fully served by utilities, urban perimeter, no habitat/farmland) bypass CEQA but cannot have land use inconsistent with existing community character—this is reviewed during the entitlement hearing. Menlo Park projects successfully used Class 32 to avoid CEQA by proactively documenting that no exceptions applied; spend $10K–$15K on preliminary environmental screening to build the record.

- **EIR Litigation Window (30 Days Post-Approval) Is a $100K–$500K Cost Contingency**: After your EIR is certified and the project approved, opponents have 30 days to file CEQA litigation. Budget $100K–$500K for legal defense. CEQA cases rarely result in complete project denial but often result in supplemental EIR requirements (18+ month delays). The Castilleja 7-year saga and San Jose biologist challenge demonstrate the litigation risk is real and consequential—not theoretical.

- **Housing Ministerial Pathways (SB 35, SB 9, SB 684, AB 2011) Are Explicitly Exempt; AB 130 Exemption Is Explicitly Housing-Only**: These pathways are designed to remove CEQA as a barrier to housing. Developers building housing projects qualify for multiple CEQA advantages that don't apply to hotels or commercial development. Non-housing projects default to traditional categorical exemptions or full CEQA review—there is no state-level exemption streamlining comparable to housing legislation.

:::

::: checklist

**Before Filing Application:**

- [ ] Determine if project requires discretionary approval; if ministerial only → CEQA does not apply
- [ ] Check if project qualifies for SB 35/423, SB 9, SB 684, or AB 2011 ministerial housing exemption (zero CEQA)
- [ ] Run AB 130 exemption checklist: ≤20 acres, urban infill, GP-consistent, Mullin residential density, no DTSC hazard sites, no wetlands, no farmland, no sensitive habitat, not on excluded project list (hotels, motels, transient lodging)
- [ ] If AB 130 qualifies → file ministerially with Phase I ESA only; no full CEQA required
- [ ] If AB 130 near-miss (fails one criterion) → evaluate SB 131 limited review pathway instead of full EIR

**CEQA Pathway Selection (If Not Ministerial or AB 130):**

- [ ] Identify applicable categorical exemptions (Class 3, Class 32, Class 15) and check if all criteria are met
- [ ] If categorical exemption appears to apply: prepare preliminary environmental screening ($10K–$15K) to document that no exceptions to exemption apply and build administrative record
- [ ] If no exemption available: assess whether ND, MND, or EIR is required based on environmental constraints and project controversy
- [ ] For controversial projects: budget for EIR ($200K–$2M) and 18–24 month timeline as baseline assumption

**Environmental Consultant Engagement:**

- [ ] Engage environmental consultant during pre-application phase (not after filing)
- [ ] Provide consultant with all preliminary project information to assess CEQA trajectory
- [ ] Request comprehensive initial study that addresses every conceivable impact category, including unlikely ones (opponents will attack gaps)
- [ ] For EIR projects: request 30-day litigation defense cost estimate in scoping phase

**Administrative Record and Documentation:**

- [ ] Document every environmental study, agency consultation, expert comment, and public comment response from day one
- [ ] The record is what courts review—if it's not in the record, it doesn't exist for litigation purposes
- [ ] For Class 32 or categorical exemptions: build affirmative case why exceptions do not apply (don't leave it implicit)
- [ ] Calendar 30-day CEQA litigation window post-approval in project schedule

**Modeling CEQA Costs in Pro Forma (Appendix G):**

- [ ] Level 1 Napkin Test: Use $0 for ministerial/exempt projects, $100K–$150K for MND, $400K–$800K for EIR
- [ ] Level 2 Full Underwriting: Model CEQA pathway and timeline explicitly; use carrying cost multiplier ($85K/month typical Bay Area) to calculate holding cost impact
- [ ] Level 3 Stress Test: Model CEQA litigation costs ($100K–$500K) as separate line item; assess whether EIR timeline exceeds financing commitment term (Kill Criteria #8)
- [ ] If CEQA costs + timeline exceed financing term, kill the project early rather than absorb sunk costs

## Overview

CEQA is the **single most consequential regulatory process** in California development. It can add 1–3 years and $100K+ to a project's cost, and is the primary vehicle for project opponents to challenge approvals in court. Mastering CEQA is not optional.

---

## 5.1 The CEQA Decision Tree

Every project follows a decision tree that determines the required level of environmental review:

```
1. Is it a "project" under CEQA? (§15378)
   └── If no discretionary approval required → CEQA does not apply

2. Is it exempt?
   ├── Statutory exemptions (§15260–15285)
   └── Categorical exemptions (§15300–15333)
       └── If exempt → No CEQA review required

3. Is there substantial evidence of significant effect?
   ├── No → Negative Declaration (ND)
   ├── Effects can be mitigated → Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND)
   └── Significant unmitigable impacts → Full EIR required
```

---

## 5.2 Key Categorical Exemptions for Development

| Class | Name | Typical Application | Size/Scope Limits |
|-------|------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Class 1** (§15301) | Existing Facilities | Minor alteration, addition, remodel | Up to 10,000 SF in non-sensitive area |
| **Class 3** (§15303) | New Small Structures | New SFR, duplex, apartment (up to limits) | Up to 6 units in urbanized area; 4 commercial buildings |
| **Class 4** (§15304) | Minor Alterations to Land | Grading, fill, landscaping on < 5 acres | Must not be in sensitive area |
| **Class 11** (§15311) | Accessory Structures | On-site accessory structures | Garage, carport, fence, small utility |
| **Class 15** (§15315) | Minor Land Divisions | Subdivision into 4 or fewer parcels | Must conform to GP and zoning; all services available |
| **Class 32** (§15332) | Infill Development | Urban infill meeting specific criteria | Must be GP-consistent, ≤5 acres, no habitat/farmland impact |

### Legislative Exemptions for Housing

| Law | Exemption | Applies To |
|-----|-----------|-----------|
| **SB 35/423** | Qualifying multifamily → no CEQA (ministerial) | Housing only |
| **SB 9** | Duplexes/lot splits → no CEQA (ministerial) | SFR lots only |
| **SB 684** | Small lot subdivision ≤10 units → no CEQA (ministerial) | Infill, MF zones |
| **AB 2011** | Housing on commercial land → no CEQA (ministerial) | Commercial zones |
| **AB 130** *(2025)* | Broad infill housing exemption ≤20 acres → **CEQA exempt** (Phase I ESA only) | Urban infill housing |
| **SB 131** *(2025)* | "Near-miss" projects missing 1 exemption criterion → limited focused CEQA review only | Housing near-miss |

> **Critical 2025 Update**: AB 130 and SB 131 (signed June 30, 2025) represent the most sweeping CEQA reforms since 1970. AB 130 creates a statutory CEQA exemption for infill housing far broader than Class 32 — no unit cap, up to 20 acres, no traffic/noise/air studies required (Phase I ESA only). SB 131 eliminates the "cliff effect" where missing one exemption criterion by a small margin triggered a full EIR. Together, they compress the CEQA hierarchy from five levels to effectively three for most urban housing. See **Chapter 3, §3.6.2** for the full AB 130/SB 131 analysis, eligibility tables, and approval timelines. See **§3.6.6** for the updated CEQA Decision Tree incorporating these reforms.

## 5.3 Exceptions to Categorical Exemptions

Even if a project fits a categorical exemption, the exemption **does not apply** if any of these six exceptions apply (§15300.2):

1. **Cumulative Impact**: Successive projects of the same type in the same area create a significant cumulative impact
2. **Significant Effect**: Unusual circumstances cause a reasonable possibility of significant effect
3. **Scenic Highway**: Project damages a scenic resource on or near a state scenic highway
4. **Hazardous Waste Site**: Project is on a listed hazardous waste site
5. **Historical Resource**: Project may cause a substantial change to a historical resource
6. **Location**: Project is in a particularly sensitive environment (wetlands, species habitat)

---

## 5.4 The Fair Argument Standard

This is the **most litigated and misunderstood** aspect of CEQA.

When a project opponent argues that an EIR (rather than a Negative Declaration) is required, the **"fair argument" standard** applies:

> If there is substantial evidence in the record supporting a fair argument that the project *may* have a significant environmental effect, an EIR must be prepared — **even if there is also substantial evidence that the effect will not be significant**.

### What Counts as Substantial Evidence

- Expert opinion
- Agency comments
- Even testimony from lay witnesses with relevant personal observations

> **Practical Implication**: If your project is controversial, assume opponents can find a "fair argument" and plan accordingly. Budget for EIR risk.

---

## 5.5 Negative Declaration vs. Mitigated Negative Declaration

| Type | When Used | Requirements |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Negative Declaration (ND)** | No substantial evidence of any significant environmental effect | Initial Study shows no impacts |
| **Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND)** | Project may have significant effects, but all can be mitigated to less-than-significant | Mitigation measures identified and adopted; monitoring program required |

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Palo Alto — Castilleja School CEQA Battle (7+ Years)

The Castilleja School expansion project in Palo Alto became one of the Bay Area's most notorious CEQA sagas. The school sought to demolish and rebuild its campus, increasing enrollment from 438 to 540 students. Neighbors challenged the city's use of a Mitigated Negative Declaration, arguing a full EIR was required under the "fair argument" standard. Key contested issues included traffic (student drop-off/pick-up), construction noise, tree removal, and underground parking impacts on groundwater.

After years of hearings, appeals, revised environmental studies, and a second MND, the project was eventually approved — but the process consumed over 7 years and millions in legal and consulting costs.

**Lesson Learned**: In affluent Palo Alto neighborhoods, assume that any project generating neighborhood opposition will face a "fair argument" challenge. If your project involves traffic increases, tree removal, or construction near homes, the safe strategy is to prepare a focused EIR from the start rather than risking an MND that gets challenged. The cost of "saving money" with an MND that triggers litigation far exceeds the cost of an EIR.

---

### Case 2: Menlo Park — Class 32 Infill Exemption for Small Projects

Several small-to-mid-size residential projects along El Camino Real in Menlo Park have successfully used the **Class 32 infill exemption** to avoid CEQA entirely. A 24-unit mixed-use project near the Caltrain station qualified because it met all Class 32 criteria: GP-consistent, on a site under 5 acres, fully served by utilities, surrounded by urban uses, and with no impact on habitat or farmland.

The developer conducted a preliminary environmental screening (not required by law, but a best practice) to document that no exceptions to the exemption applied. This documentation proved critical when a neighbor filed a CEQA challenge — the court upheld the exemption because the record clearly demonstrated no exceptional circumstances.

**Lesson Learned**: Class 32 is your strongest categorical exemption for urban infill projects, but proactively document why no exceptions apply. Spend $10K–$15K on a preliminary environmental screening even when you believe you're exempt. That upfront investment creates an administrative record that defends the exemption in court.

---

### Case 3: San Jose — The "Fair Argument" Trap on a Downtown Tower

A developer proposed a 20-story mixed-use tower in downtown San Jose and sought to rely on the city's Downtown Specific Plan programmatic EIR. Opponents hired a biologist who submitted a letter claiming that peregrine falcons nesting on a nearby building could be impacted by construction vibration and light pollution. Despite the city's environmental staff finding no credible risk, the court held that the biologist's letter constituted "substantial evidence" of a "fair argument" that a significant biological impact could occur — overturning the MND and requiring a supplemental EIR.

The supplemental EIR took 14 months and cost $350K. Its conclusion: no significant biological impact. But the developer lost a year and a half of construction schedule and carrying costs.

**Lesson Learned**: The "fair argument" standard is an extraordinarily low bar. A single expert letter — even one that seems far-fetched — can force an EIR. In your environmental consultant's initial study, affirmatively address every conceivable impact category, including seemingly unlikely ones. Leave no category unanalyzed, because that's where opponents will attack.

- [ ] Determine if the project requires any discretionary approval (if not → no CEQA)
- [ ] Check all applicable categorical exemptions (especially Class 3, 32)
- [ ] Evaluate the six exceptions to categorical exemptions
- [ ] For housing: check SB 35, SB 9, SB 684, and AB 2011 ministerial exemptions
- [ ] **For infill housing: run AB 130 eligibility checklist (≤20 acres, urban, Mullin density) — see Ch 3, §3.6.2**
