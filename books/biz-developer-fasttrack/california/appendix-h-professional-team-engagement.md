## The Central Problem

Development is a team sport, but most developers — especially new entrants — get the **team composition and timing wrong**. The two most common failures:

1. **Engaging too late**: The Peninsula project in Appendix E discovered its PUE problem 18 months in because Public Works was never consulted before design. A 30-minute pre-application conversation would have surfaced it on Day 1.

2. **Engaging too early (wasting money)**: Ordering a full ALTA survey, Phase I ESA, and geotechnical report before confirming the deal even pencils (Appendix G, Level 1 Napkin Test) is $15K–$30K spent on a deal that should have been killed with a $0 back-of-envelope calculation.

This appendix provides the **sequenced engagement map** — who to engage, when, why, and what it costs — organized by development phase.

**Duration**: 2–8 weeks
**Capital at Risk**: $5K–$20K (additional studies + pre-app fees)
**Decision Gate**: Appendix E, Section 6 Go/No-Go Decision

| Sequence | Professional | Purpose | Deliverable | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

| 2nd | **Civil Engineer** | Attend pre-app (utility/grading questions) | Utility feasibility memo | Included in Phase 1 scope or $2K–$5K |

**The Pre-Application Conference — Who Must Be in the Room**:

| Person | Why They Must Attend | What Goes Wrong If They Don't |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **You (Developer)** | Decision authority, asks business questions | Project stalls on unanswered questions |
| **Land Use Attorney** | Interprets staff comments legally; preserves your rights | You miss legal leverage (HAA, SB 35 eligibility, PSA deadlines) |
| **Architect** | Understands design implications of staff comments | Design team discovers constraints months later |
| **Civil Engineer** | Addresses grading, utility, stormwater questions | Public Works issues surface at plan check instead of pre-app |

> **The Pre-App Rule**: Every dollar spent on the pre-application conference saves $100 in downstream revisions. Bring your land use attorney AND your civil engineer. The Peninsula project's 3-year delay traces directly to a pre-app that did not include Public Works.

### Phase 3: Design & Entitlement (Design Start to Entitlement Approval)

**Duration**: 3–36 months (depending on pathway — see Ch 16, Part A/B)

**Decision Gate**: Entitlement approval / conditions of approval

| Professional | Engagement Level | Key Deliverables | Typical Cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Architect** | Full engagement (primary consultant) | Schematic design → design development → entitlement plan set | 8–15% of hard costs (or $40K–$200K+) |
| **Land Use Attorney** | Active throughout | CEQA strategy, hearing preparation, conditions negotiation, appeals | $15K–$100K+ (complex projects) |
| **Civil Engineer** | Active from SD forward | Grading plan, preliminary stormwater, utility plan | $20K–$60K |

| **Traffic Engineer** | Active if TIA required | Traffic impact analysis, parking study | $15K–$40K |
| **Arborist** | If tree removal proposed | Tree protection plan, replanting plan | $2K–$5K |
| **Acoustical Consultant** | If noise is an issue | Full noise study for CEQA/conditions | $8K–$15K |
| **GC / CM** | Pre-construction services | Updated cost estimate at each design phase; value engineering | $5K–$20K (or against future GMP) |

**Phase 3 — Critical Sequencing Rules**:

1. **Architect FIRST, then engineers follow**. The architect produces the design; structural, civil, and MEP engineers make it buildable. Do not engage structural or MEP before the architect has a schematic design.

2. **Land use attorney BEFORE the first hearing, not at the hearing**. Your attorney needs time to prepare: review the staff report, analyze conditions of approval, prepare legal arguments, and strategize your testimony. Engaging them the week before the hearing is too late.

3. **Environmental consultant BEFORE the application is filed**. If your project requires CEQA, the consultant should be involved in determining the pathway (exempt, MND, EIR) before you submit. Filing without a CEQA strategy wastes 3–6 months when the city tells you what study they want.

4. **GC at every design milestone**. The most expensive sentence in development is: *"The architect designed it, but the contractor says it costs $X more than the budget."* Have the GC price each design iteration (schematic, design development, construction documents) so cost stays aligned with the pro forma.

**Plan Check Coordination** (see Ch 16, §16.5–16.6):

The building permit phase involves multiple departments reviewing simultaneously. Your **architect is the plan check coordinator** — they receive comments from every department and coordinate responses. But a common failure is assuming the architect will manage interdepartmental conflicts. On complex projects, the developer should:

- Request a **plan check status meeting** with all reviewing departments after the first comment letter
- Ensure the **civil engineer** responds to Public Works comments directly (not filtered through the architect)
- Have the **structural engineer** attend any structural plan check conferences in person

## Part 3: Selection Criteria — How to Hire Right

### H.3 Land Use Attorney — The Most Important Hire

The land use attorney is your **strategic partner**, not just a legal service provider. They should be involved from deal screening through entitlement approval. The wrong attorney costs you more than their fees — they cost you months of timeline and missed opportunities.

**Selection Criteria**:

| Criterion | Why It Matters | How to Evaluate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Jurisdiction-specific experience** | Every Bay Area city has different politics, staff personalities, and precedents | Ask: "How many projects have you entitled in [target city] in the last 3 years?" |
| **Hearing track record** | Planning commission and city council hearings require persuasion skills, not just legal knowledge | Ask: "What was your approval rate at hearing last year?" |
| **Relationships with staff** | An attorney who knows the planning director can get a pre-app in 2 weeks instead of 2 months | Ask: "Who is the current planning director in [target city]?" (If they don't know, keep looking.) |
| **HAA / SB 35 expertise** | The new housing laws are your most powerful tools — your attorney must know them cold | Ask: "Have you ever filed an HAA objection or SB 35 consistency challenge?" |
| **Responsiveness** | Entitlement timelines are driven by PSA deadlines and hearing calendars — delays compound | Ask for references and check response time |

**Fee Structures**:

| Structure | When Appropriate | Typical Range |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Hourly** | Standard entitlement (CUP, design review, variance) | $350–$650/hr; $15K–$100K total |
| **Flat fee** | Defined scope (zoning analysis, SB 35 application) | $3K–$15K per deliverable |
| **Monthly retainer** | Ongoing portfolio-level work (multiple projects) | $3K–$10K/month |
| **Success fee** | Rare — for high-stakes entitlements with binary outcomes | Hourly + bonus on approval |

> **Bay Area Firms to Know**: The Peninsula/South Bay land use bar is small. The same 15–20 attorneys appear at most hearings. Before hiring, attend 2–3 planning commission meetings in your target jurisdiction and observe which attorneys are effective. This is the best due diligence you can do.

### H.4 Civil Engineer — The Utility and Grading Expert

The civil engineer is the **second most important hire after the land use attorney** for Bay Area projects, because stormwater (C.3), grading, and utility coordination drive both cost and timeline. Civil engineering issues that surface late are among the most expensive to fix.

**Selection Criteria**:

| Criterion | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| **C.3 stormwater expertise** | Bay Area MRP (Municipal Regional Permit) compliance is the most complex stormwater regime in California. Your civil must know the current C.3 requirements for your specific county. |
| **Local utility district relationships** | Every water and sanitary district has different connection requirements and timelines. A civil who has worked with your specific district knows the process. |
| **Pre-application experience** | The civil engineer should attend the pre-app conference to address Public Works questions directly. |
| **Turnaround time** | Civil engineering plan check corrections can bottleneck the entire building permit timeline. Fast turnaround on correction responses saves weeks. |

**Critical Deliverables**:

| Deliverable | When Needed | Typical Cost | Chapter Reference |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Preliminary grading feasibility | Phase 1 (due diligence) | $3K–$8K | Ch 14, §14.1 |
| C.3 stormwater preliminary sizing | Phase 2 (pre-design) | $3K–$5K | Ch 14, §14.2 |
| Full grading plan | Phase 3 (with architect) | $15K–$30K | Ch 14, §14.1 |
| Full stormwater management plan | Phase 4 (building permit) | $8K–$20K | Ch 14, §14.2 |
| SWPPP | Phase 5 (before grading) | $5K–$10K | Ch 14, §14.3 |
| Encroachment permit plans | Phase 4 (parallel with BP) | $5K–$10K | — |

### H.5 Licensed Land Surveyor — Boundaries, Elevations, and Legal Descriptions

The surveyor defines the **physical and legal reality** of your site. Everything the architect draws, everything the civil engineer grades, and everything the title company insures is based on the surveyor's work.

**Survey Types and When to Order**:

| Survey Type | Purpose | When to Order | Cost | Chapter Reference |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Boundary Survey** | Confirms property lines, monuments, encroachments | Phase 1, Day 3–5 | $3K–$8K | Ch 13, §13.2 |
| **ALTA/NSPS Survey** | Comprehensive survey required by lenders; includes easements, improvements, access | Phase 1, Day 3–5 (with Table A items specified) | $5K–$15K | Ch 13, §13.2 |
| **Topographic Survey** | Existing grades, trees, structures, utilities | Phase 1 (usually combined with boundary) | $3K–$8K | Ch 14, §14.1 |
| **Construction Staking** | Locates building corners, grades, utilities for construction | Phase 5 (after permit, before construction) | $3K–$8K | — |
| **Record of Survey / Corner Record** | Filed with county after subdivision | Phase 6 (if subdivision) | $5K–$10K | Ch 7 |

**ALTA/NSPS Table A Items — What to Request**:

When ordering an ALTA survey, you must specify which optional "Table A" items to include. The standard items cost extra but are essential for development:

| Table A Item | What It Adds | Cost Adder | Importance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Item 1** | Monument placed or verified | $500–$1K | Essential — confirms physical corners |
| **Item 5** | Exterior dimensions of buildings | $500–$1K | Essential if existing structures |
| **Item 6(a)** | Setback and building lines | $1K–$2K | Critical — shows buildable envelope |
| **Item 8** | Substantial features observed (walls, paths, drives) | $500–$1K | Essential — shows encroachments |
| **Item 11** | Utilities (observed + recorded) | $1K–$2K | Critical — maps utility easements |
| **Item 16** | Evidence of earth moving or building construction | $500 | Important if recent work on site |
| **Item 18** | Offsite easement evidence | $1K–$2K | Important — catches offsite encroachments |
| **Item 19** | Professional liability insurance | Varies | Required by most lenders |

> **Budget Rule**: For any site where you plan to build, order the ALTA survey with Items 1, 5, 6(a), 8, 11, 18, and 19. Total cost: $8K–$20K. This is non-negotiable — the survey is the foundation of every drawing that follows.

### H.6 Geotechnical Engineer — What's Underground

The geotech report determines your **foundation type and cost** — which can swing from $20/SF (spread footings on good soil) to $80+/SF (deep piles in liquefiable soil). A $15K geotech report can reveal a $500K foundation cost difference.

**Selection Criteria**:

| Criterion | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| **Local soil knowledge** | Bay Area soils vary wildly — bay mud in East Palo Alto, expansive clay in San Jose, bedrock in the hills. Your geotech should have a library of borings from your target area. |
| **Lab capacity** | Firms with in-house labs return results 2–3 weeks faster than those who outsource. |
| **Structural coordination** | The geotech must communicate directly with your structural engineer. Foundation recommendations that don't translate into buildable structural details cause delays. |

**Deliverable Sequence**:

| Deliverable | When | Cost | What You Learn |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Desktop study** (preliminary) | Phase 0–1 | $1K–$3K | Soil type, liquefaction zone, known hazards |
| **Full geotechnical investigation** | Phase 1 (Day 5–7) | $8K–$20K | Soil borings, lab tests, foundation recommendations |
| **Supplemental investigation** | Phase 3–4 (if design changes) | $3K–$8K | Additional borings if building footprint shifts |
| **Construction observation** | Phase 5 | $5K–$15K (hourly) | Foundation verification, compaction testing |

### H.8 Architect — The Design Lead

The architect is your **most expensive consultant** (by total fee) and the professional who most directly determines whether the project is buildable, beautiful, and profitable.

**Selection Criteria for Developer Projects**:

| Criterion | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| **Code fluency** | The architect who knows the zoning code, daylight planes, and setbacks as well as a planner will submit plans that pass review faster. |
| **Jurisdiction experience** | Design review criteria vary dramatically — what passes in Redwood City gets redesigned three times in Palo Alto (see Ch 2, §2.5). |
| **Plan check efficiency** | Ask: "What is your average number of plan check correction cycles?" Best architects: 1–2 cycles. Average: 3+. |
| **Construction cost awareness** | Architects who design without cost input produce beautiful, unbuildable projects. Require your architect to coordinate with the GC at every design phase. |
| **Responsiveness to corrections** | Plan check corrections are time-critical (see Ch 16, §16.6 on AB 1114 deadlines). Your architect must turn corrections in days, not weeks. |

## Part 4: Common Mistakes — The "Too Late" Failures

### H.9 The 8 Most Expensive Timing Mistakes

Each mistake below is drawn from real Bay Area projects. The cost is not the professional's fee — it is the **project delay and rework** caused by engaging too late.

| # | Mistake | When It Usually Happens | What It Costs | Prevention |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | **No PTR before design** | Architect starts design without reading title exceptions | $50K–$200K in redesign when easements surface | Order PTR Day 1 of DD; read every Schedule B exception |
| 2 | **No geotech before design** | Foundation type assumed, not verified; design doesn't accommodate actual soil conditions | $30K–$150K in foundation redesign | Order geotech borings in first week of DD |
| 3 | **No land use attorney at pre-app** | Developer attends pre-app alone; misses legal leverage points; accepts staff interpretation without challenge | $100K+ in unnecessary entitlement conditions | Land use attorney attends every pre-app conference |
| 4 | **No civil engineer at pre-app** | Public Works issues (PUE, stormwater, grading) surface 12+ months into plan check instead of Day 1 | $200K–$800K (Peninsula project — see App E) | Civil engineer attends pre-app |
| 5 | **No GC pricing during design** | Architect completes CDs; GC prices 30% over budget; 3+ months of value engineering | $50K–$100K redesign cost + 3 month delay | GC provides budget check at SD, DD, and CD milestones |
| 6 | **No CEQA consultant before filing** | Application filed without CEQA strategy; city determines EIR required; 18-month surprise | $100K–$500K+ in EIR cost + 18 month delay | Environmental consultant determines CEQA pathway before application |
| 7 | **No traffic engineer for hotel/large MF** | Traffic impact not studied; opposition files "fair argument" based on traffic; EIR triggered | $100K–$300K EIR cost + 12–18 month delay | Traffic engineer scoping at pre-app for any project >20 units or hotel |
| 8 | **Surveyor not ordered early enough** | ALTA survey takes 4–6 weeks; architect starts design without survey; first floor plan doesn't match lot | 4–8 week delay + $15K–$30K redesign | Order ALTA on Day 3–5 of DD with rush turnaround if needed |

## Practitioner Checklist

### Before LOI (Phase 0)
- [ ] Run 3-minute GIS screen yourself — no professionals needed
- [ ] Run 5-minute Napkin Test (Appendix G, Level 1) — no professionals needed
- [ ] Quick call to land use attorney only if jurisdiction is unfamiliar

### Due Diligence Period (Phase 1)
- [ ] Day 1: Order preliminary title report (PTR)
- [ ] Day 3–5: Order ALTA/NSPS survey with appropriate Table A items
- [ ] Day 5–7: Engage geotechnical engineer for soil borings
- [ ] Day 5–7: Engage environmental consultant for Phase I ESA (if warranted)
- [ ] Day 7–10: Engage land use attorney for zoning analysis and entitlement strategy
- [ ] Day 10–15: Engage transaction attorney for PSA review and entity formation
- [ ] Day 10–15: Engage arborist if heritage trees are present
- [ ] Day 15–20: Engage civil engineer for preliminary utility/grading feasibility
- [ ] Day 20–30: Engage GC for preliminary cost estimate
- [ ] Day 20–30: Engage CPA for entity and tax structure advice
- [ ] Day 20–30: Engage lender for pre-qualification and term sheet

### Pre-Design (Phase 2)
- [ ] Pre-application conference scheduled with ALL departments
- [ ] Land use attorney confirmed to attend pre-app
- [ ] Civil engineer confirmed to attend pre-app
- [ ] Architect engaged for preliminary massing study (AFTER constraints are known)
- [ ] Written pre-app summary sent to all attendees for confirmation

### Design & Entitlement (Phase 3)
- [ ] Architect has PTR, ALTA survey, geotech report, and zoning analysis BEFORE drawing Line 1
- [ ] GC provides budget check at schematic design
- [ ] GC provides updated estimate at design development
- [ ] Environmental consultant engaged BEFORE application filed
- [ ] Traffic engineer scoped (if CUP, hotel, or 20+ units)
- [ ] Land use attorney preparing for hearing at least 30 days in advance

### Building Permit (Phase 4)
- [ ] Architect coordinates all consultant plans into unified permit set
- [ ] Civil engineer responds directly to Public Works plan check comments
- [ ] Structural engineer available for plan check conferences
- [ ] GC provides final GMP/lump sum before permit issuance

### Construction (Phase 5)
- [ ] Geotechnical engineer present at foundation excavation
- [ ] Structural engineer performs field observation at framing
- [ ] Special inspector engaged for all required inspections
- [ ] Land use attorney reviews conditions of approval 30 days before C of O request

## Key Takeaway

> The development team is not a cost center — it is a **risk reduction engine**.
