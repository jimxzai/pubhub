# Chapter 14: Civil Engineering, Stormwater & Environmental Site Assessment

**Statutory Reference**: Clean Water Act §402 (NPDES/SWPPP); 40 CFR Part 312 (ASTM E1527, Phase I ESA); California Water Code §13000+; Health & Safety Code §25300+ (Hazardous Substances)

## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

Civil engineering, stormwater management, and environmental assessment are three interconnected disciplines that directly impact site design, construction cost, and regulatory compliance—yet are frequently underestimated in pro forma budgeting. This chapter decodes Bay Area stormwater (C.3) requirements that consume 5–10% of buildable site area, plus the environmental screening protocols that catch contamination liabilities early. Grading plans establish cut/fill balance, drainage adequacy, and retaining wall requirements ($50–$150/SF per wall face). More critically, Provision C.3 of the Bay Area Municipal Regional Permit mandates stormwater treatment for projects creating 10,000+ SF of impervious surface—bioretention areas that must sit 10 feet minimum from building foundations, often eating 8–15% of site area on typical projects. Hotels with large parking footprints and restaurant grease systems face additional requirements (grease interceptors, oil/water separators, parking area containment). Phase I ESAs ($3K–$6K) identify Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs) requiring Phase II investigation ($15K–$100K); adjacent property contamination (within 1,000 ft) must be evaluated for vapor intrusion risk. Menlo Park case study: C.3 stormwater consumed 12% of site area, forcing redesign of building footprint and loss of 1 unit + 8 parking spaces. Palo Alto case: SWPPP violations resulted in $45K fine + 2-week stop-work order + mandatory third-party monitoring. San Carlos Hotel Indigo: $150K environmental assessment suite (Phase I, Phase II, vapor intrusion, hazmat surveys) uncovered nearby tetrachloroethylene plume requiring precautionary vapor mitigation. **Core principle**: design stormwater management and environmental constraints before finalizing building footprint, not after.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **C.3 stormwater is the silent site-plan killer**: Budget 5–10% of site area for bioretention or other treatment measures. Bioretention requires 10-foot setbacks from building foundations, meaning usable building envelope shrinks by that setback distance per side. Menlo Park case: 2-acre site lost 1 unit to stormwater compliance—equivalent to $600K revenue reduction. Design stormwater early; don't treat it as post-design obligation.

- **Stormwater treatment options create different site planning impacts**: Bioretention is most common (planted, filters runoff); pervious paving reduces impervious calculation (parking, driveways); green roofs reduce impervious total while adding structural load. Evaluate each option's site footprint impact early in massing studies—choice of treatment directly affects buildable area and revenue.

- **Hotels require specialized stormwater controls beyond standard C.3**: Grease interceptors for food service (required by law); oil/water separators for parking areas; fire access lane maintenance; loading dock containment; and emergency generator fuel secondary containment. Budget these as part of landscape/site utility design, not afterthought. Environmental health departments inspect these regularly.

- **SWPPP compliance is actively enforced by Regional Water Quality Control Board**: Phase I ESAs ($3K–$6K) are mandatory on all sites. Projects disturbing 1+ acre require SWPPP under NPDES General Construction Permit—not optional. Palo Alto case: sediment-laden runoff to creek = $45K fine + stop-work + mandatory third-party monitoring = $120K total cost. Budget professional SWPPP preparation and qualified SWPPP practitioners (QSPs); prevention ($15K–$30K) is fraction of violation cost.

- **Phase I ESA findings categorize contamination risk**: REC (Recognized Environmental Condition) requires Phase II investigation; CREC (Controlled REC) with institutional controls may proceed; HREC (Historical REC) generally requires documentation only; De Minimis conditions need only note in file. San Carlos Hotel Indigo: nearby tetrachloroethylene plume triggered Phase II and vapor intrusion mitigation (precautionary but required by EIR).

- **Adjacent property contamination within 1,000 ft must be evaluated**: Check DTSC EnviroStor and SWRCB GeoTracker for sites within 1,000 ft (not just on your parcel). Groundwater plumes migrate; vapor intrusion can occur even if contamination is off-site. Budget Phase II and vapor intrusion analysis if upgradient contamination identified.

- **Grading cost overruns are endemic in Bay Area development**: Civil engineers often underestimate earthwork costs by 25–40%. Rock layers at unexpected depths, utility conflicts, and environmental constraints surface during construction. San Jose subdivision case: geotech estimate $85K, actual $125K due to rock layer at 4 feet. Budget grading at 1.4x civil engineer estimate; require test pits (not just borings) to 8 feet minimum.

- **Environmental assessment costs are NOT just Phase I—plan for full suite**: Phase I ($3K–$6K) + Phase II if triggered ($15K–$100K) + vapor intrusion ($5K–$50K) + asbestos/lead surveys if demolition ($10K–$50K). San Carlos Hotel Indigo case: $150K total environmental assessment before EIR costs. Include environmental contingency (5–10% of soft costs) for surprises.

:::

::: checklist

**Grading & Drainage:**

- [ ] Review grading plan for earthwork balance: cut/fill quantities, import/export needs and costs ($15–$40/CY)
- [ ] Verify positive drainage away from all structures; identify low spots that may pond water
- [ ] Assess retaining wall requirements and costs ($50–$150/SF wall face); note wall heights and locations
- [ ] Check ADA compliance for paths of travel (max 5% running slope, 2% cross slope)
- [ ] For modular/ICF projects: assess subgrade conditions and bearing capacity (from geotech report)

**Stormwater (C.3) Requirements:**

- [ ] Determine if project triggers C.3 (10,000+ SF impervious surface created/replaced)
- [ ] If C.3 triggered: budget 5–10% of site area for stormwater treatment (bioretention, permeable paving, green roof, detention)
- [ ] Design treatment measures compatible with site layout; factor 10-foot foundation setbacks for bioretention into building footprint
- [ ] Evaluate pervious paving strategy to reduce impervious surface calculation and recover buildable area
- [ ] Calculate stormwater management plan (ongoing maintenance obligations, property owner responsibilities)
- [ ] For hotels: specify grease interceptors (food service), oil/water separators (parking), loading dock containment, emergency generator secondary containment

**SWPPP & NPDES Compliance:**

- [ ] Determine if project disturbs 1+ acre (triggers SWPPP requirement)
- [ ] If SWPPP required: budget professional SWPPP preparation ($3K–$8K) and Qualified SWPPP Practitioner (QSP) management ($15K–$30K)
- [ ] Plan for SWPPP filing through State Water Board SMARTS system
- [ ] Designate Legally Responsible Person (LRP) for SWPPP compliance
- [ ] Develop erosion and sediment control BMPs appropriate to site risk level (Level 1, 2, or 3)
- [ ] For near-creek/sensitive-water sites: budget enhanced BMPs and continuous monitoring (more expensive but enforcement risk is high)

**Phase I ESA & Environmental Screening:**

- [ ] Complete Phase I ESA before closing ($3K–$6K); mandatory due diligence
- [ ] Review all Phase I findings: categorize as REC, CREC, HREC, or De Minimis
- [ ] Check DTSC EnviroStor database for contamination sites (on-site and within 1,000 ft)
- [ ] Check SWRCB GeoTracker database for groundwater contamination (may be upgradient, affecting vapor intrusion)
- [ ] For any REC identified: budget Phase II investigation ($15K–$100K depending on scope)
- [ ] For RECs with potential vapor intrusion: budget soil vapor testing and mitigation if positive ($5K–$50K)

**Phase II ESA (if Phase I triggers):**

- [ ] If Phase I identified RECs, engage Phase II consultant: soil/groundwater sampling, laboratory analysis, fate/transport modeling
- [ ] Budget Phase II at $15K–$100K depending on site complexity and number of test pits/monitoring wells
- [ ] Timeline: 4–8 weeks for field work and reporting
- [ ] Compare Phase II results against regulatory screening levels (EPA/DTSC standards)
- [ ] If contamination confirmed: develop remediation plan (cost $50K–$500K+); may require additional Phase III assessment

**Site-Specific Considerations:**

- [ ] For South Bay sites near creeks (Palo Alto, San Jose): evaluate Endangered Species Act compliance if in protected habitat
- [ ] For Bay-adjacent sites (Menlo Park): liquefaction risk requires coordination with geotech (Chapter 13) + stormwater (elevated design)
- [ ] For sites with basements: coordinate drainage design with geotech water table analysis; plan dewatering if water table shallow
- [ ] For modular projects with heavy point loads: verify grading plan accommodates foundation requirements from Chapter 12

**Cost Budgeting & Timeline:**

- [ ] C.3 stormwater compliance: budget $50K–$200K total (design + construction) plus ongoing maintenance obligations
- [ ] SWPPP preparation + monitoring: $15K–$30K for project duration
- [ ] Phase I ESA: $3K–$6K (mandatory before closing)
- [ ] Phase II ESA (if triggered): $15K–$100K; adds 4–8 weeks to timeline
- [ ] Grading: budget civil engineer estimate × 1.4 for actual cost; require test pits before finalizing estimate
- [ ] Environmental contingency: 5–10% of soft costs for surprises

## Overview

Civil engineering, stormwater management, and environmental assessment are three interconnected disciplines that directly impact **site design, construction cost, and regulatory compliance**. In the Bay Area, stormwater requirements alone can consume 5–10% of buildable site area.

---

## Part A: Grading and Drainage

### 14.1 Grading Plans

Grading plans show existing and proposed contours, cut/fill quantities, drainage patterns, retaining walls, and erosion control measures.

### Key Review Items

| Item | What to Evaluate | Cost Impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Earthwork Balance** | Cut/fill quantities; import or export needed | $15–$30/CY for import; $20–$40/CY for export + disposal |
| **Drainage Adequacy** | Positive drainage away from structures; no ponding | Re-grading costs if inadequate |
| **Retaining Walls** | Height, type, location relative to property lines | $50–$150/SF of wall face |
| **Erosion Control** | Temporary BMPs during construction | Required for grading permit |
| **Accessibility** | ADA-compliant slopes and paths of travel | Maximum 5% running slope; 2% cross slope |

---

## Part B: Stormwater Management

### 14.2 C.3 Stormwater Requirements (Bay Area)

In the San Francisco Bay Area, **Provision C.3** of the Municipal Regional Permit (MRP) requires new development and redevelopment projects that create or replace **10,000+ SF of impervious surface** to implement stormwater treatment measures.

### Treatment Options

| Measure | Description | Site Impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Bioretention** | Planted areas that filter runoff through engineered soil | Most common; consumes buildable area |
| **Pervious Paving** | Permeable concrete, pavers, or asphalt | Reduces impervious area; maintenance requirements |
| **Green Roofs** | Vegetated roof assemblies | Reduces impervious calculation; structural load increase |
| **Rain Gardens** | Shallow planted depressions | Landscape integration; setback from foundations |
| **Detention/Retention** | Underground or surface storage | Space for peak flow management |

### Site Planning Implications

- **Budget 5–10% of site area** for stormwater treatment on typical projects
- Bioretention areas require **setbacks from foundations** (typically 10 ft minimum)
- Ongoing **maintenance obligations** must be documented in a Stormwater Management Plan
- Hotels have **large impervious footprints** — more treatment area required

### Hotel-Specific Requirements

| Requirement | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Grease interceptors** | Required for food service areas |
| **Oil/water separators** | Required for parking areas |
| **Fire access** | Must maintain fire lane access around building perimeter |
| **Loading dock design** | Containment for spills and wash-down |
| **Generator fuel containment** | Secondary containment for emergency generators |

---

### 14.3 SWPPP and NPDES Requirements

Projects disturbing **one or more acres of soil** require a Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan (SWPPP) under the NPDES General Construction Permit.

### SWPPP Requirements

- Erosion and sediment control BMPs
- Hazardous materials management
- Monitoring requirements throughout construction
- Filed through the State Water Board's **SMARTS system**
- A **Legally Responsible Person (LRP)** must be designated
- Active from grading permit through final stabilization

### Risk Categories

| Risk Level | Total Rainfall/Erosion | Requirements |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Level 1** | Low | Standard BMPs, visual monitoring |
| **Level 2** | Moderate | Enhanced BMPs, sampling during rain events |
| **Level 3** | High | Maximum BMPs, continuous monitoring, rain event action plans |

## Part C: Environmental Site Assessment

### 14.4 Phase I ESA (ASTM E1527-21)

A Phase I ESA evaluates the potential for contamination through records review, site reconnaissance, interviews, and evaluation of adjoining properties.

### Finding Categories

| Finding | Acronym | Meaning | Action Required |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Recognized Environmental Condition** | REC | Likely release or material threat of release of hazardous substances | Phase II investigation recommended |
| **Controlled REC** | CREC | Past release with institutional/engineering controls in place | Evaluate controls; may affect development design |
| **Historical REC** | HREC | Past release that has been addressed to regulatory standards | Generally no action; document in due diligence |
| **De Minimis Condition** | N/A | Environmental condition unlikely to warrant further investigation | Note in file; no action needed |

### Phase II ESA (If Triggered)

If a Phase I identifies RECs, a Phase II investigation involves:
- **Soil sampling**: Bore holes, test pits
- **Groundwater sampling**: Monitoring wells
- **Soil vapor testing**: For volatile organic compounds
- **Laboratory analysis**: Against regulatory screening levels
- **Cost**: $15K–$100K+ depending on scope
- **Timeline**: 4–8 weeks for field work and reporting

### Common Contamination Sources

- Former gas stations, dry cleaners, auto repair shops
- Agricultural use (pesticides, herbicides)
- Underground storage tanks (USTs)
- Industrial operations (solvents, heavy metals)
- Adjacent properties with known contamination

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Menlo Park — C.3 Stormwater Consumes 12% of Site Area

A developer designing a 3-story, 80-unit apartment complex on a 2-acre site in Menlo Park's Belle Haven neighborhood discovered that **C.3 stormwater compliance required 10,500 SF of bioretention** — 12% of the total site area. The bioretention areas also required 10-ft setbacks from building foundations, further constraining the building footprint.

The original site plan had to be completely redesigned: the building was repositioned, 8 parking spaces were eliminated, and one ground-floor unit was removed to accommodate bioretention planters. The net loss: 1 unit ($600K revenue reduction) plus 8 parking spaces.

The developer eventually incorporated pervious concrete pavers in the parking area, which reduced the impervious surface calculation and brought the bioretention requirement down to 7,800 SF. This recovered 4 parking spaces but not the lost unit.

**Lesson Learned**: In the Bay Area, design stormwater management before finalizing your building footprint — not after. C.3 requirements are non-negotiable and can consume 8–15% of site area on typical projects. Consider pervious paving and green roofs early in design to reduce the bioretention footprint. Every square foot of reduced impervious surface translates directly to recovered buildable area.

---

### Case 2: Hotel Indigo San Carlos — Full Environmental Assessment Suite

The Hotel Indigo project at 501 Industrial Road in San Carlos required an extensive environmental assessment suite as part of its EIR. The 2.09-acre site, previously occupied by three single-story commercial buildings, required:

- **Phase I ESA**: Identified RECs from a former dry cleaner within 500 ft (tetrachloroethylene contamination in groundwater). The developer had to demonstrate that the contamination plume did not extend beneath the hotel site.
- **Phase II Investigation**: Soil vapor sampling confirmed the site itself was clean, but a vapor intrusion mitigation system was required as a precautionary measure in the EIR mitigation monitoring plan.
- **Hydrology study**: The site's proximity to Pulgas Creek required detailed drainage analysis and enhanced stormwater treatment.
- **Hazardous materials assessment**: The existing commercial buildings required asbestos and lead paint surveys before demolition.

Total environmental assessment cost: approximately $150K — before the EIR itself.

**Lesson Learned**: Peninsula sites with prior commercial/industrial use should budget $100K–$200K for environmental assessments alone (Phase I, Phase II, vapor intrusion, hazmat surveys). These costs are in addition to the EIR. The Hotel Indigo San Carlos case shows that even a relatively clean site can trigger extensive environmental investigation due to nearby contamination sources. Always check EnviroStor and GeoTracker for contamination within 1,000 ft of your site, not just on your parcel.

---

### Case 3: Palo Alto — SWPPP Compliance and Construction Delays

A residential developer in Palo Alto's Barron Park neighborhood was cited by the Regional Water Quality Control Board for SWPPP violations during grading. Sediment-laden runoff from an inadequately protected stockpile entered Matadero Creek after a heavy rain event. The resulting enforcement action included:

- **$45K administrative fine**
- **2-week stop-work order** while enhanced BMPs were installed
- **Mandatory third-party SWPPP monitoring** for the remainder of construction ($2K/week)
- **Negative press coverage** that complicated community relations for the project

The total cost of the violation: approximately $120K in fines, monitoring, enhanced BMPs, and schedule delay.

**Lesson Learned**: SWPPP compliance is not optional — the RWQCB actively enforces in the Bay Area, especially near sensitive waterways. Budget for professional SWPPP preparation and qualified SWPPP practitioners (QSPs) to manage on-site compliance. The cost of proper SWPPP management ($15K–$30K for the project) is a fraction of the cost of a violation. Near Bay Area creeks and waterways, enforcement risk is especially high.

> **C.3 stormwater requirements are the silent site-plan killer** — they consume buildable area, require foundation setbacks, and add ongoing maintenance. Budget for them early. Phase I ESAs are non-negotiable — never close on a property without one. A $5K Phase I can save you from a $5M remediation liability.
