# Appendix A: California Wildfire Reconstruction — Cases & Lessons Learned

**Pacific Palisades | Santa Monica | Paradise | Santa Rosa**

---

## Overview

California's devastating wildfires have created **urgent reconstruction demand** across the state. Wildfire rebuilding operates under a distinct regulatory framework — streamlined permitting, relaxed zoning in some cases, and massive demand for construction labor and materials. For developers, wildfire zones present both opportunities (fast-track permits, modular demand) and challenges (contamination, infrastructure gaps, insurance crises).

---

## A.1 Los Angeles — Pacific Palisades & Eaton Fires (January 2025)

### What Happened

The Palisades Fire destroyed approximately **5,300+ structures** in Pacific Palisades, Malibu, and surrounding areas in January 2025. The Eaton Fire destroyed approximately **7,000+ structures** in Altadena and Pasadena. Combined, these fires caused an estimated **$250B+ in damage** — the costliest natural disaster in US history.

### Regulatory Response

| Action | Authority | Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Executive Order N-4-25** | Governor Newsom | Suspended CEQA for rebuilding; waived permit fees; streamlined plan check |
| **Emergency permits** | LA City/County | Same-footprint rebuilding permitted by right with expedited plan check |
| **FEMA debris removal** | Federal/FEMA | Government-funded hazardous debris removal before rebuilding |
| **SB 9/ADU overlay** | State law | Rebuilding may include additional units under SB 9 and ADU law |
| **Insurance emergency** | CDI | Moratorium on policy cancellations in fire zones |

### Reconstruction Challenges

| Challenge | Impact | Developer Implication |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Hazardous debris** | Ash contains lead, asbestos, heavy metals, household chemicals | Phase II-level environmental clearance before rebuilding |
| **Soil contamination** | Fire debris contaminates topsoil; some sites require remediation | Budget $10K–$50K per lot for soil testing and remediation |
| **Infrastructure destruction** | Water mains, sewer lines, gas lines, electrical grid damaged | Rebuilding depends on utility restoration timeline |
| **Labor shortage** | 5,000+ homes competing for same contractors simultaneously | Construction costs inflate 20–40% during peak rebuild |
| **Insurance crisis** | Many homeowners underinsured; insurers leaving CA market | Rebuilding cost may exceed insurance proceeds |
| **Coastal Act** | Palisades is in Coastal Zone; Coastal Commission jurisdiction | Even with CEQA waiver, Coastal permits may still apply |

### Modular Opportunity

The Palisades/Eaton rebuild represents the **largest-ever opportunity** for modular and prefab construction in California:

- **5,000+ homes** need to be rebuilt in a compressed timeline
- Conventional construction labor is severely constrained
- Factory-built homes can bypass on-site labor shortages
- Panelized and volumetric systems can deliver homes in 4–6 months vs. 12–18 months conventional
- Governor's executive order explicitly encourages prefab/modular approaches

### Lessons Learned

> **Lesson 1**: Wildfire rebuilding is a **speed game** — homeowners who deploy modular/prefab are back in their homes 6–12 months faster than those waiting for conventional construction. Position modular as the solution to labor scarcity, not just cost savings.

> **Lesson 2**: Even with CEQA waivers, the **Coastal Act still applies** in Pacific Palisades. Developers must navigate Coastal Commission requirements for any rebuilding that changes the building footprint, increases height, or modifies lot coverage. The CEQA waiver does not override Coastal Act jurisdiction.

> **Lesson 3**: **Soil contamination from fire debris** is the hidden cost of wildfire rebuilding. Every lot requires environmental clearance before foundation work. Budget for this in your pro forma — it's not optional.

## A.2 Paradise (Camp Fire — November 2018)

### What Happened

The Camp Fire destroyed **18,804 structures** in Paradise and surrounding areas — the deadliest and most destructive wildfire in California history at that time. The town of Paradise (population 26,000) was essentially erased.

### Reconstruction Timeline (7+ Years)

| Phase | Timeline | Status |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Debris removal** | 2019–2020 | Complete (FEMA/CalOES funded) |
| **Infrastructure restoration** | 2019–2023 | Water system rebuilt; some roads still pending |
| **Permit applications** | 2019–present | ~4,500 permits issued (of 11,000+ destroyed homes) |
| **Homes rebuilt** | 2019–2026 | ~3,500 completed (less than 20% of pre-fire housing stock) |
| **Population recovery** | 2019–2026 | ~10,000 residents returned (40% of pre-fire) |

### Why Reconstruction Has Been So Slow

1. **Insurance gaps**: Average insurance payout covered only 60–70% of rebuilding cost; many residents couldn't afford the difference
2. **Infrastructure bottleneck**: Water system had to be completely rebuilt before building permits could be issued
3. **Labor scarcity**: Remote location made it difficult to attract construction crews
4. **Contractor fraud**: Multiple cases of contractor fraud against vulnerable homeowners
5. **Code upgrades**: Rebuilding to current code (WUI fire standards, Title 24 energy) significantly more expensive than original construction
6. **Emotional/financial exhaustion**: Many residents chose to relocate rather than rebuild

### Modular in Paradise

Several modular/manufactured home companies entered the Paradise market. Factory-built homes were the **fastest path to occupancy** — some homeowners were in modular homes within 6 months while neighbors waited 2+ years for conventional construction. However, modular homes faced stigma from some residents who associated them with "temporary" housing.

### Lessons Learned

> **Lesson 1**: **Infrastructure restoration is the gating factor**, not permitting. In Paradise, building permits were available within months, but the water system took 3+ years to fully restore. Developers must assess infrastructure timelines before committing to wildfire rebuild projects.

> **Lesson 2**: The **insurance gap** is the largest barrier to rebuilding. Many homeowners are 30–40% short of actual rebuilding costs. Developers who can offer cost-efficient solutions (modular, panelized, standardized designs) capture the market. Premium custom homes are not what this market needs.

> **Lesson 3**: Wildfire reconstruction at the **community scale** (thousands of homes) takes 7–10+ years. Paradise is still less than 20% rebuilt after 7 years. Factor this reality into any development strategy — the demand is real but the absorption is slow.

## A.3 Santa Rosa (Tubbs Fire — October 2017)

### What Happened

The Tubbs Fire destroyed approximately **5,300 structures** in Santa Rosa, including the **Coffey Park** and **Fountaingrove** neighborhoods. These were established suburban neighborhoods with homes valued at $400K–$1.5M.

### Reconstruction: A Success Story

Santa Rosa's rebuilding has been **the most successful** large-scale wildfire reconstruction in California history:

| Metric | Result |
| --- | --- |
| **Permits issued** | ~3,800 (of ~5,300 destroyed) |
| **Homes rebuilt** | ~3,200 completed by 2024 |
| **Average rebuild time** | 18–24 months per home |
| **Modular/prefab share** | ~15% of rebuilds |
| **Population recovery** | ~85% of pre-fire levels |

### Why Santa Rosa Succeeded Where Paradise Struggled

| Factor | Santa Rosa | Paradise |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Location** | Urban/suburban; near SF Bay Area labor market | Rural; 90 min from Sacramento |
| **Infrastructure** | Largely intact (underground utilities survived) | Completely destroyed |
| **Insurance coverage** | Higher policy limits; better-insured homeowners | Many underinsured |
| **Property values** | Higher values justified rebuilding costs | Lower values made rebuilding uneconomical for some |
| **City response** | Expedited permits; dedicated rebuild center | Small town with limited staff |
| **Labor access** | Bay Area construction labor pool | Remote; labor had to be imported |

### Coffey Park — Tract Rebuild Model

Coffey Park (1,500+ homes destroyed) was rebuilt largely as **tract housing** by production builders who purchased burned lots in bulk. This approach offered:
- **Standardized designs** that reduced plan check time to 2 weeks
- **Economies of scale** in purchasing and subcontracting
- **Faster permitting** because repetitive plans only needed review once
- **Lower cost per SF** than custom rebuilding

Several production builders used **panelized construction** for wall and roof assemblies, reducing framing time from 3 weeks to 1 week per home.

### Fountaingrove — Custom Rebuild Challenges

Fountaingrove (higher-value homes, $800K–$1.5M) faced longer rebuild timelines because:
- Custom homes required individual architectural design and plan check
- Steep hillside sites required new geotechnical reports and retaining walls
- WUI fire-hardening requirements (Class A roof, ember-resistant vents, noncombustible decking) added $40K–$80K per home
- Some lots had access issues that complicated construction staging

### Lessons Learned

> **Lesson 1**: **Production building at scale** is the fastest path to community recovery. Coffey Park's tract rebuild model — standardized designs, bulk lot acquisition, repetitive plan checks — outperformed custom rebuilding by 6–12 months per home. Developers who can offer standardized designs with limited customization options will capture the largest share of wildfire rebuild demand.

> **Lesson 2**: **WUI (Wildland-Urban Interface) fire-hardening** is now mandatory for all rebuilding in fire zones. Budget $40K–$80K per home for: Class A roofing, ember-resistant vents, tempered glass windows, noncombustible 5-ft defensible space zone, fire-resistant siding. These requirements significantly increase rebuilding costs compared to the original construction.

> **Lesson 3**: Santa Rosa succeeded because it had **urban advantages** that Paradise lacked: intact infrastructure, proximity to labor, higher property values, and a city government that dedicated resources to expedited permitting. When evaluating wildfire rebuild opportunities, assess these structural factors before committing — not all fire zones offer equal rebuilding economics.

## A.4 Regulatory Framework for Wildfire Rebuilding

### Expedited Permitting

| Provision | What It Does | Limitations |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Governor's Executive Orders** | Suspend CEQA, waive fees, streamline review | Temporary; typically 1–3 year duration |
| **Same-footprint rebuilding** | Rebuild to same size/location by right | May not apply if lot has changed (erosion, debris flow) |
| **GC §65852.25** | Expedited ADU permits in disaster zones | Encourages additional density during rebuild |
| **SB 9 overlay** | Lot splits/duplexes available during rebuild | Can add 2–4 units to former single-family lots |
| **WUI fire standards** | CBC Chapter 7A requirements | Mandatory; cannot be waived |

### Environmental Considerations

- **Debris removal**: FEMA-funded removal of hazardous materials required before rebuilding
- **Soil testing**: Post-fire lots may have elevated levels of lead, arsenic, asbestos
- **Erosion/debris flow**: Burned hillsides are susceptible to mudslides for 3–5 years post-fire
- **Stormwater**: Post-fire runoff carries contaminants; enhanced stormwater treatment may be required
- **Biological**: Fire can destroy protected habitat, triggering biological resource reviews for rebuilding

## A.5 Modular Construction for Wildfire Reconstruction

### Why Modular Fits

| Advantage | Wildfire Context |
| --- | --- |
| **Speed** | Homes delivered 4–6 months vs. 12–18 conventional |
| **Labor independence** | Factory workers are not competing with on-site labor pool |
| **Quality control** | Consistent fire-hardening standards in factory environment |
| **Cost predictability** | Fixed-price contracts reduce financial risk for homeowners |
| **Scalability** | Factory can produce dozens of homes per month |

### Modular Challenges in Fire Zones

| Challenge | Mitigation |
| --- | --- |
| **Site access** | Fire-damaged roads may not accommodate module transport; verify route |
| **Foundation readiness** | Debris removal and soil remediation must be complete before foundation pour |
| **Utility connections** | Must coordinate with utility restoration timeline |
| **Community perception** | Some residents associate modular with "temporary"; use quality finishes and marketing |
| **WUI compliance** | Modules must be manufactured with CBC Chapter 7A fire-hardening features |

## A.6 Frontier Developer Profile: Thomas James Homes (TJH) — Palisades Rebuild

**Thomas James Homes** (tjh.com) is the most significant production-scale player in the Pacific Palisades wildfire rebuild. With **350+ homes in annual production** and divisions across California, the Pacific Northwest, Colorado, and Arizona, TJH has pivoted its infill teardown-rebuild expertise to wildfire reconstruction at scale.

### TJH Business Model

TJH operates in a niche that is uniquely suited to wildfire rebuilding: **single-lot infill teardown-rebuild** in established neighborhoods. Their core model — demolishing aging homes and building new ones on the same lot — is operationally identical to post-fire lot reconstruction.

| Metric | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Annual Production** | 350+ homes |
| **Markets** | SoCal, NorCal (Silicon Valley), Pacific Northwest, Colorado, Arizona |
| **Palisades Price Guarantee** | $650/SF firm price upon contract execution |
| **Delivery Guarantee** | 12 months from permit approval, or TJH pays the homeowner per-day penalties |
| **Construction Debt Facility** | $400M (from Ascent Developer Solutions) |
| **Floorplan Library** | ~50 floorplans, 3-4 elevations, 4-5 structural options |

### TJH Palisades Rebuild Program

TJH completed the **first showcase rebuild home in Pacific Palisades** after the January 2025 fires. Their program offers:

- **Guaranteed firm price** of $650/SF — eliminating cost overrun risk for fire survivors
- **12-month completion guarantee** from permit approval — with daily penalties paid to the homeowner for delays
- **BUILD by TJH** program — turnkey design-build solution on the homeowner's existing lot
- **Pre-designed floorplans** — avoiding the 6-12 month custom design process

### Why TJH Matters for Wildfire Reconstruction

The conventional custom rebuild model fails at scale in wildfire zones because:

1. Custom architects take 6-12 months for design alone
2. Custom bids have no price certainty — costs escalate in labor-scarce markets
3. Individual homeowners have no negotiating leverage with subcontractors

TJH solves all three: pre-designed plans (no design delay), guaranteed pricing (no cost escalation), and production-scale procurement (subcontractor leverage). Their $400M construction debt facility gives them the balance sheet to carry dozens of projects simultaneously.

**Lesson Learned**: TJH's model is the closest thing to **production homebuilding in an infill/rebuild context**. For developers evaluating wildfire reconstruction opportunities, the TJH playbook — standardized designs, guaranteed pricing, production-scale operations — is the model to study. Their willingness to accept penalty risk on delivery timelines signals genuine operational confidence.

## Cross-Reference: Financial Framework for Wildfire Reconstruction

Wildfire rebuilding requires a modified version of the standard development pro forma. Key adjustments for Appendix G's framework:

| Standard Pro Forma Line | Wildfire Modification | App G Level |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Land Acquisition** | Often $0 (homeowner owns lot) or fire-sale pricing | Level 1: Napkin Test land basis fundamentally different |
| **Site Work** | Add $10K–$50K/lot for debris removal + soil remediation | Level 2: new line item |
| **Construction Cost** | +20–40% inflation during peak rebuild labor shortage | Level 3: Stress Test — model labor cost escalation |
| **Insurance Proceeds** | 60–70% of actual rebuilding cost (Paradise data) | Level 2: Sources — insurance gap must be funded |

## Key Takeaway

> California wildfire reconstruction is a **massive, recurring market** driven by climate change, aging infrastructure, and development patterns in the WUI. The developers who succeed are those who offer **speed, cost predictability, and fire-hardened construction** — all strengths of modular building. Study Santa Rosa's Coffey Park model for the production-scale approach, and position modular as the solution to the labor shortage that constrains every rebuild.
