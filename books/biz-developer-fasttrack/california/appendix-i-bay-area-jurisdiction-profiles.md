# Appendix I: Bay Area Jurisdiction Profiles

**Developer's Quick Reference for Santa Clara County & San Mateo County**

28 Jurisdictions — Entitlement Pathways, Timelines, Design Review & Strategic Assessment

---

## How to Use This Appendix

This appendix provides a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction reference for every city in Santa Clara County (South Bay) and San Mateo County (Peninsula) that a developer is likely to target. Each profile answers four questions:

1. **Can I build here?** — Zoning framework, General Plan constraints, Housing Element status
2. **How fast?** — Typical entitlement timeline, ministerial pathway availability (SB 35, SB 9, ADU)
3. **How hard?** — Design review process, political environment, CEQA exposure
4. **Is it worth it?** — Land cost context, fee levels, strategic developer assessment

### Reading the Profiles

Each city profile uses a standardized format:

- **Developer Difficulty Rating**: 1 (easiest) to 5 (most difficult) — a composite score reflecting design review burden, political opposition risk, entitlement timeline, and regulatory complexity
- **SB 35 Eligible**: Whether the city is subject to SB 35/SB 423 streamlining (most Bay Area cities are, as few have met their RHNA targets across all income categories)
- **Housing Element Status**: 6th Cycle (2023–2031) HCD certification status
- **Cross-References**: Links to case studies and detailed analysis elsewhere in this book

### Important Caveats

- Regulatory environments change. Housing Element status, SB 35 eligibility, and fee schedules are current as of **February 2026**. Always verify with the jurisdiction before committing capital.
- All Bay Area cities are subject to **SB 330** (Housing Crisis Act) protections through 2036, including the preliminary application fee-lock and the prohibition on downzoning.
- **SB 423** (2023) extended and expanded SB 35 streamlining through 2036.

## I.1 Regional Overview: Two Counties, Two Development Cultures

### Santa Clara County — The Innovation Corridor

Santa Clara County (population ~1.95M) is the economic engine of Silicon Valley. Development culture varies dramatically from north to south:

- **North County** (Palo Alto, Mountain View, Sunnyvale, Santa Clara, Cupertino): High land values ($3M–$15M/acre), intense design review, sophisticated NIMBY opposition, but strong state-law override opportunities (SB 35, density bonus)
- **Central County** (San Jose, Campbell, Los Gatos, Saratoga, Monte Sereno): San Jose offers scale and relative speed; smaller cities are increasingly restrictive
- **South County** (Morgan Hill, Gilroy): Lower land costs, faster approvals, but limited market depth and infrastructure constraints

**6th Cycle RHNA Allocation**: 129,577 units across all jurisdictions (2023–2031)

### San Mateo County — The Peninsula Premium

San Mateo County (population ~760K) commands some of the highest land values in the nation. The development culture is characterized by:

- **Mid-Peninsula** (Menlo Park, Redwood City, San Carlos, Belmont): Active development pipeline, particularly along El Camino Real corridor. Menlo Park and Redwood City are the most developer-friendly
- **North County** (Millbrae, San Mateo, Foster City): Transit-oriented development opportunities near BART and Caltrain
- **Coastside** (Half Moon Bay): Coastal Act overlay creates a fundamentally different regulatory environment
- **Wealthy Enclaves** (Atherton, Hillsborough, Woodside, Portola Valley): SFR-dominated, politically hostile to density, but SB 9 creates limited by-right opportunities

**6th Cycle RHNA Allocation**: 46,418 units across all jurisdictions (2023–2031)

### Cupertino

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Population** | ~60,000 |
| **Developer Difficulty** | 4/5 |
| **SB 35 Eligible** | Yes — significantly behind RHNA targets |
| **Housing Element** | 6th Cycle adopted; HCD certified 2024 (late, after state pressure) |
| **Design Review** | Design Review Committee + Planning Commission — High difficulty |
| **Typical Timeline** | 18–30 months (discretionary); 5–6 months (SB 35) |
| **Key Zoning** | R-3 (multifamily), BQ (Bubb Road Quasi-Public), Heart of the City Specific Plan |
| **RHNA Target** | 4,588 units (2023–2031) |
| **Land Cost** | $4M–$12M/acre |

**Strategic Assessment**: Cupertino has historically been one of the most resistant cities to new housing development in the Bay Area. The city faced HAA enforcement actions from HCD (Chapter 3, Case 4) for denying housing projects that complied with objective standards. The Vallco/The Rise project (former Vallco Shopping Mall) became a statewide symbol of SB 35's power when the developer invoked streamlining for a 2,402-unit mixed-use project that the city had repeatedly blocked. Despite political resistance, state law now forces approvals. The Heart of the City Specific Plan along Stevens Creek Blvd creates the primary development corridor.

**Case Study: Mission Development Group** — Mission Development Group (missiondevel.com) has completed 50+ luxury SFR projects in Cupertino, making them the most active single-family developer in the city. Their portfolio of addresses across Palo Vista Road, Judy Avenue, Heatherwood Drive, and throughout Cupertino's west side demonstrates that even in a high-difficulty jurisdiction, experienced local developers who understand the design review process can maintain consistent production. Their model — acquire existing SFR lots, demolish and rebuild luxury homes ($3M–$6M price points) — navigates Cupertino's design review by maintaining neighborhood-compatible massing while maximizing lot coverage. They also operate in Menlo Park, Los Gatos, Redwood City, and Sunnyvale (385–387 N. Sunnyvale Ave), showing a Peninsula-wide strategy. For developers considering SB 9 lot splits or ADU additions in Cupertino, Mission Development's portfolio demonstrates the achievable per-lot economics.

**Developer Playbook**: SB 35 for larger multifamily projects (the Vallco precedent shows it works). Heart of the City Specific Plan for commercial-to-residential conversion. SFR luxury teardown-rebuild (Mission Development model) remains profitable despite high design review burden. SB 9 lot splits viable on larger lots in R-1 zones.

### Saratoga

| Attribute | Detail |

| **Population** | ~30,500 |
| **Developer Difficulty** | 3/5 (recently improved from 4/5) |
| **SB 35 Eligible** | Yes |
| **Housing Element** | 6th Cycle HCD certified July 2024 (after RHNA appeal denied) |

| **Land Cost** | $4M–$10M/acre |

**Strategic Assessment**: Saratoga is in transition. In January–February 2025, the city adopted **new objective design standards and a ministerial review process** for projects meeting established criteria (including anything over 250 SF) — a landmark shift from the previous fully discretionary system. The Planning Commission approved 4-3 and City Council approved 4-1, indicating significant internal division and community controversy. The Heritage Preservation Commission (heritage trees, historic landmarks, heritage lanes) still adds review complexity in certain areas. The Saratoga Village area is developing new objective design standards (initiated early 2025). SB 684/SB 1123 eligibility for starter home projects (up to 10 units) provides an additional pathway.

**Developer Playbook**: The new ministerial pathway is a game-changer — projects meeting objective standards can receive approval without Planning Commission input. SB 9 and ADU development in R-1 zones. Saratoga Village for small-scale mixed-use. Heritage Preservation overlay adds time in historic areas. Monitor community reaction to new policies — political reversal is possible.

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Population** | ~8,500 |
| **Developer Difficulty** | 5/5 (Highest) |
| **SB 35 Eligible** | Yes |
| **Housing Element** | 6th Cycle adopted; HCD certification contested — Twin Oaks site controversy |

**Strategic Assessment**: Los Altos Hills is a rural-residential community with 1-acre minimum lot sizes and no commercial zones. **Developer Playbook**: Luxury estate SFR only. Not a viable jurisdiction for any density-oriented strategy. SB 9 constrained by septic infrastructure. Monitor Twin Oaks site — if approved, it may be the only multifamily opportunity in the jurisdiction.

### Kill Criteria by Jurisdiction Type

**Kill immediately** if:

- Discretionary project in a 5/5 city without a state-law override (SB 35, density bonus, HAA)
- Hotel project in Palo Alto (Chapter 8, Case 3 — three hotel projects killed by CEQA/traffic)

- Any project in a 1–2/5 city that passes Appendix G Level 1 Napkin Test
- SB 35 project in any city where the site is properly screened (Chapter 15 GIS, Appendix E)
