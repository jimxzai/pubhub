# Appendix E: Pre-Design Site Constraint Analysis Template

---

## Overview

This template is designed to be completed **before your architect draws a single line**. It systematically surfaces every constraint that can derail a project — title issues, easement conflicts, zoning violations, physical site limitations, and financial misalignment — so that your design brief reflects reality, not assumptions.

The template is based on lessons learned from a Peninsula residential project that experienced a **3-year permit delay, $800K+ in holding costs, and 5 systemic failures** — all of which could have been prevented by completing this analysis before design.

---

## How to Use This Template

Complete each section in order. Sections 1–4 should be completed within the **first 4 hours** of site evaluation. If any section reveals a fatal flaw, stop and evaluate whether the project is viable before investing in design.

Each section includes checkboxes, fill-in fields, and a status determination.

| Status | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **CLEAR** | No constraints found. Proceed to next section. |
| **MITIGATED** | Constraint exists but has a clear, documented path to resolution. Note the mitigation strategy. |
| **FATAL FLAW** | Constraint that cannot be resolved or makes the project economically unviable. STOP. |

**STOP: Do not proceed to design until every section has a status of CLEAR or MITIGATED. Any FATAL FLAW stops the project.**

## Section 5: Financial & Schedule Constraint Check

> **LESSON LEARNED**: On the Peninsula project, a bridge loan with a hard maturity date was used to carry the property through a 3-year entitlement process. Holding costs exceeded $800K. The loan nearly defaulted, requiring $56K in extension payments and threatening bankruptcy. Never structure financing without entitlement timeline contingency.

### 5.1 Acquisition Financing

- [ ] [CRITICAL] Loan type appropriate for entitlement timeline (no hard maturity during discretionary review)
- [ ] Extension options built into loan (minimum 2 extensions)
- [ ] Monthly holding cost calculated
- [ ] Maximum hold period before financial distress calculated

## Cross-Reference: Companion Appendices

This template works in concert with two other appendices to form a complete pre-development analysis framework:

| Sequence | Appendix | Purpose | When |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1st** | **Appendix G** — Pro Forma Financial Decision Framework | Run the Level 1 Napkin Test to confirm the deal pencils before investing in site analysis | Before site analysis (5 minutes) |
| **2nd** | **Appendix E** — Pre-Design Site Constraint Analysis (this document) | Surface every constraint that can derail the project before design begins | Before design (4–8 hours) |
| **3rd** | **Appendix F** — Frontier Builder Case Studies & AI-Agile Strategies | Select construction method and technology strategy based on site constraints | During design (strategic decision) |

> **Workflow**: Start with Appendix G's Napkin Test. If the deal passes, complete this Appendix E analysis. Feed the constraint findings back into Appendix G's Level 2 Development Pro Forma (e.g., geotech costs from Section 4, fee estimates from Section 5). Then use Appendix F's case studies to determine whether modular, panelized, or conventional construction best fits the site constraints identified here.

> **Frontier Builder Connection**: The five case studies in Appendix F (§F.1–F.5) each faced site-specific constraints that would have been identified by this template. Veev's failure (§F.4) was partly driven by underestimating site-specific challenges — their factory-first approach didn't adapt to individual site constraints. TJH's success (§F.1) comes from the opposite approach: adapting pre-designed plans to each lot's specific zoning and setback constraints, which is exactly what Sections 1–2 of this template documents.

## Key Takeaway

> This template exists because **prevention is cheaper than correction**. A thorough pre-design site constraint analysis costs 4-8 hours of staff time. Skipping it — as demonstrated by the Peninsula case study — can cost $800K+ in holding costs, 3 years of delay, and 5 rounds of design rework. Run this template on every site, every time, before authorizing design.
