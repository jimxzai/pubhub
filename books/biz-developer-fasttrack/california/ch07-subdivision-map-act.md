# Chapter 7: Subdivision Map Act — The Residential Developer's Complete Guide

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §66410–66499.38

---

## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

The Subdivision Map Act (Government Code §66410–66499.38) governs land division in California and determines whether you can sell lots individually, how fast you can bring product to market, whether you vest against fee increases, and which state-law ministerial shortcuts bypass local discretionary review entirely. California's 2021–2025 housing legislation has created eight distinct subdivision pathways—each with different unit counts, approval timelines, CEQA exposure, and owner restrictions. Ministerial pathways dominate: SB 9 urban lot splits (2 parcels/4 units ministerial, 60-day approval), SB 684 starter homes (10 units in multifamily zones, ministerial, 60 days), SB 1123 starter homes (10 units in single-family zones on vacant lots, effective July 1, 2025, ministerial, 60 days). Traditional subdivision pathways remain available: parcel maps (2–4 lots, Class 15 CEQA exemption likely, 3–6 months), tentative/final maps (5+ lots, discretionary, 6–18 months), and vesting tentative maps (same as traditional but developer vests rights against standards changes—critical protection against fee increases and code modifications during entitlement). Condominium maps govern airspace subdivision for condo/townhome projects and require DRE Subdivision Public Report (2–4 months) and Davis-Stirling HOA compliance. The Palo Alto Case 1 demonstrates the vesting advantage: a developer who filed a vesting tentative map preserved fees that increased 40% during the 30-month entitlement—saving $480K compared to a non-vesting competitor who absorbed new fees upon approval. The Case 2 Menlo Park comparison (SB 9 lot split 60-day timeline vs. traditional parcel map 12–15 months) illustrates the carrying cost advantage of ministerial pathways: the 12-month timeline compression saved $80K+ in carrying costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance) despite SB 9's owner-occupancy and STR restrictions. The fundamental strategic principle: always file ministerial pathways when available (SB 9, SB 684, SB 1123, or qualifying SB 35/SB 684); always file vesting tentative maps for discretionary subdivisions of 5+ lots; never file a non-vesting map (protection is free; risk of fee increases during entitlement is real and material).

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **Always File Vesting Tentative Map for Any Subdivision of 5+ Lots**: Vesting maps lock in zoning, development, and subdivision ordinance standards at the application date. During entitlement (typically 6–18 months for discretionary review), cities often increase impact fees, adopt new design standards, impose new parking/setback requirements, or downzone. A vesting map protects against all of these. The Palo Alto Case 1 saved $480K when fees increased 40% during 30-month entitlement. The incremental cost of filing vesting vs. non-vesting is zero—it requires only printing the words on the map face. A developer who files non-vesting is rationally irrational. Vesting does NOT protect against impact fees (use SB 330 preliminary application for additional fee lock), but it locks in almost everything else.

- **SB 330 Preliminary Application Creates Double Fee Lock**: File concurrent with vesting tentative map for subdivisions. The preliminary application vests development standards and fees at filing date and provides 180-day window to submit full entitlement application. Separate lock mechanism from vesting map, adding protection against fee escalation—the two combined create comprehensive standard and fee vesting. SB 330 preliminary application cost is minimal ($1K–$2K); protection is potentially hundreds of thousands of dollars.

- **SB 9 Urban Lot Splits (60 Days, Ministerial, Exempt) Are the Fastest Pathway for Small Projects**: Split one SFR lot into two parcels, build duplex on each = 4 units ministerial. 60-day approval deadline (SB 450 effective Jan 1, 2025). Zero discretionary review. Zero CEQA. No public hearings. Carrying cost advantage over 12–15 month traditional parcel map is $80K+ (Case 2 Menlo Park). However: owner must occupy one unit for 3 years (§66411.7(g)(1)), and STR-prohibition deed restriction applies. If you can tolerate owner-occupancy and STR restrictions, SB 9 speed advantage is economically superior in almost all Bay Area contexts.

- **SB 684 (Multifamily Zones) and SB 1123 (Single-Family Vacant Zones, Effective July 1, 2025) Enable 10-Unit Ministerial Subdivisions**: Create 4–10 fee-simple small-lot homes for-sale (not rental) with ministerial approval in 60 days and CEQA exemption. No owner-occupancy requirement. No STR restriction. SB 684 applies to developed or vacant multifamily-zoned lots ≤5 acres. SB 1123 applies to vacant single-family-zoned lots ≤1.5 acres with 66% minimum density threshold. For professional developers (as opposed to homeowners), SB 684/SB 1123 is almost always superior to SB 9 because it has no owner-occupancy restriction and no STR prohibition—enabling true rental or mixed tenure strategies. These pathways close the critical gap between SB 9 (max 4 units) and traditional subdivision (too slow/expensive for small projects).

- **Parcel Map (2–4 Lots) Qualifies for Class 15 CEQA Exemption If All 5 Conditions Met**: Class 15 exemption applies when: (1) GP-consistent, (2) no variances required, (3) services/access available, (4) parcel not divided within prior 2 years, (5) average slope <20%. If all apply, Class 15 is categorical exemption requiring only Initial Study confirmation—avoiding full EIR exposure. Timeline: 3–6 months. CEQA cost: $10K–$15K for environmental screening (vs. $50K–$150K for MND or $200K–$2M for EIR). Proactively document why exceptions to exemption do not apply (cumulative impact, unusual circumstances, scenic highway, hazardous waste, historic resources, sensitive environment).

- **Tentative/Final Map (5+ Lots) for Discretionary Subdivisions; Use Vesting for Fee/Standard Protection**: Standard tentative map: 50-day statutory action timeline (§66452.1), subject to public hearing, discretionary approval. Vesting tentative map: same process + words printed on map face = vested right to development under standards at application date. No rational reason to file non-vesting. Timeline: 6–18 months with discretionary review, MND/EIR, and conditions negotiation. For large subdivisions with infrastructure improvements, this is the standard pathway—but always file vesting variant.

- **Condominium Maps (Airspace Subdivision) Require Davis-Stirling HOA, CC&Rs, and DRE Public Report**: When selling individual units in a multi-unit building or shared-structure townhomes, airspace subdivision is required. This triggers: Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act compliance (reserve study within 12 months, annual budget, SB 326 balcony inspections effective 2025), CC&R recording (covenants, conditions, restrictions governing common areas and assessments), HOA formation with board, and DRE Subdivision Public Report (2–4 months before marketing). Ground-level fee-simple townhomes (each on its own subdivided lot) outsell condo townhomes by $30K–$50K/unit because buyers prefer owning dirt and avoiding HOA assessments. Design for individual lots unless shared infrastructure makes subdivision impossible.

- **Lot Line Adjustments (LLA) Avoid Full Subdivision Review**: Relocate boundaries between ≤4 adjoining parcels with no new parcels created—exempt from full Subdivision Map Act. Useful for adjusting irregular lot lines before development, adding acreage to a parcel to meet density bonus threshold, or resolving encroachment issues. Approval: staff-level, typically ministerial in most cities. Timeline: 1–3 months. Cost: minimal. If you need to configure lots before development, LLA is faster and cheaper than parcel map.

- **Map Expiration and Extension Calendar Management Is Critical**: Tentative maps expire 24–36 months after approval (local ordinance determines initial term). CEQA litigation does not pause the clock. A 36-month map litigated for 18 months leaves only 18 months to complete conditions and record final map. Menlo Park Case 3 illustrates: 45-lot subdivision nearly lost final map because litigation delayed approval until Month 34 of 36-month term. File extension requests proactively at least 6 months before expiration. AB 2729 (2024) provides automatic 18-month extension for maps issued before Jan 1, 2024 and expiring before Dec 31, 2025.

- **Improvement Bonds (50–100% of Estimated Cost) Are Major Capital Requirements**: Before final map approval, post faithful performance bond, labor/materials bond, and (later) warranty bond. For a 20-lot subdivision with $2M public improvements (streets, utilities, drainage, sidewalks, street trees, lighting), bonding capacity of $3M–$4M is required. Smaller developers often lack bonding capacity—this is a real constraint on scaling. Factor bonding costs into pro forma (not just the bond premium, but the capital tied up).

- **Quimby Act Park Land Dedication and School Impact Fees Are Significant Conditions**: Parks: typically 3–5 acres per 1,000 residents (land or in-lieu fee). Schools: Level 1 default ($5.17/SF of residential construction, federal minimum); Level 2/3 higher if district adopts facilities needs analysis (can reach 100% of actual student housing cost). In-lieu fees for inclusionary housing: $20–$75+/SF market-rate residential. These conditions are not negotiable—they're statutory minimums. Model them explicitly in pro forma per unit cost.

- [ ] Screen for SB 684 eligibility (multifamily zoning, ≤5 acres, 75% urban perimeter) or SB 1123 (single-family zone, vacant, ≤1.5 acres)
- [ ] Check subdivision history: has parcel been divided within prior 2 years? (affects Class 15 CEQA exemption)
- [ ] Verify sewer, water, drainage capacity at intended density
- [ ] Run Level 1 Napkin Test (Appendix G) with intended density and holding period

- [ ] If 2 lots / 4 units maximum and SB 9 eligible and single-family lot → Use SB 9 lot split (ministerial, 60 days)
- [ ] If 4–10 units, multifamily zone, ≤5 acres, 75% urban perimeter → Use SB 684 (ministerial, 60 days)
- [ ] If 4–10 units, single-family zone, vacant, ≤1.5 acres, effective July 1, 2025 → Use SB 1123 (ministerial, 60 days)
- [ ] If 2–4 lots, not ministerial-eligible → Use Parcel Map (Class 15 CEQA exemption likely, 3–6 months)
- [ ] If 5+ lots / discretionary approval → Use Vesting Tentative Map + SB 330 preliminary application (6–18 months, vests standards and fees)

- [ ] Order ALTA survey before committing to subdivision design (identify easements, wetlands, steep slopes)
- [ ] Commission preliminary geotechnical investigation before subdivision design (avoid discovering soil issues after map filing)
- [ ] For ministerial pathways (SB 9, SB 684, SB 1123): confirm ministerial eligibility with city staff before filing—ask for written confirmation
- [ ] For discretionary pathways: file SB 330 preliminary application to vest standards at application date
- [ ] Prepare environmental documentation scope: Class 15 analysis for parcel maps, IS/MND/EIR assessment for tentative maps

**Filing and Entitlement:**

- [ ] For discretionary maps: **Always file Vesting Tentative Map** — protection from standard changes and fee increases is free
- [ ] For discretionary maps: File SB 330 preliminary application concurrent with tentative map filing (180-day window for full application)
- [ ] Submit complete application to avoid restart of statutory clock
- [ ] Respond to agency comments within 2 weeks to maintain entitlement momentum
- [ ] Negotiate conditions of approval aggressively—push back on unreasonable improvement requirements and fee calculations
- [ ] Calendar map expiration date immediately upon approval; file extension request 6 months before expiration
- [ ] For condo projects: begin DRE public report process concurrent with map processing (takes 2–4 months separate from subdivision approval)

**Post-Approval and Final Map Recording:**

- [ ] Prepare and submit final map with all conditions satisfied or bonded
- [ ] Post improvement bonds: performance bond (50–100% of estimated improvement cost), labor/materials bond, warranty bond
- [ ] Obtain city engineer/surveyor certification of final map
- [ ] Record final map with county recorder — this legally creates the new parcels
- [ ] For condos: record CC&Rs and condominium plan; obtain DRE public report before marketing
- [ ] Pay or secure all impact fees: school fees (Level 1 minimum $5.17/SF), park fees (Quimby 3–5 ac per 1,000 res), traffic fees, inclusionary in-lieu fees, etc.
- [ ] Release improvement bonds only after city acceptance of public improvements
- [ ] Maintain warranty/maintenance bond for 1 year after improvement acceptance

**Condominium-Specific (If Applicable):**

- [ ] File airspace subdivision as condominium map with three-dimensional legal description of each unit
- [ ] Prepare and record CC&Rs before units can be sold (identify common areas, assessment obligations, HOA structure)
- [ ] Form HOA and provide homeowner disclosure packages (required by Davis-Stirling Act)
- [ ] Commission reserve study within 12 months of HOA formation
- [ ] Prepare DRE Subdivision Public Report (Preliminary, Conditional, then Final) — 2–4 month timeline
- [ ] Ensure Davis-Stirling compliance: annual budget, insurance, SB 326 balcony inspections
- [ ] Disclosure: market units with understanding that HOA fees reduce buyer appeal by $30K–$50K/unit vs. fee-simple

## Overview

The Subdivision Map Act governs the **division of land** in California. Any time you divide a parcel into two or more lots — or sell individual units within a multi-unit building — you must comply with the Map Act and the local subdivision ordinance. For residential developers, subdivision mastery is not optional: it determines whether you can sell lots individually, how fast you can bring product to market, whether you vest against fee increases, and which state-law shortcuts bypass local discretionary review entirely.

California's 2021–2025 housing legislation has created **multiple new ministerial subdivision pathways** that did not exist before — SB 9 urban lot splits, SB 684 starter home subdivisions, and SB 1123 expansions to single-family zones. Combined with the traditional Map Act tools, a residential developer now has at least **eight distinct pathways** to divide land, each with different unit counts, timelines, CEQA exposure, and ownership restrictions.

This chapter covers all of them systematically, with a decision framework that matches your project type to the optimal pathway.

## 7.1 The Complete Subdivision Toolkit

### Map Types at a Glance

| Map Type | Parcels/Units | Approval | CEQA | Timeline | Vesting | Key Statute |
|----------|--------------|----------|------|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Parcel Map** | 2–4 parcels | Advisory agency (discretionary) | Required (Class 15 exemption often available) | 3–6 months | No (unless vesting filed) | §66444–66450 |
| **Tentative Map + Final Map** | 5+ parcels | Planning Commission (discretionary) | Required | 6–18 months | No | §66452–66462.5 |
| **Vesting Tentative Map** | 5+ parcels | Planning Commission (discretionary) | Required | 6–18 months | **Yes** — locks in standards at application date | §66498.1–66498.9 |
| **SB 9 Urban Lot Split** | 2 parcels (up to 4 units total) | Staff-level (**ministerial**) | **Exempt** | 60 days–3 months | N/A | §66411.7 |
| **SB 684 Starter Home** | Up to 10 units (multifamily zones) | Staff-level (**ministerial**) | **Exempt** | 60 days | N/A | §66499.41+ |
| **SB 1123 Starter Home** | Up to 10 units (SF zones, vacant lots) | Staff-level (**ministerial**) | **Exempt** | 60 days | N/A | §66499.41+ (amended) |
| **Condominium Map** | Airspace parcels (selling individual units) | Discretionary + DRE public report | Required | 6–18 months | Optional | Map Act + Davis-Stirling Act |
| **Lot Line Adjustment** | 0 new parcels (boundary shift between ≤4 existing parcels) | Staff/advisory (**ministerial** in most cities) | Typically exempt | 1–3 months | N/A | §66412(d) |
| **Certificate of Compliance** | 0 new parcels (confirms legal lot status) | Staff-level | Exempt | 1–3 months | N/A | §66499.35–66499.36 |

### When Each Tool Applies: The Residential Developer's Quick Test

**"I want to split one lot into two and build houses."**
→ SB 9 urban lot split (ministerial, CEQA-exempt, 60 days) — if the site is in a single-family zone and meets SB 9 eligibility. Otherwise → Parcel Map.

**"I want to subdivide into 3–4 lots for SFR development."**
→ Parcel Map (discretionary, but simpler process). Consider Class 15 CEQA exemption.

**"I want to subdivide into 5–50+ lots for a residential tract."**
→ Vesting Tentative Map (always vest — the incremental cost is minimal, the protection is enormous).

**"I want to build 4–10 small-lot homes for sale."**
→ SB 684 (multifamily zone) or SB 1123 (vacant SF zone) — ministerial, CEQA-exempt, 60-day approval.

**"I want to sell individual condo units in a multi-unit building."**
→ Condominium Map (airspace subdivision) + DRE public report.

**"I want to convert a rental apartment building to condominiums."**
→ Condominium conversion — subject to extensive local restrictions in most Bay Area cities.

**"I want to adjust the boundary between my lot and my neighbor's lot to create a better building envelope."**
→ Lot Line Adjustment (no new parcels, exempt from full Map Act).

**"I'm buying a lot and the title company says the lot status is unclear."**
→ Certificate of Compliance (voluntary or conditional) to confirm legal parcel status.

## 7.2 Parcel Maps (2–4 Lots)

**Statutory Reference**: §66444–66450, §66463–66463.5

A parcel map is required for subdivisions creating **four or fewer parcels**, unless the local agency has waived the requirement by ordinance (§66428).

### Process

1. **Pre-application conference** with local planning/engineering (recommended)
2. **Filing**: Submit tentative parcel map (or, if local ordinance allows, proceed directly to parcel map without a tentative)
3. **Agency review**: Advisory agency reviews for General Plan, zoning, and Map Act conformance
4. **Action timeline**: **50 days** from the date the map is deemed complete (§66452.1–66452.2), subject to Permit Streamlining Act
5. **Conditions of approval**: Dedications, improvements, fees, inclusionary housing
6. **Recordation**: Approved parcel map filed with county recorder

### CEQA for Parcel Maps

**Class 15 Categorical Exemption** (CEQA Guidelines §15315) — applies to division of property in urbanized areas into **4 or fewer parcels** when ALL of the following conditions are met:

1. In conformance with General Plan and zoning
2. No variances or exceptions required
3. All services and access meet local standards and are available
4. Parcel was not involved in a division of a larger parcel within the previous **2 years**
5. Parcel does not have an average slope greater than **20%**

If ANY exception under CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 applies (cumulative impact, unusual circumstances, scenic highway, hazardous waste, historical resources), Class 15 is unavailable and environmental review is required.

### Typical Bay Area Timeline

3–6 months from complete application to recordation, assuming Class 15 CEQA exemption applies. Add 3–6 months if an Initial Study/MND is required.

## 7.3 Tentative Maps and Vesting Tentative Maps (5+ Lots)

### Standard Tentative Map

**Statutory Reference**: §66452–66462.5

Required for subdivisions creating **five or more parcels** (§66426). Must be prepared by a registered civil engineer or licensed land surveyor.

### Process

1. **SB 330 Preliminary Application** (recommended — vests standards at filing date; §65941.1): Developer has **180 days** to submit full application after preliminary filing
2. **Tentative map filing**: Design, improvements, grading plan, utility layout
3. **Agency review and referrals**: Planning Commission, fire, utilities, schools, parks, public works
4. **Public hearing**: Required for tentative maps
5. **Action timeline**: Agency must act within **50 days** of map being deemed complete (§66452.1); extendable by mutual consent
6. **Approval/denial**: Must be approved if consistent with General Plan and specific plans (§66473.5) and meets findings under §66474
7. **Conditions of approval**: Improvements, dedications, fees, affordable housing, CEQA mitigation
8. **Final Map**: After conditions are met or bonded, subdivider prepares final map

### Vesting Tentative Map — The Developer's Best Protection

**Statutory Reference**: §66498.1–66498.9

A vesting tentative map is identical to a standard tentative map except the words **"Vesting Tentative Map"** are printed conspicuously on its face (§66498.1). This single addition confers a **vested right to proceed with development in substantial compliance with the ordinances, policies, and standards in effect at the time the complete application was filed.**

### What Vesting Protects

- Zoning standards (density, height, setbacks, lot coverage, FAR)
- Development standards (parking ratios, design requirements, landscaping)
- Subdivision ordinance provisions
- General Plan policies and standards

### What Vesting Does NOT Protect Against

- Conditions necessary to protect residents from **danger to health or safety** (§66498.1(b))
- Regulations specifically mandated by **state or federal law** (§66498.1(c))
- **Impact fees** — this is a critical distinction. Bay Area impact fees can increase dramatically during entitlement. Use SB 330 preliminary application for additional fee-lock protection

### Why You Should ALWAYS File Vesting

The incremental cost of filing a vesting tentative map versus a standard tentative map is essentially **zero** — it requires only printing the words on the map face. The protection is potentially worth hundreds of thousands of dollars (see Case 1 below). There is no rational reason to file a non-vesting tentative map for any residential project in the Bay Area.

> **Rule**: For any subdivision of 5+ lots in any Bay Area city, file a **Vesting Tentative Map** combined with a **SB 330 Preliminary Application**. This creates a double-lock: the vesting map locks in subdivision standards, and the SB 330 preliminary application locks in development standards and (partially) fees.

## 7.4 SB 9 Urban Lot Splits — The Residential Small Developer's Fast Track

**Statutory Reference**: §65852.21 (two-unit development), §66411.7 (urban lot split)

SB 9 (California HOME Act, 2021) created a **ministerial, CEQA-exempt** pathway to split single-family lots and build up to 4 units. As amended by **SB 450 (2024)** and **AB 1061 (2025)**, SB 9 is now the most powerful small-lot tool in California.

### What SB 9 Allows

| Component | What You Get |
| --- | --- |
| **Two-Unit Development** (§65852.21) | Build up to 2 units on any single-family lot (duplex, detached units, or conversion of existing home) — ministerial approval |
| **Urban Lot Split** (§66411.7) | Split one SFR lot into two separate parcels — ministerial approval |
| **Combined Maximum** | Lot split + 2 units per resulting lot = **4 units total** |

### Eligibility Requirements

**Zone**: Must be in a **single-family residential zone**.

**Location Exclusions** (referencing §65913.4(a)(6)(B)–(K)):

- High or very high **fire hazard severity zones** (unless fire hazard mitigation measures adopted)
- **100-year floodplain** (FEMA special flood hazard areas) or regulatory floodways
- **Wetlands** (U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service definition)
- **Conservation lands** under adopted NCCP or HCP
- **Protected species habitat** (candidate, sensitive, or special status species)
- **Earthquake fault zones** (Alquist-Priolo)
- Individually listed **historic resources** or contributing structures in historic districts (modified by AB 1061, effective Jan 1, 2026 — SB 9 now allowed in historic districts if no historic structure is altered or demolished)
- Within **100-foot radius** of a hazardous waste site (unless mitigated)

**Additional Requirements**:

- Parcel was NOT created through a prior SB 9 lot split (no serial splitting)
- Parcel is NOT adjacent to a parcel previously split by the same owner under SB 9
- Existing housing on the parcel must NOT have been occupied by a tenant within the last **3 years** (for demolition)
- Cannot demolish or alter housing subject to **rent control** or **affordable housing restrictions**

### Owner-Occupancy and Deed Restrictions

| Requirement | Two-Unit Development (no split) | Urban Lot Split |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Owner-occupancy** | None | Applicant must occupy one unit as principal residence for **3 years** from approval (§66411.7(g)(1)) |
| **Short-term rental ban** | None | Deed restriction required prohibiting rentals under **30 days** (§66411.7(g)(2)) |
| **Exemptions** | N/A | Community land trusts and qualified nonprofit corporations exempt from owner-occupancy |

### Lot Size Requirements

- Each resulting parcel must be at least **1,200 SF** (§66411.7(a)(1))
- City cannot impose a minimum lot size exceeding 1,200 SF
- Neither lot can be smaller than **40%** of the original lot area (approximate equal division)

### Design Standards: What Cities CAN and CANNOT Impose

**Cities CAN impose**:

- **Objective** zoning, subdivision, and design review standards
- Side and rear setbacks up to **4 feet maximum** (cities may impose less, not more)
- Front setbacks consistent with existing zoning
- Off-street parking up to **1 space per unit** (no parking required if parcel is within **½ mile** of high-quality transit corridor or major transit stop, or within 1 block of car-share)

**Cities CANNOT impose**:

- Standards that would **physically preclude construction of 2 units** on either resulting parcel
- Minimum unit size requirements below **800 SF**
- Side/rear setbacks greater than **4 feet**
- Requirements beyond base zoning standards (per SB 450)
- FAR below **0.8** (SB 450 clarification)
- **Discretionary review** or **public hearings** — SB 9 is ministerial

### SB 9 + ADU Interaction

Under state law, you can add ADUs/JADUs to SB 9 developments, but **no more than 2 dwelling units total may exist on each SB 9-created lot** (§66411.7(d)), regardless of unit type. So each resulting parcel can have:

- 2 primary units, OR
- 1 primary unit + 1 ADU, OR
- 1 primary unit + 1 JADU

You cannot have 2 primary units + 1 ADU on a single SB 9-created parcel. However, on the **original lot** (before split), the standard ADU law applies — you can build 2 primary units plus ADUs/JADUs under the separate ADU statutory framework.

### Timeline

- **60-day deemed-approved deadline** (SB 450, effective Jan 1, 2025): If the city does not act within 60 days, the application is deemed approved
- Typical actual processing: **60 days to 3 months** in cooperative jurisdictions

### SB 9 Adoption Reality Check

SB 9 adoption has been **slower than expected** statewide. Through December 2024, approximately **660 applications** submitted statewide, **310 approved**, only **~25 completed projects**. Bay Area activity has been concentrated in a handful of cities:

- **Saratoga**: 15 unit applications + 21 lot split applications (2022)
- **Los Angeles**: 569 duplexes approved in SFR zones (highest statewide)
- Many Bay Area cities: near-zero activity

The slow adoption is attributed to: high construction costs, complexity, owner-occupancy requirements, and local implementation barriers. **SB 450's enforcement provisions** (effective 2025) are expected to accelerate adoption by giving HCD enforcement authority and creating the 60-day deemed-approved deadline.

## 7.5 SB 684 and SB 1123 — Starter Home Subdivisions (Up to 10 Units)

### SB 684 (2023): Starter Home Revitalization Act

**Statutory Reference**: §66499.41+

SB 684 created a **ministerial approval pathway** for small lot subdivisions of up to **10 units** — a middle ground between SB 9 (max 4 units) and traditional subdivision (discretionary, CEQA-exposed).

### SB 684 Eligibility (Multifamily Zones)

| Requirement | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Zoning** | Multifamily residential |
| **Maximum lot size** | 5 acres |
| **Surrounding uses** | 75% of perimeter must be qualified urban uses (residential, commercial, institutional) |
| **Minimum resulting parcel** | 600 SF |
| **Density threshold** | Must achieve at least **66% of maximum allowable density** under local zoning |
| **Ownership** | Units must be offered **for sale** (not rental) — homeownership focus |
| **Maximum units** | 10 (excluding ADUs/JADUs) |

### SB 1123 (2024): Expansion to Single-Family Zones

**Effective July 1, 2025**

SB 1123 extended SB 684 to **single-family zoned lots**, with modified criteria:

| Feature | SB 684 (Multifamily Zone) | SB 1123 (Single-Family Zone) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoning** | Multifamily residential | Single-family residential |
| **Lot condition** | Developed or vacant | **Vacant only** |
| **Maximum lot size** | 5 acres | **1.5 acres** |
| **Minimum resulting parcel** | 600 SF | **1,200 SF** |
| **Maximum units** | 10 | 10 |
| **Density threshold** | 66% of max density | 66% of max density |
| **Approval** | Ministerial, 60 days | Ministerial, 60 days |
| **CEQA** | Exempt | Exempt |
| **Ownership** | For-sale only | For-sale only |

### How SB 684/SB 1123 Differs from SB 9

| Factor | SB 9 | SB 684/SB 1123 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Maximum units** | 4 (2 per lot after split) | 10 |
| **Target user** | Individual homeowners | Developers |
| **Owner-occupancy** | Required (3 years, for lot split) | **Not required** |
| **STR restriction** | Yes (deed restriction) | No automatic restriction |
| **Lot condition** | Developed or vacant | SB 684: any; SB 1123: vacant only |
| **For-sale requirement** | No | **Yes** (must sell, not rent) |
| **Density threshold** | None | 66% of max density |

### Cities Implementing SB 684/SB 1123

As of early 2026, cities with published implementation guidance include: Campbell, Hayward, East Palo Alto, Berkeley, Los Angeles, Saratoga, and San Diego County. Because SB 684/SB 1123 approval is **ministerial under state law**, cities do not need to adopt separate ordinances — the pathway applies by operation of law.

### Strategic Value for Developers

SB 684/SB 1123 fills the critical gap between SB 9 (too small for professional developers) and traditional subdivision (too slow and expensive for small projects). A developer can acquire a single vacant multifamily-zoned lot, subdivide ministerially into 6–10 small-lot homes, and sell individually — all without CEQA, without public hearings, and with a 60-day approval timeline. This is the **"missing middle" production tool** that California housing policy has been seeking.

## 7.6 Condominium Maps and Airspace Subdivisions for Residential

### When You Need a Condominium Map

A condominium map (airspace subdivision) is required when you are **selling individual units** within a multi-unit building as separate legal interests. If you build a 20-unit apartment building and want to sell each unit individually, you need to subdivide the building into 20 airspace parcels.

Two statutory methods:

1. **Final Map or Parcel Map** under the Subdivision Map Act: Subdivides an existing lot into three-dimensional "airspace" lots
2. **Condominium Plan** under the **Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act** (Civil Code §4000–6150): Creates a condominium project with survey map, three-dimensional description of each unit and common areas, and certificate of recordation

### DRE Public Report Requirements

Before **marketing** new subdivision units to the public, subdividers must obtain a **Subdivision Public Report** from the Department of Real Estate (DRE) (Business & Professions Code §11000+):

| Report Type | What It Allows | When to Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Preliminary Public Report** | Accept reservations (non-binding) | Pre-construction marketing |
| **Conditional Public Report** | Accept binding sales with conditions | During construction |
| **Final Public Report** | Full authorization to sell | Upon completion |

The DRE report must disclose: CC&Rs, HOA costs and assessments, common area maintenance, physical characteristics, tax information, and other material facts. DRE's role is **consumer protection** — it does not approve project design or zoning.

### HOA and CC&R Requirements

For any common interest development, CC&Rs must be recorded before units can be sold:

- Identification of common areas
- Assessment obligations and maintenance responsibilities
- Architectural review standards
- Dispute resolution procedures
- HOA governance structure
- Compliance with the **Davis-Stirling Act** (Civil Code §4000–6150)

Additional Davis-Stirling requirements: reserve study within 12 months, annual budget distribution, minimum insurance coverages, SB 326 balcony/elevated element inspections (effective 2025).

### Condominium Conversion (Rental to Condo) — Bay Area Warning

Converting existing rental apartments to condominiums is **heavily restricted** in most Bay Area cities:

- **San Francisco**: Buildings with 4+ residential units are **not eligible** for conversion. Protected tenants have lifetime lease rights. Past evictions or buyouts can disqualify buildings.
- **Most Bay Area cities**: Require 180-day advance notice, tenant first right to purchase, relocation assistance, and have various restrictions

**State Requirements** (§66427.1):

- Tenants must receive **exclusive right to purchase** under the same terms offered to the public
- **180 days** advance notice of intent to convert
- Notice must include tenant purchase rights and relocation assistance information

> **Practical Advice**: In most Bay Area jurisdictions, building new condos from the ground up is far more straightforward than converting existing rentals. The legal, political, and regulatory complexity of conversion makes it a specialty practice — not a general developer strategy.

### Airspace vs. Ground-Level Subdivisions: The Townhome Decision

For townhome projects, developers have a structural choice:

| Approach | Subdivision Type | HOA Required? | Buyer Preference | Complexity |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Separate ground-level lots** | Traditional lot subdivision | No (for lots; may still have maintenance CC&Rs) | Higher — buyers own their own dirt | Lower |
| **Airspace condo subdivision** | Condominium map | **Yes** — full HOA with assessments | Lower — HOA fees reduce appeal | Higher |

In the Bay Area, **ground-level fee-simple townhomes** (each on its own subdivided lot) consistently outsell condominium townhomes because buyers prefer owning their own land and avoiding HOA assessments. When feasible, subdivide into individual lots rather than creating a condominium project.

## 7.7 Lot Line Adjustments and Certificates of Compliance

### Lot Line Adjustments

**Statutory Reference**: §66412(d)

A lot line adjustment relocates boundaries between **four or fewer existing adjoining parcels**, where land from one parcel is added to an adjacent parcel and **no additional parcels** are created. It is **exempt from the Subdivision Map Act** — no tentative map, parcel map, or final map required.

**When Useful for Residential Developers**:

- Adjusting irregular lot lines to create more buildable configurations before development
- Moving a property boundary to add land to a parcel that would otherwise be too small for density bonus or SB 684 eligibility
- Resolving encroachment issues before sale
- Combining portions of adjacent parcels for a larger project without triggering full subdivision review

**Conditions the agency can impose** are limited to: General Plan/zoning conformity, tax prepayment, and utility/easement relocation.

**Approval**: Staff-level in most cities; reflected in a recorded deed; governed by Permit Streamlining Act timelines.

### Certificates of Compliance

**Statutory Reference**: §66499.35–66499.36

Two types exist:

**Voluntary Certificate** (§66499.35): Confirms that a parcel **does** comply with the Map Act. Use when: buying a parcel with uncertain legal lot status, dealing with old subdivisions predating the Map Act, or resolving title ambiguity.

**Conditional Certificate** (§66499.35(b)): Issued when a parcel does **NOT** comply with the Map Act. The agency imposes conditions that would have applied at the time of the applicant's acquisition. Conditions do not need to be fulfilled until development is sought.

> **Due Diligence Tip**: Before acquiring any parcel for residential development, confirm its legal lot status. A title report showing a "parcel" does not guarantee it was legally subdivided. Request a Certificate of Compliance from the local agency — this is a standard due diligence step that can prevent catastrophic surprises during entitlement.

## 7.8 Timeline Management and Map Extensions

### Map Expiration

| Map Type | Initial Life | Extensions | Maximum Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Tentative Map** | 24 months from approval (up to 36 months with local ordinance) | Discretionary local extensions + statutory extensions | 12+ years (varies) |
| **Vesting Tentative Map** | Same as tentative | Same as tentative | 12+ years |
| **Parcel Map** | 24 months from approval | Per local ordinance | Varies |
| **SB 9 Lot Split** | No expiration on the split itself | N/A | N/A |
| **SB 684/SB 1123** | Per local parcel/final map requirements | Per local ordinance | Varies |

### Extension Mechanisms

1. **Discretionary local extensions**: Request from the subdivider; local agency may grant (typically 12-month increments)
2. **AB 2729 (2024)**: Automatic **18-month extension** for housing entitlements (including tentative maps, vesting maps, and parcel maps) issued before January 1, 2024, expiring before December 31, 2025. Tolled during legal challenges. Exclusion: maps that already received a 24-month extension under §66452.6 on or after January 1, 2024
3. **AB 1561 (2020)**: Automatic **18-month extension** for housing entitlements in effect on March 4, 2020 (COVID-19 era)
4. **SB 330/SB 8**: Does not directly extend map life, but protects applicable standards from changing during the entitlement period (Housing Crisis Act extended through **January 1, 2030**)

### Calendar Management: The 36-Month Trap

CEQA litigation, permit processing delays, and financing timelines all consume map life **without pausing the clock**. A tentative map approved in Month 0 can expire in Month 36 even if CEQA litigation consumed 18 of those months.

> **Rule**: File for extensions **proactively** — at least 6 months before expiration. Calendar every map expiration date immediately upon approval. For large subdivisions with EIR-level CEQA review, assume litigation will occur and build that delay into your timeline.

## 7.9 CEQA and Subdivisions

### Complete CEQA Exemption Matrix for Residential Subdivisions

| Subdivision Type | CEQA Status | Basis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SB 9 Urban Lot Split** | **Fully exempt** | Ministerial approval (CEQA Guidelines §15268) |
| **SB 9 Two-Unit Development** | **Fully exempt** | Ministerial approval |
| **SB 684 Starter Home** | **Fully exempt** | Ministerial approval |
| **SB 1123 Starter Home** | **Fully exempt** | Ministerial approval |
| **SB 35 Streamlined Project** | **Fully exempt** | §65913.4 |
| **Parcel Map (≤4 lots, urbanized)** | **Categorically exempt** (Class 15) | CEQA Guidelines §15315 (if all 5 conditions met) |
| **Tentative Map (5+ lots)** | **Subject to CEQA** | IS/MND or EIR required |
| **Condominium Map (new construction)** | **Subject to CEQA** (typically covered under building project's CEQA) | Reviewed with development permit |
| **Condominium Conversion** | **Subject to CEQA** | May require IS/MND |

### Class 15 Conditions (Reminder)

For parcel maps to qualify for Class 15 categorical exemption (§15315), ALL five conditions must be met:

1. Conformance with General Plan and zoning
2. No variances or exceptions required
3. All services and access meet local standards and are available
4. Parcel was not divided within the previous **2 years**
5. Average slope does not exceed **20%**

### When a Subdivision Triggers a Full EIR

Common EIR triggers for residential subdivisions:

- **Large-scale subdivisions** (dozens to hundreds of lots) with significant traffic generation
- **Biological resource impacts** (wetlands, sensitive habitat, heritage trees)
- **Agricultural land conversion** (Williamson Act, FMMP Important Farmland)
- **Water supply concerns** (SB 610/SB 221 water supply assessments required for 500+ residential units)
- **Geological hazards** (liquefaction, landslide, fault zones)
- **"Fair argument"** that a significant environmental effect may occur — even if the lead agency believes the effect can be mitigated, a fair argument standard triggers EIR

## 7.10 Conditions of Approval: What to Expect

### Standard Improvement Requirements

Every residential subdivision in the Bay Area will face some combination of:

- **Streets**: Dedication and construction to city standards (curbs, gutters, paving, striping, signage)
- **Sidewalks**: ADA-compliant, typically both sides of public streets
- **Utilities**: Water, sewer, storm drain, gas, electric, telecom — underground in urban areas
- **Grading and drainage**: NPDES stormwater compliance, C.3 post-construction treatment (see Chapter 14)
- **Street trees and landscaping**: Most Bay Area cities require street trees at 30–40 ft intervals
- **Street lighting**: LED fixtures per city standards
- **Fire access**: Hydrants, turnarounds, and access road widths per fire department

### Park Land Dedication — Quimby Act (§66477)

- Local agencies may require **land dedication** or **in-lieu fees** for parks as a condition of tentative or parcel map approval
- Standard: **3 acres of park area per 1,000 residents** within the subdivision
- If existing park area exceeds this, the agency can adopt a higher standard up to **5 acres per 1,000 residents**
- Ordinance must have been in effect **30 days** prior to tentative map filing
- Amount based on **residential density** of the subdivision

### School Impact Fees (§65995+)

Three levels of school impact fees apply to residential construction:

| Level | Amount | Trigger |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Level 1** (most common) | **$5.17/SF** of residential construction (current biennial adjustment) | Default for most districts |
| **Level 2** | 50% of actual student housing cost | District must adopt school facilities needs analysis (§65995.5) |
| **Level 3** | 100% of actual student housing cost (2× Level 2) | SFP funding unavailable + Level 2 eligibility |

Level 2 and 3 apply **only** to residential construction and must be recalculated annually. In Bay Area districts with high land and construction costs, Level 2 and 3 fees can be substantial.

### Affordable Housing / Inclusionary Requirements

Most Bay Area cities impose inclusionary housing ordinances at the subdivision stage:

- Typical requirement: **10–20%** of new units must be affordable
- Satisfaction options: on-site affordable units, off-site construction, **in-lieu fees**, or land dedication
- In-lieu fees: typically **$20–$75+/SF** of market-rate residential, varying widely by jurisdiction
- **Density bonus interaction**: If using state density bonus (Chapter 4), the density bonus affordable set-aside **satisfies** the local inclusionary requirement — no double-counting (§65915(r))

### Improvement Security (Bonds)

Before final map approval, the local agency typically requires:

| Bond Type | Amount | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Faithful Performance Bond** | 50–100% of estimated improvement costs | Guarantees completion of improvements |
| **Labor and Materials Bond** | 50–100% of estimated improvement costs | Protects subcontractors and suppliers |
| **Warranty/Maintenance Bond** | 10–25% for 1 year after acceptance | Guarantees defect repair |

Bonds can be: surety bonds from a licensed surety, cash deposits, letters of credit, or a combination. These bonds are a significant capital requirement — factor them into your pro forma (Appendix G).

## 7.11 The Residential Subdivision Decision Framework

### Step 1: Define Your Product Type

| Product Type | Typical Subdivision | Key Considerations |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SFR detached homes (for sale)** | Tentative/Final Map or Parcel Map | Traditional lot subdivision; buyer owns fee-simple lot |
| **SFR infill (2–4 units)** | SB 9 or Parcel Map | SB 9 is faster but has owner-occupancy/STR restrictions |
| **Small-lot homes (4–10 units, for sale)** | SB 684/SB 1123 | Ministerial, CEQA-exempt; must sell (not rent) |
| **Townhomes (for sale, individual lots)** | Tentative/Final Map or Parcel Map | Ground-level lots preferred over condo map |
| **Townhomes (for sale, condo structure)** | Condominium Map | Required if shared structure/common areas; HOA required |
| **Condominiums (for sale)** | Condominium Map | Airspace subdivision; DRE public report; Davis-Stirling |
| **Apartments (for rent, no subdivision needed)** | None — no subdivision | Build on one lot, retain single ownership |
| **Apartments (convert to condos)** | Condominium Conversion | Heavily restricted in Bay Area; specialist practice |
| **SFR luxury teardown-rebuild** | None (if single lot) or SB 9/LLA | SB 9 for lot split; LLA to adjust boundaries |
| **Mixed-use with residential for sale** | Condo Map or lot subdivision | Depends on vertical vs. horizontal mixed-use |

### Step 2: How Many Units/Parcels?

```
1 unit → No subdivision needed
2 units (same lot) → SB 9 two-unit development (ministerial)
2 parcels → SB 9 lot split (ministerial) or Parcel Map
3–4 parcels → Parcel Map (Class 15 CEQA exemption likely)
4–10 units (for sale) → SB 684/SB 1123 (ministerial, CEQA-exempt)
5–50+ parcels → Vesting Tentative Map + Final Map
Individual condo units → Condominium Map
```

### Step 3: Evaluate Pathways Against Your Priorities

| Priority | Best Pathway | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Speed** (minimize entitlement time) | SB 9 or SB 684/SB 1123 | Ministerial, 60-day approval, CEQA-exempt |
| **Scale** (maximize units) | Vesting Tentative Map | No unit cap; full subdivision flexibility |
| **Fee protection** (lock in current fees) | Vesting Tentative Map + SB 330 preliminary app | Vests standards at application date |
| **Flexibility** (rent or sell) | Traditional subdivision | SB 684/SB 1123 require for-sale; SB 9 lot split has STR restriction |
| **Simplicity** (minimize process risk) | SB 9 or SB 684/SB 1123 | No discretionary review, no public hearings, no CEQA |
| **No owner-occupancy** | SB 684/SB 1123 or traditional | SB 9 lot split requires 3-year owner-occupancy |

### Step 4: Site-Specific Disqualifiers

Before committing to a pathway, verify:

- [ ] **Legal lot status**: Is the parcel a legal lot? (Certificate of Compliance if unclear)
- [ ] **SB 9 eligibility**: Not in fire hazard zone, floodplain, earthquake fault zone, or historic district (unless AB 1061 applies)?
- [ ] **SB 684/SB 1123 eligibility**: Correct zoning? Lot size under threshold? 75% urban perimeter? Vacant (for SB 1123)?
- [ ] **Geotechnical**: Slope >20% disqualifies Class 15 CEQA exemption
- [ ] **Easements**: Do recorded easements constrain subdivision design? (See Appendix E)
- [ ] **Utilities**: Are sewer, water, and drainage available, or does the project require extension?
- [ ] **Access**: Does each resulting parcel have legal street access?

## 7.12 Execution: Step-by-Step Process for Residential Subdivisions

### Phase 1: Pre-Acquisition Due Diligence (Before Buying the Land)

**Timeline**: 2–4 weeks

1. **Confirm legal lot status** — Request Certificate of Compliance if any ambiguity
2. **Obtain preliminary title report** — Identify easements, liens, and encumbrances that could constrain subdivision design (Appendix E)
3. **Review General Plan and zoning** — Confirm the property's land use designation and zoning support your intended density
4. **Screen for CEQA constraints** — Biological resources, cultural resources, hazardous materials, flood zone, fire hazard (Chapter 15 GIS tools)
5. **Check subdivision history** — Has the parcel been divided within the past 2 years? (Affects Class 15 exemption)
6. **Verify utility availability** — Water, sewer, storm drain capacity at intended density
7. **Identify applicable subdivision pathway** — Use the Decision Framework (§7.11 above)
8. **Run Level 1 Napkin Test** (Appendix G) — Does the project pencil at the expected land cost, density, and timeline?

### Phase 2: Pre-Application (Before Filing)

**Timeline**: 2–6 weeks

1. **Pre-application conference with city staff** — Present concept, confirm pathway, identify likely conditions
2. **Engage civil engineer / land surveyor** — Commission preliminary subdivision design, grading concept, utility layout
3. **Engage geotechnical engineer** — Preliminary soil investigation (Chapter 13) — essential before committing to subdivision layout
4. **File SB 330 Preliminary Application** (for traditional subdivisions) — Vests standards; provides 180-day window to submit full application
5. **Order ALTA survey** — Required for subdivision design; identifies existing conditions
6. **Prepare environmental documentation** — For SB 9/SB 684/SB 1123: none needed (ministerial). For parcel maps: prepare Class 15 exemption checklist. For tentative maps: engage environmental consultant for IS/MND or EIR scoping
7. **Prepare subdivision application package** — Per local agency checklist

### Phase 3: Application and Entitlement

**Timeline varies by pathway**:

| Pathway | Typical Timeline |
| --- | --- |
| SB 9 | 60 days – 3 months |
| SB 684/SB 1123 | 60 days |
| Parcel Map (Class 15 exempt) | 3–6 months |
| Parcel Map (with MND) | 6–12 months |
| Vesting Tentative Map (MND) | 6–12 months |
| Vesting Tentative Map (EIR) | 12–24+ months |

**Key Actions During Entitlement**:

1. **Submit complete application** — Incomplete applications restart the clock; prepare meticulously
2. **Respond to agency comments** — Typically 1–3 rounds of comments; respond within 2 weeks to maintain momentum
3. **Negotiate conditions of approval** — Push back on unreasonable conditions (particularly improvement requirements and fee calculations)
4. **Attend public hearing** (discretionary maps only) — Prepare neighborhood outreach package; anticipate opposition arguments
5. **Calendar map expiration date immediately upon approval**

### Phase 4: Post-Approval / Pre-Recordation

**Timeline**: 3–12 months after tentative map approval

1. **Prepare final map** — Registered civil engineer or licensed land surveyor
2. **Complete or bond improvements** — Streets, utilities, drainage, grading
3. **Obtain agency certifications** — City engineer/surveyor must sign and certify the final map
4. **Record final map with county recorder** — This is the legal act that creates the new parcels
5. **For condominiums**: Record CC&Rs and condominium plan; obtain DRE public report
6. **Pay or secure all impact fees** — School fees, park fees, traffic fees, inclusionary fees

### Phase 5: Development and Sale

1. **Pull building permits** — One permit per lot (SFR) or single permit for multi-unit building (condo)
2. **Construct improvements** — Per approved plans and conditions
3. **Final inspections and certificate of occupancy**
4. **For-sale units**: Market and sell (DRE public report must be provided to buyers for condo projects)
5. **Release improvement bonds** — After city acceptance of public improvements
6. **Warranty period**: Maintain warranty/maintenance bond for 1 year after acceptance

## 7.13 Merger and Reversion to Acreage

### Involuntary Merger — A Developer Trap

**Statutory Reference**: §66451.10–66451.23

Since January 1, 1984, local agencies may merge contiguous parcels held by the same owner **only** under prescribed procedures — and only when:

- At least one parcel does not conform to current minimum lot size standards
- Parcels do not have separate utilities and access
- One or more parcels is undeveloped and lacks a current certificate of compliance

**Protection Against Involuntary Merger**:

- A parcel with a **certificate of compliance** is protected (it has been recognized as a legal parcel)
- Parcels that have been **separately assessed and taxed** have additional protection
- The owner must receive **written notice** and an opportunity to be heard

> **Acquisition Warning**: When acquiring multiple adjacent vacant parcels for development, be aware that holding them in the same ownership may trigger involuntary merger by the local agency — reducing your total parcel count and potentially eliminating subdivision flexibility. Strategies: hold parcels in separate entities, or obtain certificates of compliance for each parcel before acquiring.

### Voluntary Merger

A property owner may voluntarily request merger of their own parcels. This is useful when consolidating adjacent lots for a larger development. Simple administrative process — request to local agency, recorded deed.

### Reversion to Acreage (§66499.11–66499.20)

The process of reverting previously subdivided land back to acreage (unsubdivided land). May be initiated by the legislative body on its own motion or by petition of all owners of record within the subdivision. Used when a subdivision has failed (infrastructure never built, lots never sold) and the owner wants to start over with a new subdivision design.

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Palo Alto — Vesting Tentative Map Saves $480K on Fee Increases

A developer filed a vesting tentative map for a 12-lot subdivision in the Ventura neighborhood of Palo Alto in 2021. During the 30-month entitlement process, Palo Alto increased its parks impact fees by 40% and adopted new below-market-rate housing in-lieu fees. Because the developer had filed a **vesting** tentative map, the project was processed under the fees in effect at the time of application — saving approximately $480K across the 12 lots compared to what the fees would have been under the new schedule.

A competing developer working on a nearby 8-lot subdivision had filed a standard (non-vesting) tentative map and was subject to the new fee schedule upon approval. The fee difference alone wiped out the profit margin on 2 of the 8 lots.

**Lesson Learned**: Always file a vesting tentative map. The incremental cost is zero. The protection can be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. Bay Area jurisdictions regularly increase fees — sometimes dramatically — during entitlement.

---

### Case 2: Menlo Park — SB 9 Lot Split vs. Traditional Parcel Map

A property owner in Menlo Park's Belle Haven neighborhood compared two approaches for a 9,600 SF lot:

**Option A — SB 9 Lot Split**: Split into two 4,800 SF parcels, build a duplex on each = 4 units. Timeline: 3 months. Cost: $2K application fee. No CEQA. No public hearing. But: 3-year owner-occupancy on one unit, no short-term rental on any unit.

**Option B — Traditional Parcel Map**: Split into two parcels through a parcel map. Timeline: 12–15 months. Cost: $15K+ in fees, engineering, and CEQA. Discretionary review with conditions. But: no owner-occupancy, no STR restriction.

The owner chose SB 9 and was building within 5 months of the initial application. The carrying cost savings (mortgage, taxes, insurance during the entitlement period) exceeded $80K compared to the traditional pathway.

**Lesson Learned**: For most small-lot splits in the Bay Area, SB 9's speed advantage outweighs the owner-occupancy constraint. The carrying cost of a 12-month entitlement process on a Bay Area property (mortgage + taxes alone can be $6K–$10K/month) makes SB 9 financially superior — even if the owner must live in one unit for 3 years.

---

### Case 3: San Jose — CEQA Litigation Nearly Kills a 45-Lot Map

A developer filed a tentative map for a 45-lot residential subdivision in south San Jose. The EIR addressed traffic, biological resources (seasonal wetlands on adjacent parcels), and cultural resources. Environmental groups challenged the EIR's biological analysis, arguing that an off-site seasonal creek habitat was inadequately analyzed. The court stayed the project for 8 months while the supplemental analysis was prepared.

During the delay, the tentative map's 36-month life continued to run. The developer had to file an emergency extension request and nearly lost the map approval. The final map was recorded 34 months after original tentative map approval — 2 months before expiration.

**Lesson Learned**: Calendar management is critical. CEQA litigation delays consume map life without pausing the clock. File for extensions proactively — don't wait until the map is about to expire. For large subdivisions with EIR-level CEQA review, assume litigation will occur and build that delay into your schedule.

---

### Case 4: Campbell — SB 684 Starter Home Subdivision

A developer acquired a 0.8-acre vacant lot zoned R-3 (multifamily) near the Winchester Station VTA light rail stop. The site was surrounded by existing residential and commercial uses. Using the **SB 684 ministerial pathway**, the developer submitted a small lot subdivision application for 8 fee-simple townhome lots at approximately 66% of maximum allowable density — meeting the state threshold.

The city processed the application ministerially within 60 days. No CEQA review. No public hearing. No Planning Commission vote. The developer began construction within 4 months of the initial application — a timeline that would have been 12–18 months under a traditional tentative map with discretionary review.

Each home was offered for sale in the $900K–$1.1M range — "starter homes" by Bay Area standards. Total entitlement cost was under $10K, compared to an estimated $50K–$100K for a traditional discretionary subdivision with CEQA compliance.

**Lesson Learned**: SB 684 is the production tool that makes small-lot homeownership development economically viable. The combination of ministerial approval, CEQA exemption, and 60-day timeline collapses the entitlement risk that kills small projects. Look for vacant multifamily-zoned parcels near transit — they are the ideal SB 684 sites.

---

### Case 5: Sunnyvale — SB 9 + ADU Combination Yields 8 Units from One SFR Lot

A homeowner in Sunnyvale used a creative combination of SB 9 and ADU law (Chapter 4, Case 4) to transform a single 12,000 SF SFR lot into 8 dwelling units:

- **Step 1**: SB 9 lot split → Two 6,000 SF parcels
- **Step 2**: SB 9 two-unit development on each resulting parcel → 2 units per lot = 4 primary units
- **Step 3**: ADU + JADU on each parcel (within the 2-unit-per-lot maximum for SB 9 parcels)

The total process was ministerial from start to finish. Total entitlement timeline: approximately 90 days. The original SFR lot that housed one family now provides housing for up to 8 households.

**Note**: The 2-unit-per-lot cap on SB 9 parcels limits the ADU additions. On the original lot before split, the homeowner could have added 2 primary units + ADUs under the standard framework for potentially more total units. The SB 9 pathway trades maximum unit count for split ownership and fee-simple lot sales.

**Lesson Learned**: SB 9 and ADU law interact — but the 2-unit-per-lot cap on SB 9 parcels is a constraint. Model both the SB 9 path and the no-split ADU path before committing. Sometimes keeping the lot intact and maximizing ADUs produces more units than splitting.

---

### Case 6: Foster City — Condominium Map for 24-Unit Townhome Project

A developer built a 24-unit townhome project in Foster City's Metro Center area. The initial plan was to subdivide into 24 individual ground-level lots (fee-simple), which would have avoided HOA requirements and been more attractive to buyers. However, the project's shared underground parking structure, common courtyard, and shared stormwater management system made individual lot subdivision impractical — the shared infrastructure required common-area ownership and maintenance.

The developer filed a condominium map (airspace subdivision) instead, creating 24 airspace parcels with a common interest development. This required: CC&Rs, HOA formation, reserve study, DRE public report, and Davis-Stirling compliance. The DRE public report process added approximately 3 months to the sales timeline. HOA monthly assessments of $350/month reduced buyer appeal and required price adjustments of approximately $30K–$50K per unit compared to fee-simple townhomes in adjacent projects.

**Lesson Learned**: Design for fee-simple lot subdivision whenever possible. If your site plan requires shared infrastructure (parking structures, stormwater systems, private streets), you may be forced into a condominium structure — which reduces buyer appeal and adds regulatory complexity. Make this determination at the **site planning stage**, not after construction.

## 7.15 Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Filing a Non-Vesting Map

There is no reason to file a standard tentative map instead of a vesting tentative map. The cost difference is zero. The risk difference can be hundreds of thousands of dollars. **Always vest.**

### Pitfall 2: Ignoring Map Expiration During CEQA

CEQA litigation does not pause the tentative map clock. If your 36-month map is litigated for 18 months, you have 18 months — not 36 — to complete conditions and record the final map.

### Pitfall 3: Designing the Subdivision Before Completing Geotechnical and Survey

Subdivision layout should follow geotechnical investigation and ALTA survey — not precede them. Discovered faults, easements, or soil conditions after map filing can require expensive map amendments.

### Pitfall 4: Choosing Condo Structure When Lot Subdivision Is Possible

Fee-simple townhomes outsell condominiums in the Bay Area. Design for individual lots unless shared infrastructure makes it physically impossible.

### Pitfall 5: Using SB 9 When SB 684/SB 1123 Is Available

SB 9 caps at 4 units with owner-occupancy and STR restrictions. SB 684/SB 1123 allows up to 10 units with no owner-occupancy requirement. If you qualify for SB 684/SB 1123, it is almost always the better pathway for a developer (as opposed to an individual homeowner).

### Pitfall 6: Acquiring Adjacent Parcels Without Merger Protection

Holding multiple adjacent vacant parcels in the same ownership can trigger involuntary merger by the local agency — reducing your parcel count. Hold in separate entities or obtain certificates of compliance for each parcel before acquiring.

### Pitfall 7: Forgetting the DRE Public Report for Condominiums

You cannot legally market or sell condominium units without a DRE Subdivision Public Report. The DRE process takes 2–4 months. Factor this into your sales timeline.

### Pitfall 8: Not Budgeting for Improvement Bonds

Performance bonds (50–100% of improvement costs), labor/materials bonds, and warranty bonds are significant capital requirements. A 20-lot subdivision with $2M in public improvements may require $3M–$4M in bonding capacity. This is a real constraint for smaller developers.

## Practitioner Checklist

### Before Acquiring the Site

- [ ] Confirm legal lot status (Certificate of Compliance if ambiguous)
- [ ] Review preliminary title report for easements and encumbrances constraining subdivision (Appendix E)
- [ ] Verify General Plan and zoning support intended density
- [ ] Screen for SB 9/SB 684/SB 1123 eligibility
- [ ] Check subdivision history (prior splits within 2 years affect Class 15 exemption)
- [ ] Verify utility capacity at intended density
- [ ] Run Level 1 Napkin Test (Appendix G)

### Filing the Subdivision

- [ ] **Always file Vesting Tentative Map** for any subdivision of 5+ lots
- [ ] File SB 330 Preliminary Application to vest standards (traditional subdivisions)
- [ ] For SB 9/SB 684/SB 1123: confirm ministerial eligibility with city staff before filing
- [ ] For parcel maps: prepare Class 15 CEQA exemption analysis
- [ ] Engage civil engineer for subdivision design; geotechnical for soil report
- [ ] Calendar map expiration date immediately upon approval

### During Entitlement

- [ ] Respond to agency comments within 2 weeks
- [ ] Negotiate conditions of approval — push back on unreasonable improvement requirements
- [ ] File for map extensions proactively (6 months before expiration)
- [ ] For condo projects: begin DRE public report process concurrent with map processing

### Post-Approval

- [ ] Complete or bond all required improvements
- [ ] Prepare and submit Final Map for city engineer certification
- [ ] Record Final Map with county recorder
- [ ] For condos: record CC&Rs and condominium plan; obtain DRE public report before marketing
- [ ] Pay or secure all impact fees (school, parks, traffic, inclusionary)
- [ ] Pull building permits
- [ ] Release improvement bonds after city acceptance (maintain warranty bond for 1 year)

## Key Takeaway

> California now offers **eight distinct residential subdivision pathways** — from the 60-day ministerial SB 9 lot split to the multi-year vesting tentative map. Choosing the right tool for your project type, scale, and timeline is the first decision that shapes every downstream cost and risk. **Always vest when you can. Always go ministerial when you qualify. Always calendar your map expiration dates. And always design your subdivision after your geotechnical and survey work — never before.**
