## Overview

California has enacted a suite of laws designed to **accelerate housing production** by limiting local government's ability to deny or delay housing projects. These laws interact in complex ways and collectively represent the most significant shift in California land use law in decades.

> **Critical Distinction**: These housing acceleration laws apply **only to housing projects**. Hotel developers must navigate the full discretionary approval process.

---

## 3.1 SB 35 — Streamlined Ministerial Approval

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65913.4

SB 35 creates a **ministerial (by-right)** approval pathway for qualifying multifamily housing projects in jurisdictions that have not met their RHNA targets. This is one of the most powerful tools in a California housing developer's arsenal.

### Eligibility Requirements

- Multifamily housing (2+ units) on an infill site in an urbanized area
- Zoned for residential or mixed-use with at least **2/3 residential** square footage
- Consistent with **objective** zoning and design standards
- Affordable set-aside:
  - **10% affordable** — if jurisdiction has not met above-moderate RHNA
  - **50% affordable** — if jurisdiction has not met any income category
- **Not located in**: coastal zone, conservation area, prime farmland, floodplain, fire hazard severity zone, hazardous waste site, or earthquake fault zone
- Does not demolish rent-controlled or deed-restricted affordable housing
- Pays **prevailing wages** (10+ units must also use skilled and trained workforce)

### Review Timeline

| Project Size | Review Period | If Jurisdiction Fails to Act |
|-------------|--------------|------------------------------|
| < 150 units | 90 days | **Deemed approved** |
| 150+ units | 180 days | **Deemed approved** |

### Process

1. Applicant submits application with SB 35 eligibility documentation
2. Jurisdiction determines completeness within review period
3. If eligible: **must approve ministerially** — no CEQA, no public hearing, no discretionary denial
4. Only **objective** design review is allowed (not subjective aesthetic judgment)

> **Developer Strategy**: Check HCD's annual SB 35 determination letter for your target jurisdiction. Most Bay Area cities are subject to SB 35. If your project qualifies, this can cut your entitlement timeline from **2–3 years to 6 months**.

## 3.2 SB 9 — California HOME Act

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65852.21 (duplex), §66452.6 (lot split)

SB 9 allows up to **four units** on a single-family zoned parcel through a combination of duplexes and lot splits. This is a **ministerial** approval that bypasses CEQA entirely.

### Two Components

| Component | What It Allows | Key Requirements |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Duplex** (§65852.21) | Two units on any single-family zoned parcel | Ministerial; no CUP, variance, or CEQA; max 800 SF minimum allowed by city |
| **Lot Split** (§66452.6) | Split one SFR parcel into two parcels | Both parcels ≥ 40% of original; ≥ 1,200 SF each; ministerial; no CEQA |
| **Combined** | Duplex on each lot = **4 units total** | Owner-occupancy required for lot split (3 years); deed restriction against STR |

### Limitations and Exclusions

- Does **not** apply in historic districts, prime farmland, flood zones, fire severity zones, or protected habitat
- Cannot demolish rent-controlled, deed-restricted affordable, or recently tenant-occupied housing (within 3 years)
- Prior lot split under SB 9 on same parcel is disqualifying
- Local jurisdictions may impose objective standards but **cannot reduce density below 2 units** per parcel
- **No setback greater than 4 feet** from side and rear lot lines for new construction

## 3.3 SB 330 — Housing Crisis Act of 2019

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65589.5, §65905.5, §65913.10, §65941.1, §66300

SB 330 enacted sweeping protections for housing development. Originally set to expire in 2025, it was **extended through 2030** by SB 423.

### The State-to-City Push-Down: Why SB 330 Exists

SB 330 does not exist in a vacuum — it is the **enforcement arm** of California's top-down housing mandate. Understanding this hierarchy is critical for any developer operating in the state.

**The Mechanism — How Sacramento Forces Cities to Build:**

Since 1969, California law (Government Code §65580–65589.8) has required every city and county to adopt a **Housing Element** — a plan demonstrating how the jurisdiction will accommodate its share of regional housing growth. The process works as a cascading mandate:

```text
State of California (HCD)
    │
    │  Determines total regional housing need
    ▼
Regional Council of Governments (e.g., ABAG for Bay Area)
    │
    │  Allocates units to each city via RHNA methodology
    ▼
Individual Cities (e.g., Palo Alto, Menlo Park, Cupertino)
    │
    │  Must adopt compliant Housing Element + zone for capacity
    ▼
Developers
    │
    │  Build the housing — with state law backing their right to do so
    ▼
Housing Units Built (or not — with consequences)
```

**The 8-Year Cycle**: RHNA allocations are updated every **8 years** (commonly referred to as "cycles"). The current 6th Cycle covers **2023–2031**. Each city must demonstrate it has zoned enough land at sufficient density to accommodate its full RHNA allocation across four income categories: very low, low, moderate, and above moderate.

**The 6th Cycle Shock**: The 6th Cycle RHNA numbers were dramatically higher than previous cycles — in many cases **4x to 10x** the prior allocation. This reflected the state's recognition that decades of local obstruction had created a catastrophic housing shortage. Examples:

| City | 5th Cycle RHNA | 6th Cycle RHNA | Increase |
|------|---------------|----------------|----------|
| **Palo Alto** | 1,988 | **6,086** | **3.1x** |
| **Cupertino** | 1,064 | **4,588** | **4.3x** |
| **Mountain View** | 2,926 | **11,135** | **3.8x** |
| **Los Altos** | 285 | **1,958** | **6.9x** |
| **Saratoga** | 439 | **3,356** | **7.6x** |

**What Happens When Cities Don't Comply:**

1. **Builder's Remedy** (Government Code §65589.5(d)): If a city's Housing Element is not HCD-certified, any housing project with at least 20% affordable units can bypass local zoning entirely. The city **cannot deny it** based on inconsistency with the general plan or zoning — because the city itself is out of compliance. This is the nuclear option.

2. **Loss of Local Control**: Non-compliant cities lose discretionary authority over housing projects, cannot downzone, and face SB 35 streamlining at the most permissive level (50% affordable threshold drops to 10%).

3. **Court Action**: HCD can refer non-compliant jurisdictions to the Attorney General. Courts can impose compliance orders and appoint receivers.

> **Developer Insight**: The Housing Element cycle is the single most important macro-level driver of development opportunity in California. When a new cycle begins with dramatically increased RHNA numbers, it creates a **window of opportunity** — cities must upzone, and developers who move first capture the best sites at pre-upzone economics. SB 330's preliminary application is the tool that lets you lock in those economics.

### Key Provisions

| Provision | Code Section | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Preliminary Application** | §65941.1 | Locks in development standards at time of filing (no project design needed) |
| **Five-Hearing Cap** | §65905.5 | Maximum 5 public hearings for any housing project |
| **No Net Loss of Zoning** | §66300 | Prohibits downzoning or moratoriums that reduce housing capacity |
| **Housing Accountability Act** (strengthened) | §65589.5 | Burden of proof on city to deny housing; must cite specific objective standard |
| **Demolition Replacement** | §65915.5 | Must replace any demolished protected units 1:1 at same affordability |
| **Permit Processing** | §65913.10 | 60-day completeness review; 30-day correction period |

### The Preliminary Application — Strategic Power Tool

The SB 330 preliminary application **locks in all applicable development standards, fees, and exactions** as of the filing date — before you've even designed the project.

**Requirements** (minimal):
- Site address
- Project description
- Number of units / square footage
- Site plan showing project footprint

**Does NOT require**: architectural drawings, engineering plans, or environmental studies.

> **Strategy**: Always file your SB 330 preliminary application **as early as possible**, even before your design is finalized. Fee increases of $5K–$10K per unit between filing and permit issuance are common in Bay Area jurisdictions.

## 3.4 Housing Accountability Act (HAA)

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65589.5

The HAA is the **enforcement backbone** of California's housing production mandate.

### Key Rules

- A jurisdiction **may not disapprove** a housing project that conforms to objective standards unless it makes written findings of a **"specific, adverse impact upon public health or safety"**
- The impact must be identified in writing, not based on general policy objections, and have no feasible mitigation
- Conditions of approval may **not reduce density** by more than the minimum necessary
- **Financial penalties**: $10,000 per unit (minimum) for violations, plus attorney's fees

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Palo Alto — SB 35 Forces Approval of 60-Unit Affordable Project

The **3709 El Camino Real** project in Palo Alto tested SB 35's power. The developer (Vesta Pacific) submitted a 60-unit affordable housing project under SB 35, meeting all eligibility requirements. Despite significant neighborhood opposition regarding height, traffic, and neighborhood character, the city was legally required to approve the project ministerially because Palo Alto had not met its RHNA targets for any income category. The city could only conduct objective design review — subjective concerns about neighborhood compatibility were legally irrelevant.

**Lesson Learned**: SB 35 is not theoretical — it fundamentally changed the power dynamic in jurisdictions like Palo Alto that chronically underperform on housing production. If your project meets all objective standards and the jurisdiction is SB 35-eligible (most Bay Area cities are), opposition at public meetings is legally irrelevant. File under SB 35 and hold the line.

### Case 2: Menlo Park — SB 9 Lot Splits on Large Residential Lots

Menlo Park's western neighborhoods (The Willows, Suburban Park) contain single-family lots ranging from 5,000–10,000+ SF. Several property owners have used SB 9 to split larger lots and build duplexes on each resulting parcel — yielding 4 units where 1 house previously stood. The city initially attempted to impose design standards that effectively limited SB 9 projects to less than 2 units, but HCD intervened and required the city to comply with the state law's minimum density requirements.

**Lesson Learned**: Local jurisdictions will test the boundaries of SB 9 by imposing restrictive "objective" standards. Know that HCD actively monitors local ordinances and has enforcement authority. If a city's SB 9 ordinance effectively prevents you from building 2 units on a qualifying lot, it likely violates state law. Document the issue and consider reporting to HCD.

### Case 3: Palo Alto — The State Push-Down Triggers a High-Rise Development Wave

Palo Alto is the most vivid case study of how California's state-to-city housing mandate actually works on the ground. For decades, Palo Alto was one of the Bay Area's most restrictive cities — adding fewer than 100 units per year while hosting some of the highest-paying employers on the planet. Then the 6th Cycle RHNA hit: **6,086 units** (with a 10% buffer, 6,695 units) that the city must plan for by 2031.

**The cascade in Palo Alto:**

1. **State mandate**: HCD determined the Bay Area needed 441,176 new units for the 2023–2031 cycle
2. **Regional allocation**: ABAG allocated 6,086 of those units to Palo Alto — a **3.1x increase** over the prior cycle's 1,988 units
3. **City scramble**: Palo Alto had to adopt a compliant Housing Element by January 2023. When it initially failed to meet the deadline, the city was briefly exposed to **Builder's Remedy** — developers filed projects that bypassed local zoning entirely
4. **Housing Element adopted**: The city adopted its Housing Element on May 8, 2023, and identified El Camino Real as the primary corridor to absorb the required density
5. **Developer rush**: Armed with SB 330 and Builder's Remedy, developers filed a wave of high-density projects — transforming a historically 2–3 story suburban corridor into a pipeline of 4–7 story residential towers

**The El Camino Real Development Wave (2024–2025):**

**Why This Matters for Developers — The Lesson:**

The Palo Alto story is not unique — it is the **template** for what is happening across every Bay Area city with large RHNA allocations. The state push-down mechanism works as follows:

1. **Massive RHNA number forces the city to zone for density** it never would have accepted voluntarily
2. **Failure to comply triggers Builder's Remedy**, which is even worse for the city (developers bypass zoning entirely)
3. **Cities capitulate** — adopting Housing Elements, creating new zoning districts, and approving projects they would have rejected a decade ago
4. **SB 330 preliminary applications lock in the new economics** before cities can backtrack or raise fees
5. **Developers who move first get the best sites** at current fee schedules on a corridor that is being rezoned in real time

The ~1,580+ unit pipeline in Palo Alto represents more housing than the city approved in the entire **previous decade**. This is the state-to-city push-down in action — Sacramento mandated the housing, ABAG allocated it, and now developers armed with SB 330, SB 35, Builder's Remedy, and the HAA are building it whether local NIMBYs like it or not.

> **Strategy**: Monitor HCD's Housing Element compliance dashboard. When a target city's RHNA allocation jumps dramatically (as Palo Alto's did from 1,988 to 6,086), that signals a coming wave of required upzoning. File your SB 330 preliminary application on the best available sites before the competition arrives. The developer who files first locks in the lowest fees and the most favorable standards.

### Case 4: Bay Area — HAA Enforcement Actions

Multiple Bay Area cities have faced HAA enforcement actions. Woodside was forced to withdraw its attempt to designate the entire town as mountain lion habitat to block SB 9 projects. Cupertino faced HCD referral to the Attorney General for pattern violations of the HAA. These enforcement actions signal that the state is serious about housing production mandates.

**Lesson Learned**: The HAA has teeth. If a city denies your housing project that complies with objective standards, document the denial, file an appeal, and be prepared to invoke the HAA's penalty provisions. The $10,000/unit minimum penalty, plus attorney's fees for prevailing plaintiffs, creates a strong enforcement mechanism.

### Case 5: Cupertino — Vallco Mall and the Limits of SB 35 + BMR

The Vallco Mall saga is the most instructive case study in California housing law — not because everything went smoothly, but because it exposes both the **raw power** and the **financial limits** of SB 35's affordable housing mandate.

**Background**: Vallco Fashion Park opened in 1976 as one of Silicon Valley's largest shopping centers. By the mid-2010s, it was a ghost mall — anchor tenants gone, vast parking lots empty — sitting on **50 acres** of prime land in Cupertino, a city with a 6th Cycle RHNA allocation of **4,588 units** (4.3x its prior cycle). The site was the obvious answer to Cupertino's housing mandate. The city didn't want it.

**The SB 35 Nuclear Option (2018–2020):**

Sand Hill Property Company, the site owner, initially proposed a mixed-use redevelopment. Cupertino residents and council members resisted. In 2018, Sand Hill escalated — filing an application under **SB 35** with a plan for **2,402 housing units, 50% below-market-rate (1,201 BMR units)**. Because Cupertino had not met its RHNA targets, the project qualified for SB 35's ministerial approval: no CEQA, no public hearings, no discretionary denial.

The reaction was fierce. **Friends of Better Cupertino** sued, arguing the project didn't meet SB 35 criteria (hazardous waste site, not two-thirds residential, improper parking methodology). Sand Hill counter-sued the city. In **May 2020**, a Santa Clara County Superior Court judge ruled decisively in favor of the SB 35 application — the project could proceed.

> **The SB 35 lesson**: SB 35 is not a negotiating tactic — it is a **legal override**. Sand Hill used it as a battering ram against one of the Bay Area's most resistant cities, and the courts upheld it. The Terner Center at UC Berkeley found that between 2018 and 2021, **156 projects** used SB 35 statewide, comprising over **18,000 proposed housing units**. Most were 100% affordable developments. Projects approved under SB 35 cut approval timelines from **~2 years to ~4 months** and reduced per-unit costs by up to **25%**.

**The BMR Reality Check (2023–2025):**

Here is where the story gets uncomfortable for housing advocates. SB 35 won the legal battle — but the economics told a different story.

Building **1,201 BMR units** at 50% of a 2,402-unit project in Cupertino requires massive subsidy. Below-market-rate units generate a fraction of market rents, and the gap between construction cost and BMR revenue must be filled by tax credits, public funding, or cross-subsidy from market-rate units. At Cupertino construction costs (~$700K–$1M+ per unit for mid-rise), the math didn't close.

Sand Hill restructured the project as **"The Rise"** — expanding to **2,669 total units** but slashing affordable housing by **60%**: from 890 BMR units to **356 BMR units** in the revised plan. Office space was also cut 25%.

| Metric | Original SB 35 Plan (2018) | The Rise — Revised (2025) | Change |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Total units** | 2,402 | 2,669 | +11% |
| **BMR / affordable** | 1,201 (50%) | 356 (13%) | **-70%** |
| **Market-rate units** | 1,201 | 2,313 | +93% |
| **Office space** | ~2M SF | ~1.5M SF | -25% |
| **Approval pathway** | SB 35 ministerial | SB 35 (disputed) + city cooperation | Hybrid |

**Current Status (2026):**

- **February 2024**: City approved The Rise
- **July 2024**: Council greenlit construction start
- **2025**: Demolition, grading, and below-ground utilities underway
- **2026**: Vertical construction begins — first phase "Town Square West" includes 1,369 units (232 affordable, 744 market-rate rental, 393 for-sale)
- **2028**: First residents expected

The city also **waived millions in development fees** — a further sign that the original adversarial dynamic has been replaced by pragmatic cooperation.

**The Developer Lessons:**

1. **SB 35 is a sledgehammer, not a scalpel**. It breaks political resistance — but the 50% BMR requirement at SB 35's most stringent level can be financially unworkable at scale. Sand Hill used SB 35 to force the city to the table, then restructured the economics once political resistance collapsed.

2. **BMR percentages must survive pro forma reality**. A 50% BMR project at 2,400 units in the Bay Area requires hundreds of millions in subsidy that may not exist. The market spoke: lenders and tax credit allocators backed a plan with 13% affordable over 50% affordable. This is not cynicism — it is the current capital stack reality.

3. **The SB 35 threat has permanent value even if the project pivots**. Cupertino went from actively blocking Vallco to waiving fees and cooperating on approvals. The legal precedent — court ruling that SB 35 overrides local objections — changed the city's calculus permanently. Future projects in Cupertino benefit from this shifted baseline.

4. **Phased construction de-risks mega-projects**. The Rise is being built in phases (Town Square West first), allowing Sand Hill to prove out demand before committing to later phases. This is a textbook approach for 2,600+ unit projects where capital markets require demonstrated absorption.

> **Strategy**: Use SB 35 as leverage, but build your pro forma around a realistic BMR percentage. If your target city is SB 35-eligible, the **threat** of a 50% affordable project that bypasses all local control is often more powerful than actually building one. File the SB 35 application, let the city absorb the legal reality, then negotiate a project that pencils — with the SB 35 approval as your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement).

**9. 因地制宜 (Fit the Market) — No Basement, No Ego, No Wasted Dollar**

Sun Tzu: *"Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground over which it flows; the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe whom he is facing."* The winning product is not the one the developer wants to build — it is the one the **market wants to buy**. Every design decision must answer one question: *Does this accelerate the sale, or slow it down?*

**The "No Basement" Decision — A Case Study in Market Fit:**

In the Midwest and Northeast, basements are standard — they provide additional living space, storm shelter, and mechanical room below grade. A developer from Chicago might instinctively include a basement in every plan. In Cupertino, that instinct would be **catastrophic**.

| Factor | Basement | No Basement (Slab-on-Grade) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Construction time** | +6–10 weeks (excavation, forming, waterproofing, backfill) | Slab poured in 1–2 weeks |
| **Cost** | +$150K–$300K (excavation, structural, waterproofing, egress windows, sump) | Included in standard foundation cost |
| **Cupertino soil conditions** | Bay Area clay soils = expensive excavation, drainage, and waterproofing | Slab-on-grade is standard California residential construction |
| **Seismic considerations** | Below-grade structures add structural complexity in Seismic Zone D | Slab-on-grade is the seismically optimal California foundation type |
| **Buyer demand** | Near zero. Cupertino buyers don't expect or want basements. | 100%. Every comp in the market is slab-on-grade. |
| **Appraisal treatment** | Below-grade SF is valued at 50–70% of above-grade SF in California | All SF is above-grade = fully valued |
| **Resale marketability** | Unfamiliar to California buyers; may raise moisture/mold concerns | Standard product = no buyer hesitation |
| **Time-to-sale impact** | Adds 6–10 weeks to construction = 6–10 more weeks of carry cost | Faster to market = faster capital recycling |

**The math is brutal**: a basement adds $150K–$300K in cost, 6–10 weeks of carry ($12K–$25K in additional interest), and the resulting below-grade square footage appraises at a **30–50% discount** to above-grade SF. You spend more, wait longer, and get less value per square foot. In a market where slab-on-grade is universal, a basement is not a feature — it is a **liability**.

This is not about basements specifically. It is about the principle: **build what the market values, not what you assume it should value.**

**The Complete Market-Fit Matrix — What Cupertino Buyers Actually Pay For:**

| Design Decision | Market-Fit Choice | Wrong Choice | $/SF Impact |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Foundation** | Slab-on-grade (no basement) | Basement | Saves $40–$80/SF in below-grade waste |
| **Stories** | 2-story (maximize lot coverage) | 3-story (requires elevator conversation) or ranch (wastes lot) | 2-story is the Cupertino default; deviations require justification |
| **Bedrooms** | 5 BR + study (fits family + WFH + guests) | 3 BR ("We were going for a minimalist feel") | 5BR is table stakes at $5M+; fewer BR = fewer buyers |
| **Kitchen** | Open to great room, 10-ft island, Thermador/Wolf | Closed kitchen, builder-grade appliances | Open kitchen is #1 buyer demand feature; premium appliances pay for themselves in comp support |
| **Primary bath** | Spa-level: freestanding tub, rain shower, double vanity, heated floor | Standard 5-piece with builder tile | At $5M+, the primary bath is a **selling room** — buyers decide here |
| **Outdoor space** | Covered patio, outdoor kitchen-ready, flat usable yard | All lawn, no hardscape, no indoor-outdoor transition | California living = indoor-outdoor flow; flat yard for kids is non-negotiable |
| **Smart home** | Pre-wired: Lutron lighting, Ring/doorbell, EV charger, structured wiring | "We can add that later" | Tech buyers expect smart infrastructure; retrofit is 3x the cost |
| **ADU / flex space** | ADU-ready or built (multigenerational families; AB 976 + AB 1033 optionality) | No ADU consideration | 30%+ of Cupertino buyers have parents or in-laws who will live with them |
| **Garage** | 2-car minimum, EV-ready, Tesla charger stub | 1-car or tandem | Tech families own 2–3 vehicles including at least 1 EV |
| **Landscaping** | Drought-tolerant front; flat, usable backyard; specimen trees | Builder-minimum bark and shrubs | First impression drives initial buyer interest; backyard drives final decision |

**The Ego Trap:**

The most expensive mistake in development is building the home **you** want instead of the home the **market** wants. Every developer has aesthetic preferences — maybe you love industrial loft style, or minimalist concrete, or craftsman details. In Cupertino, the market has spoken: modern contemporary, warm finishes, open floor plan, family-oriented layout. If your personal taste diverges from this, you have two choices:

1. **Build to market** — and make $1.5M per project
2. **Build to ego** — and sit on an unsold home for 6 months while carrying $15K/month in interest

Mission Development chose option 1, forty times. The market doesn't care about your aesthetic vision. It cares about 5 bedrooms, a spa bathroom, and a flat backyard for the kids.

> **Market-Fit Validation**: Before committing to a design, walk the 5 most recently sold new-construction homes in your target neighborhood. Note the floor plans, finishes, bedroom count, lot use, and features. If your proposed design deviates from these comps, you need a **very good reason** — and "I think it looks better" is not a reason. The comps are the market speaking. Listen.

## 3.5 The Extended Legislative Arsenal — Beyond the Big Three

SB 35, SB 9, and SB 330 are the headline laws, but California has enacted a **deep bench** of supporting legislation that collectively reshapes every phase of development — from site selection through construction. Developers who know only the big three are leaving significant tools on the table.

### Housing Supply & Density

#### SB 423 (2023) — The Permanent SB 35

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65913.4 (as amended) | **Effective**: January 1, 2024 | **Sunset**: January 1, 2036

SB 423 extends and substantially strengthens SB 35's ministerial approval process through **2036**. In jurisdictions not meeting RHNA targets, qualifying multifamily projects receive streamlined ministerial approval — bypassing discretionary review, public hearings, and CEQA. The bill shifts review authority to the **planning director** (not city council or board of supervisors), expands eligibility to **mixed-income and market-rate housing** (SB 35 was primarily affordable), and now applies within **coastal zones** as of January 1, 2025.

**Key expansions beyond SB 35:**

- Planning director makes consistency determination with **60-day timeline** (<150 units) or **90-day** (>150 units)
- Mixed-income and market-rate projects now eligible (not just affordable)
- **Coastal zone** eligibility added January 1, 2025 for sites with certified LUP or LCP
- Modified fire hazard zone restrictions: projects allowed in VHFHSZ if local jurisdiction has adopted fire hazard mitigation measures
- Deeper affordability for Bay Area rental: **20% at <120% AMI** with average at <100% AMI
- Pre-application requirements: tribal consultation (AB 168) and public meeting in certain census tracts
- Prevailing wage required for all workers; apprenticeship standards for projects >50 units

San Francisco became the first city subject to SB 423 in June 2024. The SB 35/423 framework has produced over **40,000 approved homes** statewide since 2018.

> **Cross-reference**: SB 423 also extended SB 330's provisions through 2030 (§3.3 above).

---

#### AB 2011 (2022) — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65912.100–65912.140 (as amended by AB 2243) | **Effective**: July 1, 2023 | **Sunset**: January 1, 2033

AB 2011 creates a ministerial approval pathway for multifamily housing on commercially zoned land with **two tracks**. Analysis estimates it could enable **1.6 to 2.4 million new homes** statewide, including 300,000–400,000 affordable units. Adoption has been slow — only a handful of Bay Area projects in the first 18 months — but interest is accelerating as AB 2243 (effective January 1, 2025) broadened eligibility.

**Two Tracks:**

| Track | Site Eligibility | Affordability | Density |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **100% Affordable** | Any commercially zoned land | All units affordable | No density limit |
| **Mixed-Income** | Commercial corridors with 70–150 ft right-of-way | Rental: 8% VLI + 5% ELI; Ownership: 30% moderate | Statute sets minimums by transit proximity (30–65 ft height) |

**Key Requirements:**

- Ministerial approval, CEQA exempt — processing within **60 days** (<150 units) or **90 days** (>150 units)
- **Prevailing wage** required for all workers (adds 15–25% labor cost — factor into pro forma)
- Cannot demolish existing housing, historic structures, or displace tenants within last 10 years
- No sites within VHFHSZ, 3,200 ft of refinery, or on tribal cultural resources
- Building conversion projects exempt from maximum density (AB 2243)

**AB 2243 (2025) Amendments:**

- Expanded corridor eligibility and width definitions
- Projects allowed within 500 ft of freeway **if air quality standards met**
- Consistency determination timeline requirements changed

> **Developer Strategy**: Identify underperforming commercial properties along El Camino Real, Stevens Creek Boulevard, or similar arterials. AB 2011 lets you convert these to housing without a general plan amendment or rezoning — a process that normally adds 1–2 years and significant political risk. Check AB 2243 updates before any application: corridor definitions changed January 1, 2025.

#### SB 684 / SB 1123 — Small Lot Subdivision Streamlining

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §66499.40–41 (as amended by SB 1123) | **SB 684 Effective**: July 1, 2024 | **SB 1123**: July 1, 2025 | **Sunset**: None (permanent)

SB 684 creates a **ministerial, CEQA-exempt** approval process for subdivisions resulting in **10 or fewer parcels** and housing developments of **10 or fewer units** on multifamily-zoned urban infill lots under **5 acres**. SB 1123 (July 2025) expanded eligibility to include **vacant single-family zoned** properties — dramatically broadening the applicable site inventory.

**Key Provisions:**

- **60-day approval deadline**; deemed approved if no action — no discretionary review, no CEQA, no appeal
- Building permits can issue **before final map recordation** (accelerates construction start)
- Multiple ownership models: fee simple, common interest development, co-op, community land trust
- Subject to local inclusionary housing ordinances

**Prohibited Local Standards** (cities cannot impose):

- Setbacks between units
- Enclosed or covered parking requirements
- FAR below 1.0 (3–7 units) or 1.25 (8–10 units)

Cities actively using SB 684: Hawthorne, Cudahy, Rosemead, Hayward, and many Bay Area jurisdictions.

> **Strategy**: SB 684 is the missing-middle power tool. Combined with CFS prefab construction: ministerial approval in 60 days + 90-day CFS framing = potential **6-month permit-to-occupancy timeline**. SB 1123's expansion to single-family zones (July 2025) opens far more potential sites in suburban Bay Area. For-sale townhomes at attainable price points using SB 684 is a high-velocity product type.

#### SB 4 (2023) — Affordable Housing on Faith & Higher Education Lands

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65913.6 | **Effective**: January 1, 2024 | **Sunset**: January 1, 2036

SB 4 enables **100% affordable housing to be built by-right** on land owned by religious institutions and independent nonprofit colleges/universities. UC Berkeley's Terner Center estimated approximately **171,000 acres** statewide could be eligible — a massive untapped land inventory.

**Key Provisions:**

- By-right, ministerial approval — CEQA exempt, no discretionary review or public hearing
- Applies to land owned by churches, synagogues, mosques, temples, and independent nonprofit higher education institutions
- Must be **100% affordable** housing
- Prevailing wage and labor standards required
- Must meet objective local standards; height and density minimums established by statute

> **Strategy**: Partnership opportunity for developers. Many Bay Area churches and colleges hold large parcels in high-demand residential areas but lack development expertise. A developer who brings construction capability (CFS/prefab for cost efficiency) and entitlement knowledge can partner with institutional landowners — the land comes at below-market basis (often ground-lease or donated), the developer builds, and the institution fulfills its community mission. Explore South Bay church properties as potential development sites.

### Entitlement & Permitting Acceleration

#### AB 2234 (2022) — One-Stop Permitting & Timeline Accountability

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65940.1

AB 2234 requires local agencies to:

- Establish **one-stop permitting centers** (online or in-person)
- **Post fee schedules and processing timelines** online
- Create **accountability mechanisms** for jurisdictions that miss their own posted timelines

> **Leverage**: When a jurisdiction's plan check drags past its own posted timeline, AB 2234 gives you documented grounds to escalate. Useful in jurisdictions like Menlo Park or San Jose where plan check delays add months.

---

#### Permit Streamlining Act (PSA) — Hard Deadlines with Teeth

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65920–65964 | **Effective**: 1977 (continuously updated) | **Sunset**: None (permanent)

The PSA imposes **mandatory deadlines** on local agencies to approve or deny development projects. If the agency misses the deadline, the project is **deemed approved by operation of law**. These are hard statutory clocks that protect developers from indefinite plan check delays.

| Action | Deadline | Consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Completeness determination | **30 days** from filing | Application deemed complete if city misses deadline |
| Agency notice of deficiencies | **30 days** | Must list **all** deficiencies in writing |
| Ministerial projects | **60 days** | **Deemed approved** if city fails to act |
| ND/MND projects | **120 days** from environmental review completion | **Deemed approved** |
| EIR projects | **180 days** from EIR certification | **Deemed approved** |
| Clock restart on resubmittal | **30 days** | Agency gets new completeness review period |

**Enforcement**: Document everything. Note the exact date your application is deemed complete. Track the statutory clock. When plan check drags past the deadline, send formal written notice citing §65950 et seq.

> **Combined with HAA**: Creates two-pronged enforcement — cities can't deny compliant projects (HAA) and can't delay them indefinitely (PSA). AB 2234 requires posted timelines — if the city's own posted timeline exceeds statutory limits, you have additional leverage. Cross-reference Chapter 16 (§16C) for detailed PSA workflow.

---

#### SB 8 (2021) — SB 330 Extended + Fee Lock Codified

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §66300 (amended)

SB 8 extended SB 330's provisions (including the **fee-lock at application date**) and clarified that when you submit a project application, the fees in effect at that date are locked — the city **cannot retroactively increase them** during your entitlement process. This was later further extended through 2030 by SB 423.

> **Practical note**: SB 8 and SB 330's fee-lock interact. File your SB 330 preliminary application to lock fees, then use SB 8's protections to ensure the lock holds through permit issuance. In Bay Area jurisdictions where fee increases of $5K–$10K per unit per year are common, this sequencing can save six or seven figures on a project.

### ADU Law Updates

#### SB 13 (2020) — ADU Impact Fee Elimination + Amnesty

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65852.2 (amended)

The 2020 ADU reform package (AB 68, SB 13, AB 881) eliminated most local barriers to ADU construction, establishing the modern framework. Subsequent amendments have continued expanding ADU rights. Key provisions:

- **No owner-occupancy requirement** (made permanent by AB 976 in 2024)
- Ministerial approval within **60 days**; all reviewing agencies bound to same timeline (AB 2221, 2023)
- **4 ft rear/side setbacks** for detached ADUs; no front setback requirement for conversions
- **No parking required** within 0.5 miles of transit or historic district
- Maximum 1,200 SF detached; 850 SF studio/1BR attached; 1,000 SF 2+BR attached (state minimums)
- Pre-approved ADU plan schemes required by January 2025 (AB 434)
- **18 ft height** allowed near transit or on multifamily properties (SB 897)
- Up to **8 detached ADUs** on multifamily lots (SB 1211)
- **Unpermitted ADU legalization** pathway for ADUs built before January 1, 2020 (AB 2533, 2025) — cities must allow permitting if health/safety codes are met, without penalties or impact fees

SB 13 specifically:

- **Eliminated impact fees** for ADUs under 750 SF
- **Proportional fees** for ADUs 750+ SF (cannot exceed the fee for equivalent-size multifamily unit)
- Created an **amnesty program** for unpermitted ADUs built before January 1, 2018

> **State law preempts local ordinance**: Where local ADU rules are more restrictive than state law, state law controls (AB 434). If a planner cites a local ordinance that contradicts state ADU provisions, cite §65852.2 preemption. Cross-reference Chapter 4 (§4.4–4.5) for comprehensive ADU standards.

---

#### AB 976 (2024) — Owner-Occupancy Removal Made Permanent

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65852.2 (amended)

The 2020 ADU reforms temporarily removed the **owner-occupancy requirement** for ADUs — homeowners could rent out their primary residence and ADU to separate tenants. AB 976 **made this removal permanent** (it was previously set to sunset in 2025). This is critical for the ADU-as-investment-property thesis: you can now build an ADU, move out of the primary dwelling, and rent both units — or sell the property as a turnkey rental duplex.

> **Combined with AB 1033** (Chapter 4, §4.5): AB 1033 allows ADUs to be sold as separate condominiums. AB 976 removes the owner-occupancy tether. Together, these laws make ADUs function as fully independent housing units — buildable by right, rentable without owner-occupancy, and sellable independently.

### Construction & Building Code

#### Title 24 / CALGreen (2025 Code Cycle) — Effective January 1, 2026

**Statutory Reference**: California Code of Regulations, Title 24, Part 6 (Energy) and Part 11 (CALGreen) | **Applies to**: Permit applications filed on/after January 1, 2026 | **Until**: 2028 code cycle

The 2025 Title 24 energy code represents a **major shift toward building electrification**. The code strongly encourages heat pump systems through energy budgets that make gas systems harder to comply with prescriptively. The code is estimated to save **$4.8 billion** in energy costs over its 30-year lifetime.

| Requirement | What Changed | Impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Heat pumps** | Prescriptive baseline is now heat pump for space heating/cooling; gas systems require additional efficiency measures to comply | Gas infrastructure eliminated for most new residential |
| **Heat pump water heater** | HPWH encouraged through prescriptive pathways; ventilation standards for HPWH installations | Mechanical design must accommodate HPWH ventilation |
| **Battery storage (BESS)** | Required for new buildings with PV systems | Adds $8K–$15K per home to electrical scope |
| **EV charging** | Mandated EV-ready infrastructure for all new residential construction | Electrical panel sizing and conduit runs must be designed for EV load |
| **Envelope performance** | Lower U-factors for walls, windows; higher-performance framing required | CFS and wood-frame assemblies may need upgraded insulation values |
| **Embodied carbon** | Reporting required for some project types | ICF and CFS may have advantages over cast-in-place concrete |
| **Gas pool/spa heaters** | **No longer code-compliant** for new construction | All-electric pool/spa heating required |
| **Commercial kitchens** | Electric-ready requirements for future gas-to-electric conversion | Coordinate kitchen specifications with electric appliance packages |

**Wildfire rebuild exception**: Executive Order N-29-25 temporarily suspends solar and BESS requirements for LA wildfire rebuild projects, but all other 2025 code provisions still apply. Design for full compliance anyway — it's a selling point.

> **Cross-reference**: See Appendix A for Title 24 energy compliance in wildfire reconstruction contexts. **AB 130 building code freeze** (§3.6.8): residential building code standards are frozen from October 2025 through June 2031 — no new requirements mid-project.

### Financial & Fees

#### AB 602 / AB 1483 — Impact Fee Transparency & Nexus Requirements

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §66000+ (Mitigation Fee Act); §65940.1 (AB 1483) | **AB 602**: January 1, 2022 | **AB 1483**: January 1, 2020 | **Sunset**: None (permanent)

AB 602 requires local agencies to conduct and publicly disclose **nexus studies** justifying the relationship between impact fees and the actual cost of infrastructure needed to serve new development. AB 1483 requires all fee schedules, timelines, and requirements to be **posted online** with annual reporting.

**Key Provisions:**

- Nexus studies required for all impact fees; must demonstrate actual infrastructure cost relationship
- Fees calculated on a **per-square-foot basis** (not just per-unit) for residential — benefits smaller units
- Jurisdictions must **post fee schedules online** with supporting nexus study and annual reporting (AB 1483)
- Developers can demand **accounting of fee expenditure** and challenge fees exceeding nexus-study levels
- School fees capped by statute (Government Code §65995)
- Quimby Act park dedication limited to subdivision projects

**Constitutional Backing — Nollan-Dolan Tests:**

| Test | Source | Standard |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Nollan v. CCC** (1987) | U.S. Supreme Court | Exactions must have **essential nexus** to legitimate government interest |
| **Dolan v. Tigard** (1994) | U.S. Supreme Court | Exactions must be **roughly proportional** to project's impact |

If a city's impact fees exceed what the nexus study supports, they may violate these constitutional standards. This is your legal basis for challenging excessive fees.

> **Strategy**: Pull nexus studies for every jurisdiction where you develop. Fee burden can be **$30K–$100K+ per unit** in Bay Area jurisdictions. A $50K/unit fee on a 10-unit SB 684 project = $500K impact on feasibility. SB 330 fee-lock strategy: file preliminary application early even if design isn't final — locks today's fees for the entire entitlement period. Build fee sensitivity analysis into every pro forma: model project IRR at current fees, +25%, and +50% to understand exposure.

## 3.6 CEQA — The Developer's Complete Guide to Environmental Review

**Statutory Reference**: Public Resources Code §21000–21189.70.10

The California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) is the **single largest source of delay, cost, and litigation risk** in California development. For decades, it has been weaponized by NIMBYs, competitors, and labor unions to block or extort concessions from housing projects — often with little genuine environmental purpose. The 2025 legislative session produced the most sweeping CEQA reforms since the law's enactment in 1970. Every developer must understand both the traditional CEQA framework and the new exemption landscape.

---

### 3.6.1 Traditional CEQA — What You're Trying to Avoid

Under the traditional CEQA process, any "discretionary" project that may have a "significant effect on the environment" requires environmental review:

```text
Project Application Filed
    │
    ▼
Lead Agency Determines: Exempt or Not?
    │
    ├── YES → Categorical Exemption (Class 1–33) → No further review
    │
    └── NO → Initial Study Required
              │
              ├── No significant impact → Negative Declaration (ND)
              │                            ~$20K–$50K; 30-day public comment
              │
              ├── Impacts mitigable → Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND)
              │                        ~$50K–$200K; 30-day comment; mitigation monitoring
              │
              └── Significant unavoidable impacts → Environmental Impact Report (EIR)
                                                     ~$200K–$2M+; 45-day comment;
                                                     12–36 months; litigation magnet
```

**The Cost of CEQA to a Housing Developer:**

| CEQA Pathway | Typical Cost | Timeline Added | Litigation Risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Categorical Exemption** | $5K–$15K | 1–3 months | Low (but challengeable) |
| **Negative Declaration** | $20K–$50K | 3–6 months | Moderate |
| **Mitigated Neg Dec (MND)** | $50K–$200K | 6–12 months | High |
| **Full EIR** | $200K–$2M+ | 12–36 months | Very High |
| **Ministerial (No CEQA)** | **$0** | **0 months** | **None** |

The bottom row is why every housing law in §3.1–3.5 exists: **ministerial approval = no CEQA**. But not every project qualifies for ministerial pathways. The 2025 reforms dramatically expanded what qualifies for **statutory CEQA exemption** — creating a new middle ground between full CEQA and ministerial bypass.

> **The CEQA Litigation Problem**: Between 2010 and 2022, CEQA lawsuits targeting housing projects averaged **2.8 years** to resolve. Even meritless suits impose delay costs of $500K–$2M on projects that cannot break ground until litigation concludes. The 2025 reforms directly address this by shrinking the litigation surface area.

---

### 3.6.2 The 2025 CEQA Revolution — AB 130 & SB 131

On **June 30, 2025**, Governor Newsom signed **AB 130** and **SB 131** as budget trailer bills — effective immediately. Together, they represent the most significant overhaul of CEQA in over five decades.

#### AB 130 — New Statutory Housing Exemption

**Statutory Reference**: Public Resources Code §21080.66

AB 130 creates a **broad new CEQA exemption** for qualifying infill housing that is dramatically more permissive than the old Class 32 categorical exemption.

**What AB 130 Exempts:**

Any housing development project — single-family, multifamily, mixed-use, transitional, or farmworker housing — that meets these criteria:

| Requirement | Standard | Old Class 32 Comparison |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Site size** | ≤ 20 acres (≤ 5 acres for Builder's Remedy projects) | Was ≤ 5 acres |
| **Location** | Incorporated city or Census-defined urban area | Similar |
| **Site character** | Previously developed or surrounded by urban uses (75% perimeter adjacency) | Similar but stricter |
| **Residential density** | ≥ 15 units/acre (metro) or ≥ 10 units/acre (suburban) — "Mullin density" | Not specified |
| **Use mix** | ≥ 2/3 residential (or ≥ 500 net units at 50% residential) | Similar |
| **General plan/zoning** | Consistent with general plan **OR** zoning (only one required) | Required both |
| **Affordable housing** | **Not required** (local inclusionary may still apply) | Not required |
| **Prevailing wage** | Only for: 100% affordable projects, buildings > 85 feet, or SF projects ≥ 50 units | Not applicable |
| **Unit cap** | **None** | Effectively limited |
| **Transit proximity** | **Not required** | Not required |
| **Environmental studies** | Phase I ESA only — **no traffic, noise, air quality, or water quality studies** | Required all technical studies |

**What AB 130 Does NOT Exempt:**

- Hotels, motels, or transient lodging (applications deemed complete after Jan 1, 2025)
- Projects on protected or hazardous lands (same SB 35 siting criteria — no wetlands, fire zones, earthquake faults, floodplains, or endangered species habitat)
- Demolition of structures listed on federal, state, or local historic registers before the preliminary application filing date

**Mandatory Conditions (Even with Exemption):**

| Condition | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| **Phase I ESA** | Environmental site assessment required before approval |
| **Freeway proximity** | Projects within 500 feet of a freeway: centralized HVAC with MERV 16 filtration; no freeway-facing balconies |
| **Tribal consultation** | 14-day notification to tribes → 60-day response window → 45-day consultation (one 15-day extension available) |
| **Cultural protections** | Binding mitigation conditions if tribal consultation identifies cultural resources |

**Approval Timeline Under AB 130:**

| Step | Deadline |
| --- | --- |
| Completeness determination | 30 days |
| If incomplete, applicant correction period | 90 days |
| Agency re-review after correction | 30 days |
| Project approval/disapproval after CEQA clearance | 90 days |
| **Total approximate timeline** | **104–178 days** |

Compare this to 12–36 months for a full EIR. AB 130 compresses the environmental review timeline by **70–85%**.

> **Developer Strategy**: AB 130 is the new default pathway for any infill housing project that doesn't qualify for ministerial approval under SB 35/423. Run your site through the AB 130 eligibility checklist before defaulting to traditional CEQA. The elimination of traffic, noise, and air quality studies alone saves $50K–$150K in consultant fees and 3–6 months in timeline.

---

#### SB 131 — The "Near-Miss" Safety Net + Rezoning Exemption

**Statutory Reference**: Public Resources Code (multiple sections amended)

SB 131 operates as the **companion bill** to AB 130, filling two critical gaps:

**1. The "Near-Miss" Provision — Limited CEQA Review**

If your housing project qualifies for a CEQA exemption (under Classes 1–5, 12, 15, 20, 27, 30, or 32) **except for a single disqualifying condition**, SB 131 limits the required CEQA review **solely to the environmental impacts that single condition may cause**.

What this means in practice:

```text
Your project qualifies for Class 32 infill exemption
EXCEPT: the site is 5.5 acres (Class 32 requires ≤ 5 acres)

Old law: Full CEQA review required — all impacts analyzed
SB 131:  Review limited ONLY to impacts caused by the extra 0.5 acres
         No alternatives analysis required
         No growth-inducing impacts analysis required
         No cumulative impacts analysis required
```

This eliminates the **cliff effect** where missing one criterion by a small margin triggered a full EIR. The focused review is dramatically cheaper and faster than traditional CEQA.

**2. Housing Element Rezoning Exemption**

Municipalities that upzone to implement their approved Housing Element are now **exempt from CEQA** for the rezoning action itself. This removes a major barrier that previously allowed CEQA litigation to delay the very upzoning that state law requires.

**Excluded from the rezoning exemption:**
- Warehouse distribution centers (≥ 50,000 SF)
- Oil and gas infrastructure
- Natural and protected lands (except farmland)

**3. Administrative Record Reform**

SB 131 **excludes internal agency communications** from the CEQA litigation administrative record — unless they were reviewed by executive or supervisory officials and presented to the final decision-making body. This dramatically reduces the litigation record size and associated discovery costs.

**4. Infill Site Mapping**

The Office of Land Use and Climate Innovation must map all eligible urban infill sites statewide by **2027** and update the mapping biennially. This will create a public database of AB 130-eligible sites — a site-acquisition tool for developers.

> **Strategy**: If your project narrowly misses a categorical exemption, don't assume you need a full EIR. SB 131's near-miss provision may reduce your review to a focused analysis of one issue. Consult your CEQA attorney before defaulting to the expensive pathway.

### 3.6.4 SB 79 — Abundant and Affordable Homes Near Transit Act

**Signed**: October 10, 2025 | **Effective**: July 1, 2026
**Statutory Reference**: Government Code (new sections)

SB 79 is the most aggressive **transit-oriented upzoning** law in California history. It overrides local height and density limits near qualifying transit stops — and it applies whether or not the local jurisdiction wants it.

**Applicable Counties**: San Francisco, San Mateo, Santa Clara, Alameda, Sacramento, Los Angeles, Orange, and San Diego

#### Transit Tier Definitions

| Tier | Transit Type | Frequency Requirement |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Tier 1** | Heavy rail or very high frequency commuter rail | Peak + off-peak service |
| **Tier 2** | Light rail, high-frequency rail, or bus rapid transit | ≤ 15-minute intervals during AM/PM peak commute |

#### Development Standards by Tier and Distance

| Zone | Distance from Stop | Minimum Height | Minimum Density |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Tier 1 — Inner** | ≤ ¼ mile | **75 feet** (~7 stories) | **120 units/acre** |
| **Tier 1 — Outer** | ¼–½ mile | **55 feet** (~5 stories) | **30 units/acre** |
| **Tier 2 — Inner** | ≤ ¼ mile | **55 feet** (~5 stories) | **30 units/acre** |
| **Tier 2 — Outer** | ¼–½ mile | Per local standards (with minimum) | **30 units/acre** |

**For very small cities**: the qualifying radius is reduced to ¼ mile.

#### Key Requirements

| Requirement | Standard |
| --- | --- |
| **Minimum project size** | 5 dwelling units |
| **Average unit size cap** | ≤ 1,750 net habitable SF |
| **Affordability (>10 units)** | Choose one: 7% extremely low income, 10% very low income, or 13% lower income |
| **Prevailing wage** | Not required for projects under 85 feet |
| **Zoning applicability** | Residential, mixed-use, or commercial zoned parcels |

#### TOD Alternative Plan

Local jurisdictions can adopt a **TOD Alternative Plan** (approved by HCD) that customizes SB 79's application — provided the plan still meets minimum overall density increases. Jurisdictions without an approved alternative by July 1, 2026, default to the state standards above.

**Unincorporated areas** are exempt until the 7th RHNA cycle.

#### Fire Zone Overlap

In jurisdictions where SB 79 zones overlap with **Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones**, local leaders have a minimum of **3 years** to adopt plans ensuring fire safety compliance before SB 79 density applies.

> **Developer Strategy — The SB 79 Arbitrage**: SB 79 creates an enormous arbitrage opportunity. Properties within ¼ mile of a Tier 1 station in Santa Clara or San Francisco counties can now support **120 units/acre at 75 feet** regardless of current zoning. If you can acquire a site currently zoned for 2-story commercial at pre-SB 79 pricing, you are buying at the old entitlement value and building at the new entitlement value. The law takes effect July 1, 2026 — the acquisition window is **now**. Cross-reference with SB 330: file your preliminary application to lock in current fees before jurisdictions adjust fee schedules to reflect the new density.

### 3.6.5 What's Coming — 2026 Proposals

Two competing proposals will further reshape CEQA in 2026:

#### BACA — Building an Affordable California Act (November 2026 Ballot)

Backed by the **California Chamber of Commerce**, BACA is a statewide ballot initiative that would be the most comprehensive CEQA overhaul since 1970 if voters approve it.

**"Essential Projects" Covered**: Housing (including senior care, student housing, emergency shelters, farmworker housing, master-planned communities), medical facilities, clean energy, water infrastructure, transportation, public safety, wildfire risk reduction, and broadband.

**Key Provisions:**

| Reform | Current Law | Under BACA |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Application completeness** | Varies by jurisdiction | **30 days** or auto-deemed complete |
| **Determine review level** | No hard deadline | **30 business days** |
| **Complete exemption/addendum** | No hard deadline | **90 business days** |
| **Adopt ND/MND** | No hard deadline | **180 business days** |
| **Certify EIR** | No hard deadline | **365 business days** |
| **Project approval after CEQA** | No hard deadline | **90 calendar days** |
| **Alternatives analysis** | Multiple alternatives required | Only project, one applicant alternative, and no-project |
| **Vested rights** | Subject to change during review | **Locked at application date** |
| **Litigation standard** | "Fair argument" (low bar for challengers) | Must show violation of **"objective, quantifiable, written standards"** |
| **Litigation timeline** | Years | **30 days to file; 270 days for all proceedings including appeals** |
| **Public comment (MND)** | 30 days (extendable) | **20 days** (no extension except by court order) |
| **Public comment (EIR)** | 45 days (extendable) | **45 days** (no extension except by court order) |

**The Litigation Reform Is the Game-Changer**: BACA would replace the "fair argument" standard — which allows virtually anyone to force a full EIR by raising any arguable environmental concern — with a requirement that challengers demonstrate violations of **objective, quantifiable, written standards** existing at the time of application. This eliminates the CEQA shakedown model where unions and competitors file suits based on speculative or subjective environmental claims.

**Environmental Protections Preserved**: All federal and state environmental laws remain in effect (ESA, Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, Coastal Act). AB 52 tribal consultation is preserved and enhanced.

> **Watch This Space**: BACA appears on the **November 2026 ballot**. If passed, it would fundamentally change the risk calculus for every project in California. Developers should model their pipeline under both current CEQA and a BACA-reform scenario.

#### AB 1083 (2026) — The Counter-Proposal

In contrast to BACA's deregulatory approach, **AB 1083** would:

- **Narrow** existing CEQA exemptions
- **Expand** administrative record requirements
- **Increase** protections for environmentally sensitive lands

AB 1083 represents the environmental community's push to ensure the 2025 reforms don't go too far. Developers should monitor this bill as a potential rollback risk.

### 3.6.7 VMT Mitigation Bank — A New Financial Tool

- Predictable construction costs for projects in the pipeline

## 3.7 Pre-Construction Sale Strategies — The TJH Model and Legal Framework

Selling homes **before construction is complete** is one of the most powerful capital-efficiency tools in residential development. It reduces carry costs, de-risks absorption, and — when structured correctly — allows developers to build with buyer deposits rather than pure equity. Thomas James Homes (TJH) has scaled this model to 350+ homes per year. Understanding both the business strategy and the California legal framework is essential.

### The TJH Pre-Construction Sale Model

**Thomas James Homes** (tjh.com) operates a **three-channel** sales model that maximizes revenue velocity across the construction timeline:

| Channel | How It Works | When Buyer Enters | Price vs. Completed Home |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-Construction** | Buyer purchases a permitted homesite with a future home during early-stage construction | Before or at foundation | **Lowest price** — preferred pricing for early commitment |
| **Market Homes (Spec)** | TJH builds on spec, sells during or after construction | Mid-construction to completed | Market price |
| **BUILD by TJH** | Homeowner hires TJH to demolish and rebuild on their own lot | Before demolition | Guaranteed $/SF pricing |

**The Pre-Construction Channel — Key Mechanics:**

1. **TJH acquires the lot** (typically a teardown in an established neighborhood — average home age 80 years)
2. **Permits obtained** using TJH's curated plan library (pre-designed plans cut design time from 4–6 months to 2–4 weeks)
3. **Homesite listed for pre-construction sale** — buyer purchases the property + the future home that will be built on it
4. **Price guaranteed at contract execution** — regardless of construction cost changes after signing
5. **Buyer personalizes** finishes and upgrades from designer-approved selections
6. **TJH completes construction within 12 months** from start of construction, or pays per-day penalties to the buyer
7. **Closing occurs upon completion** — title transfers with the finished home

**The Densification Arbitrage**: TJH's core economics rely on **lot splitting without entitlement friction**. They identify parcels already legally subdivided or eligible for by-right splitting, then build two homes where one stood. A $1M lot becomes $500K per home in land basis — enabling price points that unlock a larger buyer pool. This strategy leverages SB 9 (§3.2) and SB 684 (§3.5) ministerial pathways to avoid the zoning fights that would make densification politically impossible.

### California Legal Framework for Pre-Construction Sales

Selling homes before construction is complete triggers specific California regulatory requirements. The framework varies based on **how the project is subdivided and marketed**.

#### DRE Public Report Requirements

**Statutory Reference**: Business & Professions Code §11010–11023

Any sale of a **subdivided interest** — including lots in a residential subdivision, condo units, or community apartment units — requires a Department of Real Estate (DRE) **Public Report** before binding contracts can be executed. The DRE issues three types of reports, each permitting different levels of marketing and sales activity:

| Report Type | Common Name | What It Permits | Processing Time | Validity |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Preliminary Public Report** | "Pink Report" | Advertising + non-binding reservations + refundable deposits only | ~2 weeks | 1 year |
| **Conditional Public Report** | "Yellow Report" | Binding purchase contracts + escrow opening (no closing) | 1–4 months | 6 months (renewable) |
| **Final Public Report** | "White Report" | Full sales: advertising, contracts, escrow, **closing** | 2–9 months (CIDs longer) | 5 years |

**The Pre-Construction Sale Sequence:**

```text
Start Marketing ──► Pink Report ──► Accept Reservations
                                          │
                                    Yellow Report ──► Execute Binding Contracts
                                                      Open Escrows
                                                      Collect Earnest Money
                                                          │
                                                    White Report ──► Close Escrows
                                                                     Transfer Title
                                                                     Record Deeds
```

**Critical Rule**: No **closing** may occur until the Final (White) Public Report has been issued. A developer can market and accept binding contracts under a Conditional (Yellow) Report, but all purchase funds must be held in a **neutral escrow depository** acceptable to the DRE until title can be conveyed free and clear of any blanket encumbrance.

#### Blanket Encumbrance Rules (B&P Code §11013)

A **blanket encumbrance** is any trust deed, mortgage, mechanic's lien, or other lien that covers the entire subdivision — not just the individual lot being sold. This is common when the developer has a construction loan secured by the entire project.

To sell individual lots/units before the blanket encumbrance is released, the developer must provide one of the following protections:

| Option | Mechanism | When Used |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Purchase Money Lien Bond** (§11013.2) | Surety bond filed with DRE protecting each buyer's deposit | Most common for larger subdivisions |
| **Impound/Escrow of Funds** (§11013.4) | All buyer funds held in neutral escrow until title conveyed free of blanket lien | Standard for pre-construction sales |
| **Lender Subordination** | Construction lender agrees to release individual lots from the blanket lien upon individual lot closings | Requires lender cooperation |

**TJH Model Note**: TJH typically operates on individual lots (not multi-lot subdivisions), which simplifies the DRE framework. When TJH acquires a single lot, demolishes, and rebuilds, the transaction is a **single-home sale** — not a subdivision sale — and is generally exempt from DRE public report requirements. The exemption applies to: (1) subdivisions creating **four or fewer lots**; (2) standard single-family subdivisions entirely within an incorporated city where the subdivider **builds homes on every lot before sale**; and (3) transactions not involving subdivided interests. TJH's densification model (building 2 homes on a split lot) typically falls within the 4-or-fewer exemption.

#### Contract Structures for Pre-Construction Sales

| Structure | Description | Risk Allocation | DRE Requirement |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Purchase and Sale Agreement (PSA)** | Buyer contracts to purchase the completed home at a fixed price | Price risk on developer; buyer locked in at contract price | Public Report required if subdivided interest |
| **Reservation Agreement** | Non-binding reservation with refundable deposit | No commitment; developer retains price flexibility | Pink Report sufficient |
| **Assignment of Contract** | Original buyer assigns purchase rights to a new buyer before closing | Original buyer captures appreciation; new buyer assumes contract | Assignment must comply with contract terms and DRE rules |
| **Build-to-Suit Contract** | Buyer contracts for a custom or semi-custom home on a specific lot | Builder guarantees price/timeline; buyer selects finishes | May be exempt if not subdivided interest |

**TJH's Guarantee Structure**: TJH's competitive advantage is the **price guarantee at contract execution**. The buyer's purchase price is locked regardless of construction cost changes — all cost risk sits with TJH. This guarantee is backed by TJH's $400M construction debt facility (Oaktree Capital) and their actuarial cost database from 1,100+ completed homes. For the buyer, this eliminates the cost escalation risk that plagues traditional custom building.

#### Pre-Construction Sale Strategy Matrix

| Developer Type | Best Sale Timing | DRE Path | Key Advantage |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Production SFR (TJH model)** | Pre-construction (at permit) | Exempt (≤4 lots, builder-before-sale) | Lowest buyer price; fastest capital recycling |
| **Small lot subdivision (SB 684)** | Pre-construction or mid-construction | Pink → Yellow → White | Lock in buyers before completion; reduce spec risk |
| **Condo development** | Pre-construction (at conditional report) | Pink → Yellow → White (6–9 month processing) | Pre-sales required by lenders (typically 50–70% pre-sold) |
| **SB 9 duplex/lot split** | At completion or near-completion | Exempt (≤4 lots) | Simpler regulatory path; sell when product is visible |
| **Tract subdivision (5+ lots)** | Pre-construction with model homes | Full DRE process | Model home drives sales; phased delivery |

> **Strategy**: For small infill developers (2–4 units), structure your project to fall within the DRE exemptions wherever possible. Building on every lot before sale, or keeping the subdivision at 4 or fewer lots, eliminates months of DRE processing time and significant legal fees. TJH's single-lot model is the cleanest example of this strategy at scale. Cross-reference Chapter 7 (§7.5) for DRE public report processing details and condo map requirements.

## 3.8 Tax Considerations — IRC §1031 Like-Kind Exchange for Developers

**Statutory Reference**: IRC §1031; California Revenue & Taxation Code §18031; FTB Form 3840

The **1031 exchange** (like-kind exchange) is the single most powerful tax deferral tool in real estate — allowing investors to defer **all federal and state capital gains tax** by reinvesting sale proceeds into qualifying replacement property. However, developers face unique complications that investors do not: the **dealer exclusion** can disqualify properties held primarily for sale, and California's **clawback provision** tracks deferred gains indefinitely.

### 1031 Exchange — Core Rules

| Rule | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| **Property type** | Real property held for **productive use in trade/business or investment** (post-2017: real property only — personal property no longer qualifies) |
| **Like-kind** | Any real property for any real property (e.g., apartment building for vacant land, or retail center for residential rental) |
| **Identification period** | Replacement property must be **identified in writing within 45 days** of relinquished property closing |
| **Exchange period** | Replacement property must be **acquired within 180 days** of relinquished property closing (or tax return due date, whichever is earlier) |
| **Qualified Intermediary (QI)** | Mandatory — sale proceeds must **never touch the taxpayer's hands**; QI holds funds and facilitates the exchange |
| **Boot** | Any non-like-kind property received (cash, debt relief) is taxable as "boot" — partial exchange |
| **Same taxpayer** | The same taxpayer (or same entity) that sells must be the one that buys |

### Identification Rules (The 45-Day Clock)

| Rule | What It Allows |
| --- | --- |
| **3-Property Rule** | Identify up to **3 replacement properties** of any value |
| **200% Rule** | Identify any number of properties if total FMV ≤ **200% of relinquished property value** |
| **95% Rule** | Identify any number of properties if you acquire **95% of total identified value** (rarely used — too risky) |

### The Developer's Dilemma — Dealer Exclusion

**IRC §1031(a)(2)** explicitly excludes property **"held primarily for sale"** from 1031 exchange treatment. This is the single biggest trap for developers:

| Taxpayer Classification | 1031 Eligible? | Typical Scenario |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Investor** | Yes | Buy rental property → hold → sell → exchange into new rental |
| **Developer (held for investment)** | Yes — if structured correctly | Build → hold as rental for 12+ months → sell → exchange |
| **Developer (held for sale / "dealer")** | **No** | Build → sell immediately upon completion → **no 1031** |

**The IRS "Dealer" Test** — factors courts examine:

1. **Purpose of acquisition** — did you buy to hold or buy to flip?
2. **Frequency and continuity of sales** — are you in the business of selling real estate?
3. **Extent of improvements** — did you develop and sell, or hold and rent?
4. **Holding period** — how long did you own before selling? (12+ months is safer; no statutory safe harbor)
5. **Number of properties sold** — pattern of regular sales suggests dealer status
6. **Marketing efforts** — active marketing suggests held for sale, not investment

**Dual Role Doctrine**: A developer can be a **dealer for some properties and an investor for others** — the determination is made property-by-property, not entity-wide. This creates structuring opportunities:

```text
Developer LLC (Dealer)          Investment LLC (Investor)
├── Build and sell homes         ├── Hold rental properties
├── No 1031 eligibility          ├── Full 1031 eligibility
└── Ordinary income tax          └── Capital gains + deferral

Strategy: Separate entities for build-to-sell vs. build-to-hold
```

> **Critical Structuring Rule**: If you intend to 1031 exchange a property, hold it in a **separate entity** from your development/dealer activities, hold it as a rental for a minimum of **12 months** (24 months is safer), and demonstrate genuine **investment intent** through rental activity, depreciation, and operating as a landlord — not as a developer marketing the property for sale.

### California-Specific Rules — The Clawback

California adds a layer of complexity not present in other states:

#### FTB Form 3840 — Annual Reporting Requirement

If you sell California real property in a 1031 exchange and acquire **replacement property outside California**, you must file **FTB Form 3840** (California Like-Kind Exchanges) **every year** until the deferred gain is recognized. This is California's "clawback" mechanism:

| Scenario | California Tax Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| CA property → CA replacement | Standard 1031 deferral; no annual reporting |
| CA property → **out-of-state** replacement | Gain deferred **but tracked by FTB annually** via Form 3840 |
| Sell out-of-state replacement later | **California taxes the original deferred gain** — even if you've left the state |
| Failure to file Form 3840 | FTB may assess tax on the full deferred gain + penalties + interest |

**The Practical Impact**: Developers who sell Bay Area property and exchange into Texas or Nevada real estate (common for tax migration) must continue filing Form 3840 with the California FTB **indefinitely** — until the replacement property is sold in a taxable transaction. California will then assess state tax on the original California-source deferred gain. Moving to a no-income-tax state does **not** eliminate California's claim.

### Advanced 1031 Strategies for Developers

#### Reverse Exchange (Revenue Procedure 2000-37)

| Step | Action |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | **Exchange Accommodation Titleholder (EAT)** acquires replacement property first |
| 2 | Developer sells relinquished property within 180 days |
| 3 | EAT transfers replacement property to developer |

**Use case**: You find the perfect replacement property before your current property sells. The reverse exchange lets you acquire first — but it requires an EAT (typically the QI's affiliate), adds $15K–$30K in transaction costs, and has the same 45/180-day clocks running in reverse.

#### Improvement Exchange (Build-to-Suit)

| Step | Action |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Sell relinquished property; proceeds go to QI |
| 2 | QI's affiliate (EAT) acquires replacement property |
| 3 | **Improvements made on replacement property** using exchange funds (within 180 days) |
| 4 | Improved replacement property transferred to developer |

**Use case**: You sell a completed project and want to exchange into raw land that you'll develop. The improvement exchange lets you use exchange proceeds to fund construction on the replacement site — effectively rolling gains into the next development. The 180-day window is tight for significant construction, so this works best for land improvements, infrastructure, or modular/prefab projects with fast timelines.

#### Delaware Statutory Trust (DST)

A DST is a passive investment vehicle that qualifies as replacement property for 1031 exchanges. DSTs own institutional-grade real estate (apartments, industrial, medical office) and are structured as fractional ownership interests.

| Feature | DST Advantage |
| --- | --- |
| **Minimum investment** | Typically $100K–$250K |
| **Management** | Fully passive — no landlord obligations |
| **Debt** | Sponsor provides non-recourse debt (satisfies debt replacement requirement) |
| **Identification** | Multiple DSTs can be identified under the 3-Property or 200% Rule |
| **Exit** | DST-to-721 UPREIT conversion available for tax-deferred exit into REIT shares |

**Use case**: A developer who wants to exit active real estate but defer all gains. Sell your California development, exchange into DST interests, collect passive income, and defer gains indefinitely. The DST-to-721 UPREIT pathway eventually converts into publicly traded REIT shares — providing liquidity without triggering the deferred gain.

### 1031 Exchange Decision Matrix for Developers

| Situation | Can You 1031? | Strategy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sell completed spec home (built to sell) | **No** — dealer property | Separate entity; hold as rental 12+ months first |
| Sell rental property you developed | **Yes** — if held as investment (12+ months) | Standard forward exchange |
| Sell land you held for investment | **Yes** | Forward or reverse exchange |
| Sell CA property, buy out-of-state | **Yes** — but FTB clawback applies | File Form 3840 annually; budget for eventual CA tax |
| Need to buy replacement before selling | **Yes** — reverse exchange | Budget $15K–$30K extra for EAT structure |
| Want to improve replacement property | **Yes** — improvement exchange | 180-day construction window; plan for fast-track build |
| Want passive income, no management | **Yes** — DST | Fractional institutional real estate; fully passive |
| Related party transaction | **Caution** — 2-year hold required | Both parties must hold for 2 years or exchange is disqualified |

### Pro Forma Impact of 1031 Exchange

| Factor | Without 1031 | With 1031 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Federal capital gains tax** | 20% (long-term) + 3.8% NIIT = **23.8%** | **$0** (deferred) |
| **California state tax** | Up to **13.3%** | **$0** (deferred, or clawback if out-of-state) |
| **Depreciation recapture** | **25%** federal on accumulated depreciation | **$0** (deferred) |
| **Total tax on $1M gain** | ~$350K–$370K | **$0** (reinvested) |
| **Equity available for next project** | ~$630K–$650K | **$1M** (full reinvestment) |

The compounding effect over multiple exchanges is enormous. A developer who 1031-exchanges through five successive projects defers tax on the cumulative gains from all five — the entire chain of appreciation remains invested and compounding.

> **Strategy**: Structure your development pipeline to include at least one **hold-for-rental entity** alongside your build-to-sell operations. When a project's market conditions favor holding over selling (rising rents, soft resale market), pivot to the rental entity, hold for 12+ months, then 1031 into the next acquisition. This dual-track approach — selling some projects and exchanging others — maximizes after-tax returns across the portfolio. Always engage a **Qualified Intermediary** before listing any property you intend to exchange; the QI must be in place before closing. Cross-reference Appendix H (§H.2) for CPA/tax advisor engagement timing.

## 3.9 Factory-Built Housing — HCD Regulatory Framework, US Modular Factories & International Supply Chain

**Statutory Reference**: Health & Safety Code §§19960–19997; CCR Title 25, Division 1, Chapter 3, Subchapter 1; CBC Title 24 §1.8.3.2.5

Factory-built housing (FBH) is the most underutilized weapon in a California developer's arsenal. While site-built construction faces labor shortages ($65–$85/hr Bay Area framing crews), weather delays, and 18–24 month build cycles, factory-built modules arrive **70–95% complete** — inspected, insulated, plumbed, wired, painted, and ready for installation in weeks. California's HCD regulatory framework provides **state preemption of local building codes** for FBH bearing the Insignia of Approval, meaning local jurisdictions cannot require additional plan review, charge plan check fees, or inspect the interior of certified modules. For developers operating at scale (10+ units/year), factory-built construction compresses timelines 30–60%, reduces per-unit costs 15–30%, and eliminates the labor availability risk that kills more California projects than bad economics.

This section covers: (1) the complete HCD regulatory pathway, (2) two major US factories serving California — VBC in Tracy and Autovol in Idaho, and (3) the emerging Poland-to-US international supply chain.

---

### 3.9.1 HCD Factory-Built Housing (FBH) — The Regulatory Framework

#### What Is Factory-Built Housing?

Per HSC §19971, **factory-built housing** is any residential building, dwelling unit, dwelling room, or building component manufactured so that all concealed parts or processes of manufacturing cannot be inspected before installation at the building site. The critical distinction from manufactured housing (HUD code mobile homes) is that FBH is built to the **same California Building Standards Code** as site-built construction:

| Aspect | Factory-Built Housing (FBH) | Manufactured Housing (HUD Code) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Governing code** | California Building Standards Code (Title 24) — CBC, CRC, CEC, CPC, CMC | Federal HUD Code (24 CFR 3280) — preempts all state/local codes |
| **Regulatory authority** | California HCD + local building departments | Federal HUD only |
| **Foundation** | Permanent foundation required (identical to site-built) | Transportable chassis; may remain on wheels |
| **Property classification** | Real property (same as site-built) | Personal property (chattel) unless permanently affixed with HCD 433A |
| **Financing** | Conventional mortgage, FHA, VA — identical to site-built | FHA Title I, chattel loans; conventional only if on permanent foundation |
| **Certification** | HCD Insignia of Approval (state) | HUD Label/Data Plate (federal) |
| **Building types** | SFD, multifamily, apartments, condos, hotels, dorms, hospitals | Single-family dwellings only |
| **Appraisal treatment** | Same as site-built (no discount) | Typically discounted 10–30% vs. site-built |

> **Developer Takeaway**: FBH/modular homes are **legally and financially identical to site-built homes** in California. Lenders, appraisers, and insurers treat them the same. This eliminates the "modular discount" fear that keeps many developers on the sidelines.

#### The HCD Approval Pathway — Step by Step

```text
Step 1: Design → Architect/engineer prepares plans to CBC/CRC
     ↓
Step 2: DAA Review → Submit to Design Approval Agency (4–6 weeks)
     ↓              DAA stamps each sheet with HCD approval + expiration date
     ↓              Approval valid 3 years
     ↓
Step 3: Local Submittal → Submit DAA-stamped plans to local building dept
     ↓                    Local reviews ONLY: site plan, foundation, utility
     ↓                    connections, zoning, setbacks, fire access
     ↓                    Cannot re-review DAA-approved portions
     ↓
Step 4: Factory Construction → 70–95% of building constructed in factory
     ↓                         QAA inspects during manufacturing
     ↓                         HCD Insignia affixed after passing inspection
     ↓
Step 5: Transport & Install → Modules shipped to site
     ↓                        Local building dept inspects installation only
     ↓
Step 6: Final Inspection → Local dept inspects connections, utilities,
                            site-built portions → Certificate of Occupancy
```

**Timeline Advantage**: Steps 2–3 (DAA review + local submittal) can proceed **in parallel with** Step 4 (factory construction). Planning, historic review, engineering, and fire plan review can all begin before the DAA stamps plans. This concurrency is the source of the 30–60% schedule compression.

#### The Insignia of Approval — State Preemption Power

Per HSC §19980, every factory-built housing section sold in California must bear an **HCD Insignia of Approval**. Per HSC §19981, FBH bearing the insignia **"shall be deemed to comply"** with all local construction ordinances and regulations. This statutory language creates powerful state preemption:

**What Local Departments CANNOT Do (HSC §19981 + AB 2492):**

- Cannot require plan review or charge plan check fees for DAA-approved portions
- Cannot inspect the interior of insignia-bearing modules for building code compliance
- Cannot require alterations to meet local codes (except local design loads, aesthetics, and property location)

**What Local Departments STILL Control:**

- Zoning, setbacks, height limits, fire access, ADA paths of travel
- Foundation design review and inspection
- Connections between modules (structural, MEP)
- All site-built portions (foundation, garages, decks, utility hookups)
- Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ) compliance
- Post-installation alterations

#### Third-Party Agency System

| Agency Type | Role | How to Find |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **DAA (Design Approval Agency)** | Reviews plans against CBC + CCR Title 25; stamps approved sheets with HCD approval | HCD-certified list: hcd.ca.gov → Building Standards → Third-Party Agencies |
| **QAA (Quality Assurance Agency)** | Inspects factory production; verifies compliance with DAA-approved plans; applies HCD Insignia | Same HCD-certified list; must execute HCD MH/FBH 443 contract notice |

**Certification Application**: HCD FBH 307 (Design Approval Agency and/or Quality Assurance Agency Certification Application)

#### Fee Schedule (Effective April 1, 2024)

| Fee | Amount | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| HCD Insignia of Approval | **$23 per insignia** | Per module/section; paid to HCD |
| Preliminary Plan Check Meeting | **$238/hour** | One-hour minimum |
| DAA Plan Review | Varies by scope | Typically $5K–$25K depending on project complexity |
| Local building permit | Per local schedule | FBH portions billed separately; no plan check fee for DAA-approved portions |

**Contact**: HCD Division of Codes and Standards — (800) 952-8356 / ContactMH-FBH@hcd.ca.gov

#### Applicable Building Codes

FBH must comply with the **full California Building Standards Code (Title 24)**, including:

| Code | Full Name | Key Applicability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **CBC** (Part 2) | California Building Code | Structural, fire-resistance, occupancy |
| **CRC** (Part 2.5) | California Residential Code | 1–2 family dwellings and townhomes |
| **CEC** (Part 3) | California Electrical Code | Electrical systems |
| **CMC** (Part 4) | California Mechanical Code | HVAC, mechanical |
| **CPC** (Part 5) | California Plumbing Code | Plumbing systems |
| **CALGreen** (Part 11) | Green Building Standards | Sustainability requirements |
| **Energy Code** (Part 6) | California Energy Code | Energy efficiency (heat pumps, insulation) |
| **Fire Code** (Part 9) | California Fire Code | Fire safety; all factory-installed fire alarms require functional testing on site |

**Code Edition Lock**: Projects follow the code edition in effect at the time of DAA approval, valid for **3 years**. This creates a powerful strategy — secure DAA approval before a new code cycle takes effect to lock in the current (often less restrictive) code requirements.

#### Recent Legislative Changes (2024–2026)

| Legislation | What It Does | Developer Impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **AB 2492** | Establishes "qualified manufacturer" requirements (medical coverage, training, labor peace); codifies interior inspection prohibition; creates density bonus for FBH | Density bonus: 10% lower-income = bonus; 5% very-low = bonus; units affordable 55+ years |
| **2025 CBC** (IB 2025-03) | New building code effective January 1, 2026 | Secure DAA approval before 1/1/2026 to lock current code for 3 years |

| **SGC FBH Pilot Program** | State grant funding for factory-built housing (Round 3 NOFA released early 2026) | California Climate Investment dollars; focus on affordable housing + wildfire recovery |

> **Strategy**: Factory-built housing is uniquely synergistic with the housing acceleration laws covered in §§3.1–3.6. A modular project using SB 35 ministerial approval + AB 130 CEQA exemption + HCD FBH state preemption achieves **triple bypass** — no CEQA, no discretionary hearing, and no local building code interference. The combination compresses a traditional 24–36 month entitlement-to-occupancy timeline to **8–14 months**.

#### The Tariff Problem (2025–2026)

| Tariff Layer | Rate | Impact on Polish Modules |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Section 232 Steel Tariff** | **50%** (increased June 4, 2025) | Applies to steel content of steel-frame modules; fabricated structural steel added as protected derivative product (Feb 10, 2025) |
| **EU Reciprocal Tariff** | **15%** total (MFN + reciprocal) | Applies to non-steel value of modules under EU-US Framework Agreement |
| **Net effect on steel-frame modules** | **Potentially devastating** | 50% on steel content + 15% on remaining value = effective 25–35% tariff on total module value |
| **Timber-frame modules** | 15% reciprocal only | No Section 232 exposure; but timber faces different CBC compliance path |

> **Developer Warning**: The 2025 tariff environment makes steel-frame module import from Poland economically unviable for most projects. The 50% steel tariff alone can add $20,000–$50,000 per module. The practical path is VBC's domestic Tracy factory (no tariff exposure) or Autovol's Idaho factory (domestic, no tariff). Monitor tariff environment quarterly — a trade deal could reopen the Poland corridor.

#### HCD Compliance for International Modules

For overseas-manufactured modules to receive HCD Insignia of Approval, one of two paths is required:

| Path | How It Works | Cost/Complexity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **A. In-factory QAA inspection** | California-certified Quality Assurance Agency inspector stationed at or regularly visiting Polish factory | High — inspector travel, per diem, coordination across 9-hour time zone difference |
| **B. US-side inspection facility** | Modules shipped to US staging facility; QAA inspection + insignia applied before final delivery | High — requires US staging space, double handling, additional transit time |

**Practical Reality**: Path A requires either a full-time QAA inspector in Poland or a series of planned inspection trips timed to production batches. Path B adds 1–2 weeks and $5,000–$15,000 per batch in staging/handling costs. Both paths are viable for large projects (100+ modules) but uneconomical for small projects (<20 modules).

### 3.9.5 Factory-Built Housing Decision Framework

#### When to Use Modular vs. Site-Built

| Factor | Use Modular | Use Site-Built |
| --- | --- | --- |

| **Financing** | LIHTC, tax-exempt bonds, grants (favor fast delivery) | Conventional construction loan (no delivery premium) |
| **Building type** | Multifamily, student housing, senior, hotel | Custom SFR, hillside, irregular geometry |

#### The Modular Pro Forma Adjustment

When evaluating modular vs. site-built on the same project, adjust your pro forma:

| Line Item | Site-Built | Modular | Net Impact |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Hard construction cost** | Baseline | –15% to –30% | ↓ Major savings |
| **Contingency** | 10–15% | 5–10% | ↓ Factory control reduces unknowns |
| **Construction interest** | 18–24 months | 8–14 months | ↓ 40–60% less carry cost |
| **General conditions** | 18–24 months | 8–14 months | ↓ Shorter superintendent/trailer/fence duration |
| **Insurance (builder's risk)** | 18–24 months premium | 8–14 months premium | ↓ Shorter exposure period |
| **Revenue start** | Month 24–30 | Month 12–18 | ↑ 12+ months earlier rent/sales income |
| **Design cost** | Baseline | +10–20% (BIM/modular optimization) | ↑ Higher upfront design investment |
| **Factory deposit** | None | 10–30% at contract | ↑ Earlier capital deployment |
| **Transportation** | Minimal | $5–$30/SF depending on distance | ↑ New line item |
| **Crane/set** | None | $50K–$200K depending on building size | ↑ New line item |
| **Net project IRR impact** | Baseline | **+5 to +15 points** | Earlier revenue + lower cost = dramatically higher returns |

> **Strategy**: The biggest financial advantage of modular isn't the construction cost savings — it's the **time value of money**. A 12-month reduction in construction duration means 12 fewer months of construction loan interest (typically 7–10% on total draw), 12 fewer months of property taxes on an unproductive asset, and 12 months of **earlier revenue** from rents or sales. On a $20M multifamily project, 12 months of schedule compression saves $1.5M–$2.5M in carry costs alone — before counting the rental income gained. This is why Autovol's affordable housing clients (LIHTC) love modular: the tax credit delivery date is immovable, and faster construction means earlier stabilization and investor payoff.

::: keytakeaways

- **SB 35/423 is ministerial approval with teeth**: Qualifying multifamily projects in RHNA-deficient jurisdictions (most Bay Area cities) receive by-right approval in 90–180 days with no CEQA, no hearings, no discretionary denial—cutting entitlement timelines from 2–3 years to 6 months. Check HCD's annual determination letter before site acquisition.

- **SB 9 unlocks 4 units on any single-family lot**: Duplex pathway + lot split = 4 units ministerial, no CEQA, 60–90 day approval. Combined with ADUs, achieves 6+ units on lots previously limited to 1. The 3-year owner-occupancy requirement for lot splits is the key constraint—plan your hold strategy accordingly.

- **SB 330 preliminary application locks fees and standards before design**: File immediately upon site control to freeze development fees, exactions, and building standards—preventing mid-entitlement increases of $5K–$10K/unit common in Bay Area jurisdictions. Requires only site address, unit count, and footprint sketch—no architectural drawings.

- **AB 130 CEQA exemption eliminates EIR for 70–85% of urban infill**: Projects ≤20 acres on urban infill sites with no environmental constraints now bypass CEQA entirely—saving $200K–$2M in consultant costs and 12–24 months. If one criterion is missed, SB 131's near-miss limited review targets the single issue, not full EIR. Run the AB 130 checklist before defaulting to traditional CEQA.

- **SB 79 transit-oriented density override reaches 120 du/ac at 75 feet**: Sites within rail/BRT distance thresholds qualify for state-mandated minimums that override local zoning—unlocking 3–5x density increases near Caltrain, BART, VTA. Tier 1 (major transit): 120 du/ac within 1,320 ft. Map your site's tier and distance before running pro formas.

- **Housing Accountability Act carries $10K/unit minimum penalty**: Cities cannot deny housing projects complying with objective standards without written findings of specific, unavoidable public health/safety impacts. Attorney's fees go to prevailing plaintiffs. HAA's burden-of-proof shift means conforming projects have near-zero denial risk—a fundamental change from discretionary hotel/commercial approvals.

- **Pre-construction sales de-risk absorption and accelerate capital recycling**: TJH's model (350+ homes/year) sells at permit stage under DRE exemptions, eliminating spec inventory risk and compressing capital cycles to 8–12 months. For subdivisions, Pink Report enables marketing, Yellow Report allows binding contracts. DRE path adds 2–9 months—structure projects ≤4 lots for exemption when possible.

- **IRC §1031 exchange defers 37% combined federal + California capital gains**: Developers who hold properties as investment (12+ months minimum) can exchange into replacement property, deferring 23.8% federal + 13.3% CA tax—leaving ~37% more equity available for the next project. Separate build-to-sell entities from hold-for-investment entities to avoid dealer exclusion trap. Engage Qualified Intermediary before listing.

- **Factory-built housing delivers 30–60% schedule compression with state preemption of local codes**: HCD's Insignia of Approval (HSC §19981) means local building departments cannot re-review or charge plan check fees for factory-approved modules—only site connections and foundations. VBC's Tracy factory (577K SF, 60 miles from SF) and Autovol's Idaho facility (53 robots, $120/SF delivered to Bay Area vs. $220+/SF site-built) serve California's multifamily and affordable housing markets. The biggest financial win isn't cost savings—it's the time value: 12 fewer months of construction loan interest on a $20M project saves $1.5M–$2.5M in carry costs alone, plus 12 months of earlier revenue.

- [ ] Track Permit Streamlining Act statutory clocks (30-day completeness, 60/120/180-day approval deadlines)—send formal notice if city misses deadlines (§3.5.4)
- [ ] For jurisdictions with posted timelines (AB 2234): document delays beyond posted timelines for escalation leverage
- [ ] Confirm blanket encumbrance release or bond if accepting buyer deposits before certificate of occupancy (DRE compliance, §3.7)

**Pre-Construction / Tax Planning:**

- [ ] For projects intended for 1031 exchange: hold in separate entity from build-to-sell operations, plan 12–24 month rental hold period (§3.8)
- [ ] Engage Qualified Intermediary before listing any property intended for exchange—funds cannot touch developer's accounts
- [ ] If exchanging California property for out-of-state replacement: set up annual FTB Form 3840 filing protocol (California clawback tracking)
- [ ] For pre-construction sales: determine DRE public report requirement (≤4 lots or build-before-sale = exempt; else file Pink → Yellow → White)

**Factory-Built Housing (§3.9):**

- [ ] For multifamily projects (10+ units): run modular vs. site-built pro forma—compare hard cost, schedule, carry cost, and revenue timing (§3.9.5)
- [ ] Evaluate VBC Tracy (wood-frame, 1–5 stories, $5K–$7K feasibility study) and Autovol Idaho (robotic, affordable housing focus, $120/SF delivered)
- [ ] Verify site has crane access and adequate staging area for module delivery and installation
- [ ] Engage HCD-certified Design Approval Agency (DAA) for plan review—secure approval before 1/1/2026 to lock 2022 CBC for 3 years
- [ ] Confirm Quality Assurance Agency (QAA) availability for in-plant inspection and HCD Insignia application
- [ ] For LIHTC/affordable projects: evaluate Autovol's pipeline (4% tax credits + tax-exempt bonds + 30–40% schedule savings = faster investor payoff)
- [ ] For international modules (Poland): assess tariff exposure (50% steel + 15% reciprocal) and HCD QAA inspection logistics before committing

**Title 24 2025 Compliance (Permits Filed 2026+):**

- [ ] Design for all-electric: heat pump HVAC, heat pump water heater, EV-ready garage, battery storage (BESS)—gas pool heaters no longer code-compliant (§3.6.6)
- [ ] For sites ≤500 ft from freeway: budget for MERV 16 HVAC filtration, no freeway-facing balconies (AB 205 air quality)
