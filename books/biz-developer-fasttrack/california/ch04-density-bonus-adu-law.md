# Chapter 4: Density Bonus & ADU Law

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65915–65918 (Density Bonus); §65852.2, §65852.22 (ADU); §65852.2(h) (Pre-Approved Plans, AB 1332/AB 434)

## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

California's Density Bonus Law and ADU legislation represent two of the highest-leverage tools for residential developers to maximize unit yield and financial returns on constrained urban sites. The Density Bonus Law mandates that cities provide increased density and concessions (parking reduction, height increase, setback relaxation) when projects include affordable units—with a 50% bonus at 15% very-low-income units, and an extraordinary 80% bonus for 100% affordable projects. Government Code §65915 makes concession denials nearly impossible: the city must prove a specific, unmitigable health/safety impact, a standard almost never met. ADU law has evolved from a 2017 opt-in to a 2025 by-right mandate—any residential property can now build detached, attached, junior, or conversion ADUs with 60-day approval and no CEQA. Pre-approved ADU plans (AB 1332, effective January 2025) collapse the design-and-permit cycle from 12+ months to 4-8 weeks, with soft costs dropping from $15K–$30K to $3.5K–$5K. HCD-certified prefab modular ADUs (AIAadu, Abodu, Villa Homes) compress total delivery timelines to 3-6 months versus 9-12+ months for stick-built, making them the only scalable model for portfolio ADU builders targeting 10+ units annually. The case studies (Palo Alto's 57-unit density bonus, Menlo Park's 108-unit 100% affordable project, San Jose's pre-approved + prefab ADU yielding $550K–$650K in property value added) demonstrate that density bonus and ADU economics often transform projects that fail the Napkin Test into viable ventures.

:::

## Part A: Density Bonus Law

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65915–65918

The Density Bonus Law mandates that local governments provide **increased density and development incentives/concessions** to projects that include affordable or senior housing components. This is a critical tool for maximizing unit count and project economics.

---

### 4.1 Density Bonus Schedule

| Affordable Category | Minimum Units | Base Density Bonus | Maximum Bonus | Concessions/Incentives |
|--------------------|--------------|-------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Very Low Income** | 5% | 20% | 50% (at 15%+ VLI) | 1 (5%), 2 (10%), 3 (15%) |
| **Low Income** | 10% | 20% | 50% (at 24%+ LI) | 1 (10%), 2 (17%), 3 (24%) |
| **Moderate Income** (condo) | 10% | 5% | 35% (at 40%+ MOD) | 1 (10%), 2 (20%), 3 (30%) |
| **Senior Housing** | 100% senior | 20% | 20% | 1 |
| **Land Donation** | Per §65915(g) | 15% | 35% | Per agreement |
| **100% Affordable** | 100% | 80% | 80% | 4 + unlimited waivers |

---

### 4.2 Concessions and Incentives

A concession/incentive is a **relaxation of a development standard** that results in identifiable, financially sufficient, and actual cost reductions.

**Common concessions include**:
- Reduced parking requirements
- Increased height
- Reduced setbacks
- Increased FAR

The jurisdiction may deny a concession **only if** it can make a written finding that the concession would have a specific, adverse impact on health, safety, or the physical environment, and there is no feasible alternative.

---

### 4.3 Waivers and Reductions

Separate from concessions, a developer may request a **waiver or reduction** of any development standard that would physically preclude construction at the permitted density.

- Waivers are **unlimited in number**
- The jurisdiction **cannot deny** a waiver unless it finds a specific adverse impact on health or safety

## Part B: Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) Law

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65852.2, §65852.22

ADU law has been progressively expanded since 2017 to remove barriers to construction of secondary units. ADUs are now **permitted by right** on any residential or mixed-use lot.

---

### 4.4 ADU Types and Standards

| ADU Type | Size Limit | Setback | Key Rules |
|----------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Detached ADU** | 1,200 SF (or local standard) | 4 ft side/rear | Must allow at least 800 SF regardless of lot coverage or FAR |
| **Attached ADU** | 50% of primary or 1,200 SF | 4 ft side/rear | No replacement parking if garage converted |
| **JADU** | 500 SF max | None (within existing) | Must be within existing primary dwelling; efficiency kitchen; owner-occupancy required |
| **Conversion ADU** | No size limit | No setback required | Converting existing structure; no limit on number on multifamily lots |

### Height Standards (SB 897)

| Condition | Maximum Height |
|-----------|---------------|
| Detached ADU | 16 ft |
| Near transit | 18 ft |
| Above primary dwelling | 25 ft |

---

### 4.5 Recent ADU Law Updates

| Bill | Key Change |
|------|-----------|
| **AB 68 / AB 881** | Reduced local discretion over ADU approval; 60-day approval deadline |
| **AB 671** | Requires housing elements to incentivize affordable ADUs |
| **AB 2221** | ADUs permitted on lots with proposed or existing multifamily buildings |
| **SB 897** | Set height standards (16/18/25 ft) |
| **AB 1033** | Allows ADUs to be sold separately as condominiums (if local jurisdiction opts in) |

### 4.6 Pre-Approved ADU Plans — AB 1332 & AB 434

**Statutory Reference**: Government Code §65852.2(h) (as amended by AB 1332 and AB 434)

California enacted **AB 1332** and **AB 434** to eliminate one of the biggest friction points in ADU development: the design-and-permit review cycle. Together, these laws require every local jurisdiction to maintain a **pre-approved ADU plan program** and create a streamlined permitting pathway for homeowners who select from the pre-approved catalog.

#### How Pre-Approved Plans Work

| Step | Traditional ADU | Pre-Approved ADU |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **1. Design** | Custom architect: 4–8 weeks, $8K–$20K | Select from city catalog: 0 weeks, $0–$5K |
| **2. Plan Check** | Full review: 4–12 weeks | Expedited review: 2–4 weeks |
| **3. Permit Decision** | 60 days (statutory max) | **30 days** (AB 1332 mandate) |
| **Total Pre-Construction** | 3–6 months, $15K–$30K in soft costs | **4–8 weeks, $3.5K–$5K** in soft costs |

#### AB 1332 Key Requirements (Effective January 1, 2025)

- Every local agency **must** develop and maintain a pre-approved ADU plan program
- If a homeowner submits a building permit application using a pre-approved plan, the jurisdiction **must approve or deny within 30 days** of receiving a complete application
- Pre-approved plans must include a range of unit sizes and configurations
- Plans must comply with state ADU law and local objective standards

#### AB 434 Additions

- Jurisdictions must make pre-approved plans **publicly available** on their website
- Plans must be offered at **no cost or minimal cost** to applicants
- The program must include plans suitable for a variety of lot types and sizes

## Bay Area Case Studies & Lessons Learned

### Case 1: Palo Alto — Density Bonus Overcomes Height Limit

A developer on El Camino Real in Palo Alto proposed a 57-unit project on a site zoned for 40 units. By including 15% very low income units, the project qualified for a **50% density bonus** (40 to 60 units) plus three concessions. The developer used one concession to exceed the 50-ft height limit (to 60 ft) and another to reduce parking below code requirements. Palo Alto attempted to deny the height concession, arguing it was inconsistent with the city's character. The developer cited Government Code §65915(e) — the city could not deny the concession without a finding of specific adverse health/safety impact. The project was approved.

**Lesson Learned**: Density bonus concessions are your tool for breaking through local development caps (height, FAR, parking) that would otherwise constrain your project. Cities often resist concessions, but the burden of proof is on them to show specific adverse impacts — a very high bar. Come prepared with documentation showing your concession does not create health/safety issues.

---

### Case 2: Menlo Park — 100% Affordable Project Gets 80% Density Bonus

The **1300 Willow Road** project in Menlo Park was a 100% affordable housing project that qualified for an 80% density bonus plus 4 concessions and unlimited waivers. The base zoning allowed 60 units; the density bonus yielded 108 units. The developer used waivers to exceed height, reduce rear setbacks, and eliminate a landscape buffer — all of which were development standards that would have physically precluded construction at the permitted density. The city could not deny any waiver.

**Lesson Learned**: For 100% affordable projects, the density bonus law is extraordinarily powerful. The 80% bonus combined with unlimited waivers means you can essentially redesign the development envelope. These projects are virtually undeniable under current law. If your pro forma works at 100% affordable (often with LIHTC, tax-exempt bonds, and local subsidies), the regulatory pathway is the smoothest in California.

### Case 3: Bay Area ADU Boom — Palo Alto's 400+ ADU Permits

Palo Alto — one of the most restrictive cities for new development — has issued over 400 ADU permits since 2020. The city's ADU ordinance was forced to comply with state law after HCD found it overly restrictive. Key changes included: eliminating owner-occupancy requirements (which state law prohibited), allowing 800 SF ADUs regardless of lot coverage, and accepting garage conversions without replacement parking.

The average ADU in Palo Alto rents for $3,000–$4,500/month, making it one of the highest-return ADU markets in California. Garage conversion ADUs (the least expensive to build) achieve the highest ROI because they avoid foundation and utility connection costs.

**Lesson Learned**: ADUs are the quiet workhorse of Bay Area housing production. Even in the most restrictive jurisdictions, state law guarantees your right to build them. The 60-day approval deadline means cities cannot slow-walk the process. For investors, garage conversion ADUs in high-rent markets like Palo Alto and Menlo Park offer returns that exceed ground-up construction on a per-dollar-invested basis.

### Case 4: Sunnyvale — ADU + SB 9 Combo for Maximum Density

A Sunnyvale homeowner on an 8,000 SF lot used the following strategy: (1) SB 9 lot split into two 4,000 SF parcels, (2) duplex on each parcel under SB 9 = 4 units, (3) detached ADU on each parcel = 2 ADUs, (4) JADU within each duplex = 2 JADUs. **Total: 8 units** on a lot that previously had 1 house. All approvals were ministerial with no CEQA and no public hearings.

**Lesson Learned**: The combination of SB 9 + ADU + JADU can yield dramatically more units than any single tool alone. Run the combinatorial analysis for every single-family lot project. The math often favors this "stacking" approach over a traditional small-lot subdivision.

> **Pro Tip**: Run the Appendix G Napkin Test (Level 1) for both the base density and the density-bonus-enhanced scenario. In many Bay Area projects, the base density fails the Napkin Test but the density bonus scenario passes — making the affordable set-aside not just a regulatory requirement but a financial enabler.

> **Prefab Pro Tip**: When running the Napkin Test for an ADU project, model **two scenarios**: (1) traditional stick-built at $350K+ / 12-month timeline, and (2) prefab at $210K / 4-month timeline. The prefab scenario doesn't just save on hard costs — the 8-month earlier rent start ($3K+/month × 8 months = $24K+ additional income) and reduced carrying costs compound to dramatically improve IRR. For portfolio builders, this delta multiplied across 10+ ADUs per year is the difference between a lifestyle business and a scalable enterprise.

## Practitioner Checklist

### Density Bonus
- [ ] Calculate base density allowed by zoning
- [ ] Determine affordable unit set-aside percentage and income level
- [ ] Calculate resulting density bonus percentage
- [ ] Identify which concessions/incentives to request
- [ ] Evaluate whether waivers are needed for physical feasibility
- [ ] Run pro forma with and without density bonus to compare returns

### ADU

- [ ] Confirm lot is residential or mixed-use (ADU by right)
- [ ] Determine ADU type: detached, attached, JADU, or conversion
- [ ] Check local ADU ordinance for additional (permissible) standards
- [ ] Verify 60-day approval deadline is being met
- [ ] Consider AB 1033 condo sale option if local jurisdiction has opted in
- [ ] Evaluate ADU vs. SB 9 duplex for the specific project

### Pre-Approved Plans & Prefab

- [ ] Check target city's pre-approved ADU plan catalog before commissioning custom design
- [ ] Confirm AB 1332 30-day permit timeline applies if using pre-approved plan
- [ ] For prefab ADU: verify provider is **HCD-certified** (real property, not personal property)
- [ ] Compare all-in cost: prefab turnkey vs. traditional stick-built (include carrying costs)
- [ ] For portfolio builders (10+ ADUs/year): evaluate prefab provider pre-approvals across target jurisdictions
- [ ] Verify site access for module delivery (crane reach, street width, overhead clearances)
- [ ] Confirm foundation and utility connection scope in prefab provider's fixed-price quote
