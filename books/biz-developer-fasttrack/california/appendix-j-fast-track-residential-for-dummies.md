## Residential Remodel

### What Is It?

You already own a house. You want to change the inside — new kitchen, new bathrooms, open up walls, replace windows, upgrade electrical/plumbing. The footprint of the house stays the same. No new square footage.

### When to Use It

- Kitchen or bathroom gut renovation
- Removing or adding interior walls
- Upgrading electrical panel, plumbing, or HVAC
- Replacing windows, doors, or roofing
- Seismic retrofit

### Step-by-Step Process

```text
Step 1: DESIGN (2–6 weeks)
    Hire architect or designer for plans
    For cosmetic work (paint, flooring, fixtures): no permit needed
    For structural/MEP work: plans required
    │
Step 2: PERMIT APPLICATION (1 day)
    Submit plans to city building department
    Pay plan check fees ($500–$3,000 typical)
    │
Step 3: PLAN CHECK (2–8 weeks)
    City reviews for code compliance
    Corrections may be required (add 1–3 weeks)
    │
Step 4: PERMIT ISSUED
    Post permit card on-site
    │
Step 5: CONSTRUCTION (4–16 weeks)
    Hire licensed contractor (or owner-build for non-structural)
    Schedule inspections at each stage:
      □ Rough framing (if walls moved)
      □ Rough electrical
      □ Rough plumbing
      □ Rough mechanical (HVAC)
      □ Insulation
      □ Final inspection
    │
Step 6: FINAL SIGN-OFF
    Building inspector approves final
    Close out permit
    Done.
```

### Timeline

| Scope | Design | Permit | Construction | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Cosmetic** (no permit) | 1–2 weeks | None | 2–6 weeks | **3–8 weeks** |
| **Kitchen/bath remodel** | 2–4 weeks | 3–6 weeks | 6–12 weeks | **3–5 months** |
| **Whole-house remodel** | 4–8 weeks | 4–8 weeks | 12–24 weeks | **5–10 months** |

### Cost Ranges (Bay Area)

| Scope | Cost/SF | Typical Total |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Cosmetic** | $50–$100/SF | $25K–$75K |
| **Kitchen remodel** | $250–$500/SF | $50K–$150K |
| **Bathroom remodel** | $300–$600/SF | $25K–$75K |
| **Whole-house gut** | $200–$400/SF | $200K–$600K |

### What Kills the Project

| Gotcha | What Happens | How to Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Unpermitted prior work** | Inspector finds unpermitted additions from previous owner; must legalize before new permit closes | Pull property records and permit history **before** you buy |
| **Asbestos / lead paint** | Pre-1978 homes require testing; abatement adds $5K–$30K | Budget for testing in any pre-1978 home |
| **Structural surprises** | Open a wall, find dry rot, termites, or inadequate framing | Always budget 15–20% contingency |
| **HOA restrictions** | Exterior changes may need HOA approval | Check CC&Rs before signing any design contract |

### Key Laws

- **Building permit required** for structural, electrical, plumbing, or mechanical work
- **Title 24 energy code** applies to any permitted work (efficient windows, insulation, etc.)
- **No CEQA** — remodels are not "projects" under CEQA
- **No planning approval** — building permit only (unless exterior changes in a historic district)

## Addition (Expanding Your Home)

### What Is It?

You add **new square footage** to an existing house — a new room, second story, bump-out, or garage conversion to living space. The building footprint changes (or gains a new floor).

### When to Use It

- Add a bedroom, family room, or home office
- Build a second story on a single-story house
- Extend the back or side of the house
- Convert a garage to living space (not an ADU — this is part of the primary dwelling)

### Step-by-Step Process

```text
Step 1: ZONING CHECK (1 day)
    Verify your lot can support the addition:
      □ Setbacks: how close to property line can you build?
      □ Lot coverage: maximum % of lot that can be built on
      □ FAR (Floor Area Ratio): maximum total square footage
      □ Height limit: can you go up?
    City planning counter or online zoning lookup
    │
Step 2: DESIGN (4–10 weeks)
    Hire architect
    Structural engineer if 2nd story or significant load changes
    Energy calculations (Title 24)
    │
Step 3: PLANNING REVIEW — if required (4–12 weeks)
    Some cities require "design review" for additions visible from street
    Others are building-permit-only (no planning review)
    Check with your city's planning department
    │
Step 4: BUILDING PERMIT APPLICATION
    Submit architectural plans + structural + Title 24 calcs
    Plan check fees: $2,000–$8,000
    │
Step 5: PLAN CHECK (4–12 weeks)
    Building department reviews structural, electrical, plumbing, mechanical
    Corrections typical — add 2–4 weeks
    │
Step 6: PERMIT ISSUED
    Pay permit fees + school fees + any impact fees
    │
Step 7: CONSTRUCTION (3–9 months)
    Foundation → Framing → Rough MEP → Insulation →
    Drywall → Finish → Final inspection
    │
Step 8: CERTIFICATE OF OCCUPANCY or FINAL SIGN-OFF
    All inspections passed
    Permit closed
    Done.
```

### Timeline

| Scope | Design + Permit | Construction | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Small addition** (200–400 SF) | 2–4 months | 3–5 months | **5–9 months** |
| **Major addition** (400–1,000 SF) | 3–6 months | 5–8 months | **8–14 months** |
| **Second story** | 4–8 months | 6–12 months | **10–20 months** |

### Cost Ranges (Bay Area)

| Type | Cost/SF | Typical Total |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Ground-floor addition** | $400–$700/SF | $150K–$500K |
| **Second-story addition** | $500–$800/SF | $300K–$800K |
| **Garage conversion** (to living space) | $200–$400/SF | $60K–$160K |

### What Kills the Project

| Gotcha | What Happens | How to Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Exceeds FAR or lot coverage** | City denies permit — your lot can't support the addition | Run zoning numbers **first**, before hiring architect |
| **Setback encroachment** | Addition too close to property line; requires variance (6–12 months, $5K+ fees, no guarantee) | Design within setbacks — don't count on getting a variance |
| **Foundation issues** | Existing foundation can't support second story; requires full new foundation ($50K–$150K) | Get a structural engineer assessment before committing to 2nd story |
| **Neighbor opposition** | If design review required, neighbors can object and delay | Pre-notify neighbors; modify design proactively to address concerns |
| **Tree removal** | Protected trees in the building footprint; arborist report + replacement required | Survey trees before designing the addition footprint |

### Key Laws

- **Zoning code**: setbacks, lot coverage, FAR, height limits control what you can build
- **Building code (CBC)**: structural, fire, energy (Title 24) requirements
- **Design review**: some cities require it for visible additions; adds 1–3 months
- **No CEQA** for single-family additions (categorical exemption Class 1 or 3)
- **SB 330 fee-lock**: if filing for a multi-unit addition to an existing property, lock fees early

## Custom Home Build (New Construction)

### What Is It?

Build a **brand-new house** on a vacant lot or tear down an existing house and build new. You start from scratch — design, foundation, framing, everything.

### When to Use It

- Vacant lot you've purchased or own
- Teardown-rebuild (existing house is beyond repair or too small for the lot's potential)
- You want a custom design tailored to your needs

### Step-by-Step Process

```text
Step 1: SITE DUE DILIGENCE (2–4 weeks)
    Before you buy (or before you design):
      □ Zoning: what can you build? (density, height, setbacks, FAR)
      □ Title report: any easements, liens, deed restrictions?
      □ Geotechnical: soil type, bearing capacity, liquefaction risk?
      □ Survey: lot dimensions, topography, existing utilities
      □ Environmental: Phase I ESA if commercial history
      □ Trees: protected trees? Heritage oaks?
      □ Flood zone / fire zone? (FEMA, CAL FIRE maps)
    │
Step 2: DESIGN (8–16 weeks)
    Architect → schematic design → design development → construction documents
    Structural engineer (required)
    Civil engineer (if grading, drainage, or stormwater required)
    Title 24 energy calculations
    │
Step 3: PLANNING REVIEW (4–16 weeks)
    Most cities require planning/design review for new homes
    Some cities (Palo Alto, Los Altos) are notoriously slow
    Others are administrative review only (2–4 weeks)
    │
Step 4: BUILDING PERMIT (6–16 weeks)
    Submit full construction documents
    Plan check: building, structural, fire, MEP, Title 24
    Corrections: typically 1–2 rounds (add 2–6 weeks)
    │
Step 5: PAY FEES
    School fees: $4–$5/SF
    Impact fees: vary by city ($10K–$50K+ for new construction)
    Utility connection fees: $5K–$20K
    Permit fees: $5K–$20K
    │
Step 6: CONSTRUCTION (8–14 months)
    Demo (if teardown) → Grading/foundation → Framing →
    Roof → Windows/doors → Rough MEP → Insulation →
    Drywall → Finish carpentry → Fixtures → Landscaping →
    Final inspections
    │
Step 7: CERTIFICATE OF OCCUPANCY
    All final inspections passed
    Utility connections active
    Move in.
```

### Timeline

| Approach | Due Diligence + Design | Permits | Construction | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Production builder** (pre-designed plans) | 1–2 months | 2–4 months | 8–12 months | **12–18 months** |
| **Custom build** (architect-designed) | 3–6 months | 3–6 months | 10–14 months | **16–26 months** |
| **Teardown-rebuild** | 3–6 months | 3–6 months + demo permit | 10–14 months | **16–26 months** |

### Cost Ranges (Bay Area)

| Quality Level | Cost/SF | 2,500 SF Home |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Standard** | $400–$600/SF | $1.0M–$1.5M |
| **High-end** | $600–$900/SF | $1.5M–$2.25M |
| **Luxury** | $900–$1,200+/SF | $2.25M–$3.0M+ |

*Excludes land cost. Bay Area lots: $500K–$3M+ depending on location.*

### What Kills the Project

| Gotcha | What Happens | How to Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Bad geotech** | Liquefaction, expansive clay, or high water table = $50K–$200K extra foundation cost | **Always** get a geotech report before buying a lot |
| **Hidden easements** | Utility or access easement cuts into buildable area | Get a preliminary title report + ALTA survey **before** closing |
| **Neighbor CEQA challenge** | Neighbor files CEQA objection to your new home (rare but real in Palo Alto) | Use Class 3 or Class 32 categorical exemption; document compliance |
| **Slow city** | Some Bay Area cities take 6–12 months for plan check alone | Research city processing times **before** selecting your lot |
| **Budget overrun** | Custom homes average 15–25% over initial budget | Get a fixed-price contract or a detailed GMP (guaranteed maximum price) |

### Key Laws

- **Zoning code**: controls everything — density, height, setbacks, FAR, parking
- **Building code (CBC)**: structural, fire, energy, accessibility
- **Title 24 2025**: heat pump, EV charging, battery storage (for permits filed 2026+)
- **CEQA**: Class 3 (new construction ≤3 units) or Class 32 (infill) exemption — typically no CEQA issue
- **Chapter 7A**: if in a fire hazard zone, WUI fire-hardening required
- **SB 330**: file preliminary application to lock fees if you're building housing

This cuts design time from 4–6 months to 2–4 weeks and guarantees pricing. If you're doing teardown-rebuild, study the TJH playbook (Appendix F, §F.1).

## ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit)

### What Is It?

A **second smaller home** on your existing residential lot — a backyard cottage, garage conversion, or unit within your existing house. California law guarantees your right to build one. No city can say no.

### When to Use It

- You want rental income from your property
- You need a home for aging parents or adult children
- You want to add property value (an HCD-certified ADU adds $400K–$700K in Bay Area)
- You want to sell the ADU as a separate condo (AB 1033 — if your city has opted in)

### The Four ADU Types

| Type | What It Is | Max Size | Setback | Key Rule |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Detached ADU** | Separate structure in your yard | 1,200 SF | 4 ft side/rear | City must allow at least 800 SF regardless of lot coverage |
| **Attached ADU** | Addition to your existing house | 1,200 SF or 50% of primary | 4 ft side/rear | No replacement parking if garage converted |
| **JADU** | Room within your existing house | 500 SF | None | Efficiency kitchen OK; owner-occupancy required |
| **Garage Conversion** | Convert existing garage to living space | No size limit | No setback required | No replacement parking required |

### Step-by-Step Process

```text
Step 1: CHECK YOUR LOT (1 day)
    Is your lot zoned residential or mixed-use? → You can build an ADU. Period.
    State law guarantees it. No city can deny you.
    │
Step 2: CHOOSE YOUR PATH (1 day)
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  Option A: PRE-APPROVED PLAN (Fastest)      │
    │  Check your city's pre-approved ADU catalog  │
    │  Pick a plan → 30-day permit (AB 1332)      │
    │  Cost: $0–$5K for plans                     │
    ├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
    │  Option B: PREFAB / MODULAR (Fast + Easy)   │
    │  Choose provider: AIAadu, Abodu, Villa, etc. │
    │  Provider handles design + permit + build    │
    │  Cost: $120K–$450K all-in (turnkey)         │
    ├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
    │  Option C: CUSTOM DESIGN (Most Flexible)    │
    │  Hire architect → custom plans              │
    │  60-day permit (state law)                  │
    │  Cost: $8K–$20K design + construction       │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
    │
Step 3: PERMIT (30–60 days)
    Submit plans to building department
    Pre-approved plan: city must act within 30 days
    Custom plan: city must act within 60 days
    If city misses deadline: permit is deemed approved
    │
Step 4: CONSTRUCTION
    Prefab/modular: 1-day install + 2–4 weeks site work
    Stick-built: 3–6 months
    │
Step 5: FINAL INSPECTION + OCCUPANCY
    Inspector signs off
    Start collecting rent (or move in your family)
    │
    Optional Step 6: SELL AS CONDO (AB 1033)
    If your city has adopted AB 1033 (San Jose, San Diego, etc.)
    Convert property to condo → sell ADU separately
```

### Timeline

| Path | Design | Permit | Construction | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-approved plan + prefab** | 0 weeks | 4 weeks | 4–8 weeks | **2–4 months** |
| **Prefab (turnkey provider)** | 0–2 weeks | 4–8 weeks | 8–12 weeks | **3–6 months** |
| **Custom design + stick-built** | 4–8 weeks | 8–12 weeks | 16–24 weeks | **7–12 months** |

### Cost Ranges (Bay Area)

| Type | Cost Range | Typical Monthly Rent |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Garage conversion** | $80K–$180K | $2,500–$3,500 |
| **Prefab ADU (studio)** | $120K–$230K | $2,000–$3,000 |
| **Prefab ADU (1BR)** | $180K–$300K | $2,800–$3,800 |
| **Prefab ADU (2BR)** | $240K–$450K | $3,200–$4,500 |
| **Custom stick-built** | $250K–$500K | $3,000–$4,500 |

### What Kills the Project

| Gotcha | What Happens | How to Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **City says no** | Illegal. State law guarantees your right to build an ADU. | Cite Government Code §65852.2. Report to HCD if city obstructs. |
| **Not HCD-certified (prefab)** | Your ADU is classified as personal property — it depreciates like a car | **Always** verify HCD certification before signing with a prefab provider |
| **Sewer capacity** | Old sewer lateral can't handle second unit; city requires upgrade ($10K–$30K) | Budget for sewer lateral inspection and possible replacement |
| **No site access for crane** | Prefab module can't be craned over house to backyard | Verify crane reach and access with your prefab provider **before** signing |
| **Utility connection delays** | PG&E, water, or sewer connection takes 2–4 months | Apply for utility connections **simultaneously** with permit — don't wait |

### Key Laws

| Law | What It Does For You |
| --- | --- |
| **Gov. Code §65852.2** | Your right to build an ADU — by right, ministerial, no CEQA |
| **AB 1332 / AB 434** | Pre-approved plans + 30-day permit timeline |
| **AB 976** | No owner-occupancy required (permanent) — rent both units |
| **AB 1033** | Sell your ADU as a separate condo (if city opted in) |
| **SB 13** | No impact fees for ADUs under 750 SF |
| **SB 897** | 16–18 ft height for detached ADUs |
| **AB 2533** | Legalize unpermitted ADUs built before 2020 |

## Subdivision (Splitting Land into Lots)

### What Is It?

You take **one large parcel** and divide it into **two or more smaller parcels**, each with its own legal description, APN (Assessor's Parcel Number), and buildable area. Each new lot can then be sold or developed independently.

### When to Use It

- You own a large lot and want to sell off part of it
- You want to build multiple homes on separate lots for individual sale
- You're creating a townhome or small-lot development
- You want to maximize land value by selling individual lots to custom builders

### Subdivision Types

| Type | Lots Created | Approval | Map Required | Typical Timeline |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Lot Line Adjustment** | 0 (just moves the boundary between 2 existing lots) | Administrative | No map | 2–4 months |
| **Parcel Map** | 2–4 new lots | Planning Commission | Parcel map (recorded) | 6–12 months |
| **Tentative Tract Map** | 5+ new lots | Planning Commission + often City Council | Tract map (recorded) | 12–24 months |
| **Vesting Tentative Map** | 5+ new lots | Same as tract map | Same | 12–24 months (but **locks fees** — see Key Laws) |
| **SB 9 Lot Split** | 2 lots from 1 SFR lot | **Ministerial** (no hearing) | Urban lot split map | **2–4 months** |

### Step-by-Step Process (Traditional Parcel/Tract Map)

```text
Step 1: FEASIBILITY (2–4 weeks)
    □ Zoning: does the lot allow subdivision? Minimum lot size?
    □ General Plan: is subdivision consistent with land use designation?
    □ Utilities: can the city serve additional lots? (sewer, water, storm)
    □ Access: can each new lot have legal street access?
    □ Title: any deed restrictions prohibiting subdivision?
    │
Step 2: PRE-APPLICATION MEETING (1–2 weeks)
    Meet with city planning staff
    Discuss lot layout, access, utility requirements
    Get list of required studies and application materials
    │
Step 3: DESIGN TENTATIVE MAP (4–8 weeks)
    Civil engineer prepares tentative map showing:
      □ Proposed lot boundaries
      □ Street access and driveways
      □ Utility locations (sewer, water, storm, electric)
      □ Grading and drainage
      □ Easements
    │
Step 4: CEQA REVIEW (varies)
    ≤4 lots: often categorical exemption (Class 15)
    5+ lots: may need Initial Study → ND/MND
    Large projects: EIR possible (12–24 months)
    SB 684 (≤10 lots on MF zone): CEQA EXEMPT, ministerial
    │
Step 5: APPLICATION + PUBLIC HEARING (4–12 weeks)
    Submit tentative map + CEQA docs + application
    Staff review → staff report → Planning Commission hearing
    Conditions of approval imposed (road improvements, utilities, fees)
    │
Step 6: FINAL MAP (4–8 weeks after approval)
    Licensed surveyor prepares final map
    Record with county recorder
    Each lot gets its own APN
    │
Step 7: IMPROVE + BUILD
    Install required improvements (roads, utilities, sidewalks)
    Pull building permits for each lot
    Build and sell.
```

### Timeline

| Type | Total (Design through Recorded Map) |
| --- | --- |
| **Lot line adjustment** | 2–4 months |
| **Parcel map (2–4 lots)** | 6–12 months |
| **Tract map (5–20 lots)** | 12–24 months |
| **SB 9 lot split** | **2–4 months** (see Section 6 below) |
| **SB 684 (≤10 lots, MF zone)** | **2–4 months** (ministerial, 60-day approval) |

### Cost Ranges

| Item | Cost Range |
| --- | --- |
| **Civil engineer (tentative map)** | $15K–$50K |
| **Licensed surveyor (final map)** | $10K–$30K |
| **Application + filing fees** | $5K–$25K |
| **CEQA (if required)** | $0 (exempt) to $200K+ (EIR) |
| **Required improvements** (roads, utilities) | $50K–$500K+ (highly variable) |
| **Impact fees per lot** | $20K–$100K per lot (Bay Area) |

### What Kills the Project

| Gotcha | What Happens | How to Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Minimum lot size** | City requires 5,000 SF minimum; your lot can only produce 2 lots instead of 3 | Run zoning math **before** buying — lot area ÷ minimum lot size = max lots |
| **CEQA challenge** | Neighbor or activist group files CEQA objection | Use SB 684 (ministerial, no CEQA) if you qualify; or SB 9 for SFR lots |
| **Required improvements** | City conditions approval on $200K in road/utility work you didn't budget for | Get pre-app meeting conditions **in writing** before committing |
| **Utility capacity** | Sewer or water system at capacity; no new connections available | Verify utility capacity with provider **before** application |
| **No access** | New lot is landlocked — no legal street access | Every lot must have frontage on a public street or approved access easement |

### Key Laws

| Law | What It Does |
| --- | --- |
| **Subdivision Map Act** (Gov. Code §66410+) | The master framework — every subdivision follows this law |
| **Vesting Tentative Map** (§66498.1+) | Locks all fees and standards at application date — **critical** for Bay Area projects |
| **SB 9** (§66452.6) | Ministerial lot split on SFR lots — bypasses entire traditional process |
| **SB 684** (§66499.40) | Ministerial approval for ≤10 lots on MF-zoned infill — 60-day approval |
| **SB 330** (§65941.1) | Preliminary application locks fees before you even design the project |
| **CEQA Class 15** | Categorical exemption for minor land divisions (≤4 lots in urban area) |

> **Cross-reference**: See Chapter 7 for the full Subdivision Map Act framework, vesting map strategy, and SB 9 vs. traditional parcel map comparison.

## SB 9 (California HOME Act) — The Fast Track for Single-Family Lots

### What Is It?

SB 9 lets you take **any single-family zoned lot** and create up to **4 housing units** — with **no public hearings, no CEQA, and no discretionary review**. It's the fastest, cheapest way to add density to a single-family lot in California.

### The Two Tools

| Tool | What It Does | Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SB 9 Duplex** | Build 2 units on your existing lot | 1 lot → 2 units |
| **SB 9 Lot Split** | Split your lot into 2 lots | 1 lot → 2 lots |
| **Both Combined** | Split + duplex on each lot | 1 lot → **4 units** |

### Step-by-Step Process

```text
Step 1: CHECK ELIGIBILITY (1 day)
    Your lot must be:
      □ Zoned single-family residential
      □ In an urban area (incorporated city or Census urbanized area)
      □ NOT in: historic district, flood zone, fire hazard severity zone,
        prime farmland, conservation area, or protected habitat
      □ No existing deed restriction preventing it
      □ Not previously split under SB 9
    │
Step 2: DECIDE YOUR STRATEGY
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  Strategy A: DUPLEX ONLY (simplest)             │
    │  Build 2 units on your existing lot             │
    │  No lot split needed                            │
    │  Result: 2 units                                │
    ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
    │  Strategy B: LOT SPLIT ONLY                     │
    │  Split into 2 lots (each ≥40% of original,     │
    │  each ≥1,200 SF)                                │
    │  Build 1 home on each lot                       │
    │  Result: 2 homes on 2 lots (sellable separately)│
    ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
    │  Strategy C: LOT SPLIT + DUPLEX (maximum)       │
    │  Split into 2 lots                              │
    │  Build duplex on each lot                       │
    │  Result: 4 units on 2 lots                      │
    ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
    │  Strategy D: LOT SPLIT + DUPLEX + ADU + JADU    │
    │  (the Sunnyvale 8-unit combo — Ch 4, Case 4)    │
    │  Split → duplex on each → ADU on each →         │
    │  JADU in each duplex                            │
    │  Result: UP TO 8 UNITS                          │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
    │
Step 3: DESIGN (2–6 weeks)
    Architect prepares plans for duplex and/or lot split map
    Minimum setback: 4 ft from side and rear property lines
    City must allow at least 800 SF per unit
    │
Step 4: SUBMIT APPLICATION (1 day)
    Ministerial application — no CUP, no variance, no hearing
    City planning department processes administratively
    │
Step 5: CITY REVIEW (60 days maximum)
    City applies ONLY objective standards — no subjective design review
    City CANNOT reduce density below 2 units per lot
    If city does not act within 60 days → deemed approved
    │
Step 6: PERMIT + BUILD
    Pull building permits
    Construct units
    │
Step 7: OCCUPANCY
    For lot splits: owner-occupancy required for 3 years (one of the units)
    No short-term rental (Airbnb) on any unit — deed restriction required
    After 3 years: owner-occupancy requirement expires
```

### Timeline

| Strategy | Design | Approval | Construction | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Duplex only** | 2–4 weeks | 60 days | 4–8 months | **6–10 months** |
| **Lot split only** | 2–4 weeks | 60 days | N/A (sell lots) | **3–4 months** |
| **Lot split + duplex** | 4–8 weeks | 60 days | 6–10 months | **8–14 months** |
| **Full combo (8 units)** | 6–12 weeks | 60 days (each component) | 8–14 months | **10–18 months** |

### The Math That Makes SB 9 Work

**Example: 8,000 SF lot in Sunnyvale (Ch 4, Case 4)**

| Scenario | Units | Per-Unit Land Cost | Monthly Rent | Annual Income |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Keep as single-family** | 1 | $1.5M (entire lot) | $4,500 | $54K |
| **SB 9 duplex** | 2 | $750K | $3,800 × 2 | $91K |
| **SB 9 split + duplexes** | 4 | $375K | $3,200 × 4 | $154K |
| **Full combo (+ ADU + JADU)** | 8 | $188K | avg $2,500 × 8 | $240K |

The per-unit land cost drops from $1.5M to $188K — an **87.5% reduction**. This is why SB 9 changes the entire pro forma.

### What Kills the Project

| Gotcha | What Happens | How to Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Fire hazard zone** | SB 9 does not apply in VHFHSZ or SHFHSZ | Check CAL FIRE FHSZ maps before planning |
| **Historic district** | SB 9 does not apply to locally designated historic districts | Verify with city planning |
| **City obstruction** | City imposes restrictive "objective" standards that effectively prevent 2 units | Report to HCD — state actively enforces SB 9 (see Woodside, Menlo Park cases) |
| **Owner-occupancy (lot split)** | You must live in one unit for 3 years after lot split | Factor this into your investment timeline; doesn't apply to duplex-only strategy |
| **No STR allowed** | Deed restriction prohibits short-term rental on all SB 9 units | Plan for long-term rental only — no Airbnb |
| **Prior SB 9 on same parcel** | Can only use SB 9 lot split once per original parcel | Check parcel history |

| Law | Citation | What It Does |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SB 9 Duplex** | Gov. Code §65852.21 | 2 units on any SFR lot — ministerial |

## The Beginner's 5-Minute Test: Should I Do This Project?

Before spending a single dollar on design, run this quick sanity check:

### 1. Zoning Check (5 minutes)
Go to your city's GIS/zoning map (usually free online). Look up your parcel. Write down:
- **Zone**: _______________
- **Allowed uses**: _______________
- **Max FAR**: _______________
- **Max lot coverage**: _______________
- **Max height**: _______________
- **Setbacks** (front/side/rear): _______________

If your project doesn't fit within these numbers, you'll need a variance (hard), a zone change (harder), or a different project.

### 2. Budget Check (5 minutes)
Can you afford the **total cost** including:
- [ ] Design fees (architect, engineer, surveyor)
- [ ] Permit fees (city + school + impact)
- [ ] Construction cost (get at least 2 contractor bids)
- [ ] 20% contingency (non-negotiable for beginners)
- [ ] Carrying costs during construction (mortgage, taxes, insurance)
- [ ] Utility connections

### 3. Timeline Check (5 minutes)
Can you handle the **total time** from start to finish? Remember:
- Permits always take longer than you think
- Construction always takes longer than the contractor says
- Add 30% to whatever timeline you're told

### 4. Exit Strategy Check (5 minutes)
How do you make money (or get value) from this project?
- [ ] **Live in it** — value is in quality of life
- [ ] **Rent it** — run the numbers: monthly rent × 12 ÷ total cost = gross yield (target: 8%+)
- [ ] **Sell it** — comparable sales (comps) support your projected sale price?
- [ ] **Refinance** — new appraised value high enough to pull out your investment?

If all four checks pass, proceed to Step 1 of the relevant section above.

## Glossary for Beginners

| Term | Plain English |
| --- | --- |
| **APN** | Assessor's Parcel Number — your lot's ID number with the county |
| **By-right** | The city must approve it if you meet the rules — no vote, no hearing |
| **CBC** | California Building Code — the rules for how buildings are constructed |
| **CEQA** | Environmental review that can add months or years to your project |
| **CUP** | Conditional Use Permit — a special permission from the city (hearing required) |
| **FAR** | Floor Area Ratio — total building SF ÷ lot SF (controls how big you can build) |
| **GMP** | Guaranteed Maximum Price — contractor's max price (overruns are on them) |
| **HCD** | Housing & Community Development — state agency that enforces housing law |
| **LOC / Lot Coverage** | % of lot covered by buildings — city limits this |
| **MEP** | Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing — the guts of a building |
| **Ministerial** | Approved by staff, not by elected officials — fastest pathway |
| **MND** | Mitigated Negative Declaration — a mid-level CEQA review |
| **Phase I ESA** | Environmental Site Assessment — checks for contamination |
| **Plan Check** | City review of your construction plans for code compliance |
| **Setback** | Required distance between your building and the property line |
| **Title 24** | California energy code — requires efficiency, solar, heat pumps, etc. |
| **Variance** | Permission to break a zoning rule — hard to get, don't count on it |
| **Zoning** | The rules that control what you can build on your lot |
