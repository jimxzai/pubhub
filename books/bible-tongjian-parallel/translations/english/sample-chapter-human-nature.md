# Chapter Sample: The Corruption of Power
## David and Bathsheba | Emperor Xuanzong and Yang Guifei

> English Translation Sample
> Theme 1: Seeing Human Nature (透视人性)

---

## Introduction

Power reveals character. When external constraints are removed and a person possesses the ability to do whatever they wish, what emerges from within? This parallel study examines two men at the height of their power—one the greatest king in Israel's history, the other one of China's most celebrated emperors—and how desire led both to devastating falls.

---

## Part One: David and Bathsheba

### Scripture: 2 Samuel 11:1-27

> In the spring, at the time when kings go off to war, David sent Joab out with the king's men and the whole Israelite army. They destroyed the Ammonites and besieged Rabbah. But David remained in Jerusalem.
>
> One evening David got up from his bed and walked around on the roof of the palace. From the roof he saw a woman bathing. The woman was very beautiful, and David sent someone to find out about her. The man said, "She is Bathsheba, the daughter of Eliam and the wife of Uriah the Hittite." Then David sent messengers to get her. She came to him, and he slept with her...
>
> In the morning David wrote a letter to Joab and sent it with Uriah. In it he wrote, "Put Uriah out in front where the fighting is fiercest. Then withdraw from him so he will be struck down and die."

### Historical Context

David's reign marked Israel's golden age. He had united the twelve tribes, conquered Jerusalem, subdued surrounding enemies, and established a prosperous kingdom. He was "a man after God's own heart" (1 Samuel 13:14), the sweet psalmist of Israel, the bearer of God's eternal covenant promise.

Yet at the pinnacle of success, David fell catastrophically. The narrative is stark and unsparing:

1. **Idleness**: "At the time when kings go off to war... David remained in Jerusalem"
2. **Temptation**: "From the roof he saw a woman bathing"
3. **Investigation**: "David sent someone to find out about her"
4. **Sin**: "He slept with her"
5. **Cover-up**: First deception, then murder
6. **Consequence**: "The sword will never depart from your house" (2 Samuel 12:10)

### Key Observations

**The progressive nature of sin**: David's fall was not a single moment but a series of choices. Each small step led to the next, from remaining in Jerusalem when he should have been at war, to glancing at what he should have turned away from, to inquiring about what he should have forgotten, to acting on what he should have resisted.

**The abuse of royal power**: David "sent messengers to get her." The text does not record Bathsheba's consent. Royal power created a situation where refusal would have been nearly impossible. Power was used not to protect but to exploit.

**The desperate cover-up**: When Bathsheba became pregnant, David attempted increasingly desperate measures to hide his sin—bringing Uriah home from war, getting him drunk, and finally arranging his death in battle. One sin led inexorably to another.

**God's confrontation**: The prophet Nathan's parable (2 Samuel 12:1-14) pierced David's conscience. When David pronounced judgment on the rich man in the story, Nathan declared: "You are the man!"

## Part Two: Emperor Xuanzong and Yang Guifei

### Historical Source: Zizhi Tongjian, Tang Dynasty Records

> 玄宗之为太子也，以容貌修美而聪悟绝人，睿宗以为社稷之寄，委以庶政。既即位，英威独断，开元之治，贞观殆将过之。承平既久，渐恣豫乐。李林甫、杨国忠相继用事，纪纲大坏。杨贵妃始以寿王妃入宫，遂专宠，三千宠爱在一身。

**Translation**:

> When Xuanzong was crown prince, he was handsome and remarkably intelligent. Emperor Ruizong considered him the hope of the dynasty and entrusted him with governmental affairs. After ascending the throne, he ruled with decisive authority. The governance of the Kaiyuan era nearly surpassed that of Zhenguan [Emperor Taizong's era]. But after long peace, he gradually indulged in pleasure. Li Linfu and Yang Guozhong successively held power, and the fundamental order was greatly corrupted. Lady Yang, who had originally been the wife of Prince Shou [Xuanzong's own son], entered the palace and thereafter monopolized his affection—"three thousand beloved concubines' favor upon one body."

### Historical Context

Emperor Xuanzong (685-762 AD) ruled the Tang Dynasty for 43 years, the longest reign in Tang history. His early rule, the Kaiyuan era (713-741), is considered one of the high points of Chinese civilization. Under his leadership:

- The empire expanded to its greatest territorial extent
- Poetry and art flourished (Li Bai and Du Fu lived during his reign)
- The economy prospered
- Buddhism reached its peak influence

However, his later reign saw catastrophic decline. Central to this was his obsession with Yang Guifei (719-756), one of the "Four Beauties" of Chinese history.

### The Scandal

The essential facts are shocking even by ancient standards:

1. **Yang Yuhuan (Lady Yang's given name) was originally the wife of Xuanzong's eighteenth son, Prince Li Mao**
2. Xuanzong became infatuated with her after seeing her at the palace
3. He first had her become a Taoist priestess (as a cover for removing her from his son's household)
4. She was then brought into the imperial harem as "Precious Consort" (贵妃)
5. Her family, especially Yang Guozhong, rose to enormous power
6. The resulting corruption contributed to the An Lushan Rebellion (755-763)
7. During flight from the capital, Xuanzong's own guards demanded Yang Guifei's death
8. Xuanzong was forced to order her execution at Mawei Station

### Key Observations

**The corruption of success**: Like David, Xuanzong's fall came after great achievement. The Kaiyuan era's success bred complacency. "After long peace, he gradually indulged in pleasure."

**The violation of fundamental relationships**: Taking his son's wife violated the most basic Confucian norms. This was not merely personal failing but the corruption of the moral order that emperors were supposed to embody.

**The ripple effects**: David's sin brought tragedy to his household. Xuanzong's brought disaster to an empire. The An Lushan Rebellion killed millions and permanently weakened the Tang Dynasty.

**The tragic end**: While David found repentance and restoration (Psalm 51), Xuanzong lived out his days in bitter regret, with the famous poet Bai Juyi memorializing the tragedy in his "Song of Everlasting Regret":

> 七月七日长生殿，夜半无人私语时。
> 在天愿作比翼鸟，在地愿为连理枝。
> 天长地久有时尽，此恨绵绵无绝期。

*On the seventh night of the seventh month, in the Palace of Eternal Life,*
*At midnight, when none was around, we spoke our private vows:*
*"In heaven, may we be birds flying side by side;*
*On earth, may we be trees with branches intertwined."*
*Heaven and earth will someday end,*
*But this regret shall never cease.*

## Comparative Analysis

### Similarities

| Aspect | David | Xuanzong |
|--------|-------|----------|
| **Position** | King at the height of power | Emperor at the height of power |
| **Achievement** | United Israel, established Jerusalem | Longest Tang reign, Kaiyuan prosperity |
| **Trigger** | Visual beauty seen from a distance | Beauty encountered in the palace |
| **Violation** | Took another man's wife | Took his own son's wife |
| **Cover-up** | Murder of Uriah | "Taoist priestess" fiction |
| **Consequence** | Family tragedy, civil war | National disaster, rebellion |
| **Response** | Repentance and restoration | Regret without resolution |

### Differences

| Aspect | David | Xuanzong |
|--------|-------|----------|
| **God's Role** | Confronted by prophet; judged by God | No prophetic voice; historical judgment |
| **Outcome** | Psalm 51; forgiveness; dynasty continues | "Song of Everlasting Regret"; no forgiveness |
| **Theological Framework** | Sin against God primarily (Psalm 51:4) | Violation of cosmic/moral order |
| **Hope** | "Create in me a clean heart" (Psalm 51:10) | "This regret shall never cease" |

## Theological Reflection

### What Both Stories Reveal

**1. Power does not elevate character; it reveals it.**

Both men were genuinely great. David defeated Goliath, united Israel, wrote psalms of profound spirituality. Xuanzong was an accomplished ruler, patron of arts, religious reformer. Yet power exposed what was within. As Lord Acton observed: "Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely."

**2. Sexual sin leads to other sins.**

David's adultery led to deception, manipulation, and murder. Xuanzong's obsession led to neglect of duty, governmental corruption, and national disaster. Sexual sin is never merely "private." It always has ripple effects.

**3. No human being is immune.**

If David—the man after God's own heart—could fall, anyone can. If Xuanzong—one of history's most celebrated rulers—could be brought down by desire, no achievement grants immunity. As Paul wrote: "Let anyone who thinks that he stands take heed lest he fall" (1 Corinthians 10:12).

### Where the Stories Diverge: The Gospel Difference

Here the parallel reaches its most significant divergence:

**Nathan's confrontation**: David had a prophet who spoke God's word to him. "You are the man!" pierced through self-deception. Xuanzong had no such voice—Chinese historiography records and judges but does not confront with divine authority.

**David's response**: "I have sinned against the LORD" (2 Samuel 12:13). David recognized that his sin was ultimately against God, not merely against Uriah or Bathsheba. This theocentric understanding of sin opened the door to genuine repentance.

**God's forgiveness**: "The LORD has taken away your sin" (2 Samuel 12:13). Despite severe temporal consequences, David received genuine forgiveness. His sin, though scarlet, was washed white as snow.

**Psalm 51**: David's response became Scripture—a model of repentance for all believers:

> Have mercy on me, O God, according to your unfailing love;
> according to your great compassion blot out my transgressions...
> Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight...
> Create in me a pure heart, O God, and renew a steadfast spirit within me.

**Xuanzong's tragedy**: By contrast, Xuanzong's story ends in unresolved grief. "This regret shall never cease." Chinese culture could diagnose the problem—the violation of moral order, the corruption of power—but could not provide a solution. Regret is not repentance. Memory is not restoration.

### The Gospel Implication

This comparison illuminates the Gospel's unique gift: **not merely diagnosis but cure; not merely judgment but mercy; not merely regret but restoration**.

The Chinese tradition recognizes human fallibility. It records the falls of even the greatest. It warns against the corruption of power. But it cannot provide what the human soul most desperately needs: forgiveness that cleanses, renewal that transforms, hope that endures.

Only in Christ do we find:
- A King who uses power to serve, not exploit (Mark 10:45)
- A sacrifice that atones for sin once for all (Hebrews 10:12)
- A Spirit who creates clean hearts (Ezekiel 36:26)
- A promise that "this regret" can indeed find its end (Romans 8:1)

## Application Questions

### For Personal Reflection

1. Where in your life might success be breeding dangerous complacency?
2. What "small steps" might be leading you toward greater sin?
3. Who in your life serves as a "Nathan"—someone who can speak hard truth to you?
4. Have you experienced the difference between regret and genuine repentance?

### For Group Discussion

1. Why do you think both David and Xuanzong fell at the height of their success?
2. What structural safeguards might help leaders avoid this pattern?
3. How does the Gospel's offer of forgiveness differ from mere historical judgment?
4. What hope does David's restoration offer to those who have fallen?

### For Apologetic Conversations

1. How might you use Xuanzong's story to illustrate the universality of human sinfulness?
2. What does the contrast between "regret without end" and Psalm 51 reveal about the Gospel?
3. How does this comparison address the objection that Christianity is merely a "Western religion"?
