# Chapter 11: Marriott Modular Program
## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

Marriott International has been the **most aggressive major hotel brand in embracing modular construction**, offering pre-approved volumetric and panelized prototypes, vetted supplier networks, and key money incentives (up to $20K per key in supply-constrained markets) for developers combining CFS expertise with Asian supply chain connections. This chapter decodes the Marriott modular ecosystem: six pre-approved brands optimized for modular delivery (Fairfield Inn: 80–120 keys, highly standardized; Courtyard: 120–180 keys, select-service anchor; SpringHill Suites: 100–150 keys, larger rooms; TownePlace Suites: 90–130 keys, extended-stay; AC Hotels: 150–200 keys, compact lifestyle; Moxy: 150–250 keys, smallest rooms), three approved suppliers (Guerdon Modular: Boise ID, CFS and steel; Champion Commercial Structures: Detroit MI; Oldcastle Surepods: bathroom pods only). Three California case studies validate the model: Fairfield Folsom (97 keys, 4-story CFS, 14-week factory timeline, 3-week module setting, 6-month schedule savings vs. conventional, 3% cost within conventional bid); Courtyard Hawthorne (354 keys, 6-story CFS, largest Marriott modular in US, proved scale viability); Peninsula market gap (no Marriott modular hotel built Peninsula to date—first-mover advantage for franchise-friendly jurisdiction like Redwood City or Sunnyvale). Financial impact: 120-key hotel gains $600K–$900K additional revenue from 3-month earlier opening, $200K–$400K reduced loan interest, $800K–$1.3M total schedule value. **Core principle**: Marriott's modular program is the lowest-risk path to modular hotel development in California. Pre-approved prototypes, established suppliers, and financial incentives remove technology risk. The competitive advantage lies in CFS expertise, Asian supply chain relationships, and Bay Area seismic proficiency.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **Six Marriott brands pre-approved for modular with ready-to-deploy prototypes**: Fairfield Inn (most standardized, highest factory efficiency), Courtyard (largest select-service brand, proven modular track record), AC Hotels (compact, lifestyle-oriented), Moxy (smallest rooms, best modular fit). These brands have modular-specific construction documents already engineered. You're not designing from scratch; you're adapting a proven prototype to your site and local code.

- **Fairfield Folsom case study (97 keys, 4 stories, CFS framing) is California's modular template: 14-week factory, 3-week module setting, 6-month schedule savings, 3% cost within conventional bid**: This is the closest reference project to Bay Area mid-rise modular. Key lesson: CFS is cost-effective for SDC D sites like Folsom, but Bay Area SDC D–E sites may require upgraded connections (countersunk Hollo-Bolts, BRB frames), adding 5–10% cost premium. Use Folsom as baseline; budget 5–10% seismic uplift for Peninsula sites.

- **Guerdon Modular (Boise ID) is closest approved Marriott supplier to California; capacity is limited; 6–9 month lead times typical**: If planning 150+ key Marriott modular hotel, engage Guerdon early—Bay Area projects compete with western US projects for factory slots. For projects >200 keys, consider splitting production between Guerdon and secondary supplier (Champion Detroit), or sourcing CFS components from Asian fabricators to build modules domestically.

- **Key money up to $20K per key in supply-constrained markets reduces equity requirement significantly**: On 120-key hotel, $10K/key key money = $1.2M forgivable loan reducing equity. Marriott most aggressive with key money for AC Hotels and Moxy in Peninsula markets where they want lifestyle brand presence. Negotiate key money at franchise LOI stage; availability varies 10–20x by brand, location, and competitive dynamics.

- **Courtyard Hawthorne (354 keys, 6 stories, largest Marriott modular in US) proved scale viability but revealed capacity constraints**: Hawthorne required precise module sequencing, dedicated 6-story staging area, and additional steel bracing at lower floors to handle lateral loads from upper modules. This is the upper bound of CFS modular scalability. Lesson: for 200+ key projects, split production or explore hot-rolled steel alternatives.

- **Modular schedule savings ($600K–$900K additional revenue from 3-month opening acceleration + $200K–$400K reduced loan interest = $800K–$1.3M total value) is the primary financial driver of Marriott modular ROI**: This is NOT about construction cost savings (modular within 3% of conventional cost). It's about Death Point compression. Model this explicitly in Appendix G Level 2: pro forma with modular timeline vs. conventional timeline; the revenue and interest savings drive the decision, not materials or labor.

- **Peninsula market gap: no Marriott modular hotel built to date (citizenM Menlo Park used their own volumetric system, not Marriott)—first-mover advantage for franchise-friendly jurisdiction**: Being first carries risk but creates reference project that unlocks future deals. Position pilot in franchise-permissive municipality (Redwood City, San Mateo, Sunnyvale) rather than restrictive (Palo Alto). Success on Peninsula modular project generates $5K–$10K/key key money incentive for next 2–3 Peninsula projects.

- **CFS-based modular requires Marriott technical services inspections at key milestones: factory progress, module delivery, on-site connections, pre-opening systems testing**: Marriott's pre-opening protocol is stricter than conventional hotels. Plan 4–6 week pre-opening period for brand training, operating systems installation (PMS/Bonvoy integration), and brand sign-off. Shortcutting brand protocols delays opening.

:::

::: checklist

**Brand Selection & Market Analysis (Months 1–3):**

- [ ] Analyze Peninsula market demand by brand tier (is Courtyard already saturated? Is there white space for AC Hotel or Moxy?)—use Marriott's market analysis tools or third-party STR data
- [ ] Identify target brand (Fairfield, Courtyard, AC, Moxy) based on market positioning and modular fit—Moxy and AC highest modular priority; Fairfield most standardized
- [ ] Assess key money availability for target brand and market: Marriott most aggressive for AC and Moxy in Peninsula; key money for Courtyard typically lower. Negotiate at LOI stage.

**Franchise Application & LOI (Months 3–6):**

- [ ] Schedule meeting with Marriott development team; present modular-specific pitch with reference to Fairfield Folsom (97 keys) and Courtyard Hawthorne (354 keys) case studies
- [ ] Request Marriott pre-approved modular prototype documentation (schematic design, typical floor plan, MEP riser details, structural criteria)—this forms the design development baseline
- [ ] Negotiate franchise LOI with **minimum 36-month term** and key money incentive—typical structure: forgivable loan amortized over franchise term. Validate LOI expiration is 90+ days *after* entitlement completion.
- [ ] Confirm Marriott's modular supplier approval (Guerdon, Champion); if using alternative supplier, obtain Marriott technical approval *before* design development

**Structural & Seismic Engineering (Months 4–8):**

- [ ] Obtain geotechnical report and site-specific Seismic Design Category—if SDC E, budget upgrade from CFS to hot-rolled steel or concrete podium system (5–10% cost premium)
- [ ] Engage structural engineer experienced with modular construction; review Fairfield Folsom structural system (CFS frame, steel strap bracing, light-gauge studs 16", 16" OC) as baseline
- [ ] For Bay Area sites: confirm Hollo-Bolt countersunk connections (not standard bolted brackets) if SDC E—connection engineering must be complete *before* ordering modules
- [ ] Conduct peer review of connection design; ensure dual-performance engineering (stiff for shipping, flexible for seismic). Budget $100K–$300K for seismic-upgraded connections vs. Folsom baseline.

**Permitting & Technical Coordination (Months 6–12):**

- [ ] Coordinate with Marriott technical services during design development—brand approves prototype adaptation, MEP specifications, FF&E standards before permit submission
- [ ] Engage both HCD (state modular inspection) and local building department simultaneously—establish dual-jurisdiction coordination protocol with single points of contact
- [ ] Confirm local municipality has experience permitting modular construction; if not, educate planning/building departments early using Folsom and Hawthorne case studies
- [ ] Secure building permit; submit dual applications (local + HCD/DSA) for factory-built modules; timeline typically 4–12 weeks depending on local jurisdiction

**Modular Supplier & Production (Months 8–15):**

- [ ] Engage Guerdon Modular early if capacity is available; confirm 6–9 month lead time and factory production schedule aligned with site preparation
- [ ] If Guerdon capacity unavailable, negotiate Champion Commercial Structures (Detroit) or alternative supplier with Marriott technical approval
- [ ] Establish design-builder relationship: supplier becomes partner in design development, not post-design commodity. Supplier input on constructability, logistics, quality control is critical.
- [ ] Order CFS components and long-lead items (windows, doors, FF&E) at contract signing; production typically 12–16 weeks from order to module ready for shipment

**Logistics & Site Preparation (Months 12–18):**

- [ ] Plan module import from factory: if Guerdon (Boise), 2–3 week trucking to Peninsula. If Champion (Detroit), 4–5 weeks. If Asian CFS sourced, 6–8 weeks via Port of Oakland
- [ ] Ensure site foundation and utilities ready 30–45 days before first module arrival—staged foundation completion aligns with factory production ramp
- [ ] Arrange tower crane: dedicated crane for 3–4 week assembly period. Typical hotels set 2–4 modules/day at peak rate. Budget crane time and crane crew: $50K–$150K for assembly period
- [ ] Secure module staging area on-site or identify temporary storage yards (East Bay: $0.50–$1.00 SF/month); modules cannot stage >45 days without carrying cost impact

**Pre-Opening & Brand Integration (Months 18–24):**

- [ ] Implement Marriott Bonvoy PMS integration and loyalty program; this must be operational before opening to capture day-one reservations
- [ ] Schedule Marriott technical services inspections at factory (pre-shipment), on-site (post-assembly), and pre-opening (final approval). Plan 4–6 week pre-opening period for brand training
- [ ] Conduct staff training: front desk, housekeeping, revenue management, Bonvoy operations. Marriott training coordinator provides curriculum; typically 4–8 weeks pre-opening
- [ ] Coordinate FF&E delivery and installation: brand-specified furniture, fixtures, art, signage, linens must be ordered 4–6 months before opening; non-compliance delays opening

**Financial Modeling & Risk Management:**

- [ ] Model Death Point (Appendix G §2D) with modular timeline: 12–15 months construction (vs. 18–21 months conventional) at $120K/month carrying cost = $480K–$720K value from schedule compression
- [ ] Calculate early revenue: 3-month opening acceleration at $150–$200 RevPAR on 120 keys = $600K–$900K additional gross room revenue. This is the primary modular ROI driver.
- [ ] Evaluate key money credit in pro forma (Appendix G §2A, Sources section): $10K–$20K/key reduces equity requirement dollar-for-dollar. On $1.2M key money, leverage improves by 20%+ depending on project capital structure.
- [ ] Stress test tariff scenarios if sourcing CFS from China: baseline 7.5% tariff vs. worst-case 25%; on $40K/module cost, 25% tariff = $160K+ swing on 160 modules. Model sensitivity in Appendix G §3.

**Competitive Positioning & Exit:**

- [ ] Position as "first Marriott modular hotel on Peninsula"—this creates marketing advantage and reference project credibility for next 2–3 Peninsula modular deals
- [ ] Plan long-term: franchise term typically 15–25 years; evaluate transfer provisions for exit strategy. Modular construction may increase resale value (newer building stock, lower maintenance) or decrease (perceived as "prefab" by some buyers).
- [ ] Benchmark against conventional Marriott on Peninsula: schedule advantage, cost within 3%, schedule value $800K–$1.3M, revenue uplift $600K–$900K. Use these metrics in franchise application and investor communications.

:::
