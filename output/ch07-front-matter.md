# Chapter 7: Subdivision Map Act
## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

The Subdivision Map Act (Government Code §66410–66499.38) governs land division in California and determines whether you can sell lots individually, how fast you can bring product to market, whether you vest against fee increases, and which state-law ministerial shortcuts bypass local discretionary review entirely. California's 2021–2025 housing legislation has created eight distinct subdivision pathways—each with different unit counts, approval timelines, CEQA exposure, and owner restrictions. Ministerial pathways dominate: SB 9 urban lot splits (2 parcels/4 units ministerial, 60-day approval), SB 684 starter homes (10 units in multifamily zones, ministerial, 60 days), SB 1123 starter homes (10 units in single-family zones on vacant lots, effective July 1, 2025, ministerial, 60 days). Traditional subdivision pathways remain available: parcel maps (2–4 lots, Class 15 CEQA exemption likely, 3–6 months), tentative/final maps (5+ lots, discretionary, 6–18 months), and vesting tentative maps (same as traditional but developer vests rights against standards changes—critical protection against fee increases and code modifications during entitlement). Condominium maps govern airspace subdivision for condo/townhome projects and require DRE Subdivision Public Report (2–4 months) and Davis-Stirling HOA compliance. The Palo Alto Case 1 demonstrates the vesting advantage: a developer who filed a vesting tentative map preserved fees that increased 40% during the 30-month entitlement—saving $480K compared to a non-vesting competitor who absorbed new fees upon approval. The Case 2 Menlo Park comparison (SB 9 lot split 60-day timeline vs. traditional parcel map 12–15 months) illustrates the carrying cost advantage of ministerial pathways: the 12-month timeline compression saved $80K+ in carrying costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance) despite SB 9's owner-occupancy and STR restrictions. The fundamental strategic principle: always file ministerial pathways when available (SB 9, SB 684, SB 1123, or qualifying SB 35/SB 684); always file vesting tentative maps for discretionary subdivisions of 5+ lots; never file a non-vesting map (protection is free; risk of fee increases during entitlement is real and material).

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **Always File Vesting Tentative Map for Any Subdivision of 5+ Lots**: Vesting maps lock in zoning, development, and subdivision ordinance standards at the application date. During entitlement (typically 6–18 months for discretionary review), cities often increase impact fees, adopt new design standards, impose new parking/setback requirements, or downzone. A vesting map protects against all of these. The Palo Alto Case 1 saved $480K when fees increased 40% during 30-month entitlement. The incremental cost of filing vesting vs. non-vesting is zero—it requires only printing the words on the map face. A developer who files non-vesting is rationally irrational. Vesting does NOT protect against impact fees (use SB 330 preliminary application for additional fee lock), but it locks in almost everything else.

- **SB 330 Preliminary Application Creates Double Fee Lock**: File concurrent with vesting tentative map for subdivisions. The preliminary application vests development standards and fees at filing date and provides 180-day window to submit full entitlement application. Separate lock mechanism from vesting map, adding protection against fee escalation—the two combined create comprehensive standard and fee vesting. SB 330 preliminary application cost is minimal ($1K–$2K); protection is potentially hundreds of thousands of dollars.

- **SB 9 Urban Lot Splits (60 Days, Ministerial, Exempt) Are the Fastest Pathway for Small Projects**: Split one SFR lot into two parcels, build duplex on each = 4 units ministerial. 60-day approval deadline (SB 450 effective Jan 1, 2025). Zero discretionary review. Zero CEQA. No public hearings. Carrying cost advantage over 12–15 month traditional parcel map is $80K+ (Case 2 Menlo Park). However: owner must occupy one unit for 3 years (§66411.7(g)(1)), and STR-prohibition deed restriction applies. If you can tolerate owner-occupancy and STR restrictions, SB 9 speed advantage is economically superior in almost all Bay Area contexts.

- **SB 684 (Multifamily Zones) and SB 1123 (Single-Family Vacant Zones, Effective July 1, 2025) Enable 10-Unit Ministerial Subdivisions**: Create 4–10 fee-simple small-lot homes for-sale (not rental) with ministerial approval in 60 days and CEQA exemption. No owner-occupancy requirement. No STR restriction. SB 684 applies to developed or vacant multifamily-zoned lots ≤5 acres. SB 1123 applies to vacant single-family-zoned lots ≤1.5 acres with 66% minimum density threshold. For professional developers (as opposed to homeowners), SB 684/SB 1123 is almost always superior to SB 9 because it has no owner-occupancy restriction and no STR prohibition—enabling true rental or mixed tenure strategies. These pathways close the critical gap between SB 9 (max 4 units) and traditional subdivision (too slow/expensive for small projects).

- **Parcel Map (2–4 Lots) Qualifies for Class 15 CEQA Exemption If All 5 Conditions Met**: Class 15 exemption applies when: (1) GP-consistent, (2) no variances required, (3) services/access available, (4) parcel not divided within prior 2 years, (5) average slope <20%. If all apply, Class 15 is categorical exemption requiring only Initial Study confirmation—avoiding full EIR exposure. Timeline: 3–6 months. CEQA cost: $10K–$15K for environmental screening (vs. $50K–$150K for MND or $200K–$2M for EIR). Proactively document why exceptions to exemption do not apply (cumulative impact, unusual circumstances, scenic highway, hazardous waste, historic resources, sensitive environment).

- **Tentative/Final Map (5+ Lots) for Discretionary Subdivisions; Use Vesting for Fee/Standard Protection**: Standard tentative map: 50-day statutory action timeline (§66452.1), subject to public hearing, discretionary approval. Vesting tentative map: same process + words printed on map face = vested right to development under standards at application date. No rational reason to file non-vesting. Timeline: 6–18 months with discretionary review, MND/EIR, and conditions negotiation. For large subdivisions with infrastructure improvements, this is the standard pathway—but always file vesting variant.

- **Condominium Maps (Airspace Subdivision) Require Davis-Stirling HOA, CC&Rs, and DRE Public Report**: When selling individual units in a multi-unit building or shared-structure townhomes, airspace subdivision is required. This triggers: Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act compliance (reserve study within 12 months, annual budget, SB 326 balcony inspections effective 2025), CC&R recording (covenants, conditions, restrictions governing common areas and assessments), HOA formation with board, and DRE Subdivision Public Report (2–4 months before marketing). Ground-level fee-simple townhomes (each on its own subdivided lot) outsell condo townhomes by $30K–$50K/unit because buyers prefer owning dirt and avoiding HOA assessments. Design for individual lots unless shared infrastructure makes subdivision impossible.

- **Lot Line Adjustments (LLA) Avoid Full Subdivision Review**: Relocate boundaries between ≤4 adjoining parcels with no new parcels created—exempt from full Subdivision Map Act. Useful for adjusting irregular lot lines before development, adding acreage to a parcel to meet density bonus threshold, or resolving encroachment issues. Approval: staff-level, typically ministerial in most cities. Timeline: 1–3 months. Cost: minimal. If you need to configure lots before development, LLA is faster and cheaper than parcel map.

- **Map Expiration and Extension Calendar Management Is Critical**: Tentative maps expire 24–36 months after approval (local ordinance determines initial term). CEQA litigation does not pause the clock. A 36-month map litigated for 18 months leaves only 18 months to complete conditions and record final map. Menlo Park Case 3 illustrates: 45-lot subdivision nearly lost final map because litigation delayed approval until Month 34 of 36-month term. File extension requests proactively at least 6 months before expiration. AB 2729 (2024) provides automatic 18-month extension for maps issued before Jan 1, 2024 and expiring before Dec 31, 2025.

- **Improvement Bonds (50–100% of Estimated Cost) Are Major Capital Requirements**: Before final map approval, post faithful performance bond, labor/materials bond, and (later) warranty bond. For a 20-lot subdivision with $2M public improvements (streets, utilities, drainage, sidewalks, street trees, lighting), bonding capacity of $3M–$4M is required. Smaller developers often lack bonding capacity—this is a real constraint on scaling. Factor bonding costs into pro forma (not just the bond premium, but the capital tied up).

- **Quimby Act Park Land Dedication and School Impact Fees Are Significant Conditions**: Parks: typically 3–5 acres per 1,000 residents (land or in-lieu fee). Schools: Level 1 default ($5.17/SF of residential construction, federal minimum); Level 2/3 higher if district adopts facilities needs analysis (can reach 100% of actual student housing cost). In-lieu fees for inclusionary housing: $20–$75+/SF market-rate residential. These conditions are not negotiable—they're statutory minimums. Model them explicitly in pro forma per unit cost.

:::

::: checklist

**Pre-Acquisition Due Diligence (Before Buying the Land):**

- [ ] Obtain preliminary title report and review for easements, liens, encumbrances that could constrain subdivision design (Appendix E)
- [ ] Request Certificate of Compliance from local agency if legal lot status is ambiguous
- [ ] Confirm General Plan land use designation and zoning support intended density/unit count
- [ ] Screen for SB 9 eligibility (not in fire hazard zone, floodplain, Alquist-Priolo fault zone, FEMA wetlands, habitat, or hazardous waste sites within 100 ft)
- [ ] Screen for SB 684 eligibility (multifamily zoning, ≤5 acres, 75% urban perimeter) or SB 1123 (single-family zone, vacant, ≤1.5 acres)
- [ ] Check subdivision history: has parcel been divided within prior 2 years? (affects Class 15 CEQA exemption)
- [ ] Verify sewer, water, drainage capacity at intended density
- [ ] Run Level 1 Napkin Test (Appendix G) with intended density and holding period

**Pathway Selection (After Understanding Site Constraints):**

- [ ] If 2 lots / 4 units maximum and SB 9 eligible and single-family lot → Use SB 9 lot split (ministerial, 60 days)
- [ ] If 4–10 units, multifamily zone, ≤5 acres, 75% urban perimeter → Use SB 684 (ministerial, 60 days)
- [ ] If 4–10 units, single-family zone, vacant, ≤1.5 acres, effective July 1, 2025 → Use SB 1123 (ministerial, 60 days)
- [ ] If 2–4 lots, not ministerial-eligible → Use Parcel Map (Class 15 CEQA exemption likely, 3–6 months)
- [ ] If 5+ lots / discretionary approval → Use Vesting Tentative Map + SB 330 preliminary application (6–18 months, vests standards and fees)

**Pre-Application (Before Filing):**

- [ ] Conduct pre-application conference with city planning/engineering to confirm pathway and identify likely conditions
- [ ] Commission civil engineer or land surveyor for preliminary subdivision design, grading plan, utility layout
- [ ] Order ALTA survey before committing to subdivision design (identify easements, wetlands, steep slopes)
- [ ] Commission preliminary geotechnical investigation before subdivision design (avoid discovering soil issues after map filing)
- [ ] For ministerial pathways (SB 9, SB 684, SB 1123): confirm ministerial eligibility with city staff before filing—ask for written confirmation
- [ ] For discretionary pathways: file SB 330 preliminary application to vest standards at application date
- [ ] Prepare environmental documentation scope: Class 15 analysis for parcel maps, IS/MND/EIR assessment for tentative maps

**Filing and Entitlement:**

- [ ] For discretionary maps: **Always file Vesting Tentative Map** — protection from standard changes and fee increases is free
- [ ] For discretionary maps: File SB 330 preliminary application concurrent with tentative map filing (180-day window for full application)
- [ ] Submit complete application to avoid restart of statutory clock
- [ ] Respond to agency comments within 2 weeks to maintain entitlement momentum
- [ ] Negotiate conditions of approval aggressively—push back on unreasonable improvement requirements and fee calculations
- [ ] Calendar map expiration date immediately upon approval; file extension request 6 months before expiration
- [ ] For condo projects: begin DRE public report process concurrent with map processing (takes 2–4 months separate from subdivision approval)

**Post-Approval and Final Map Recording:**

- [ ] Prepare and submit final map with all conditions satisfied or bonded
- [ ] Post improvement bonds: performance bond (50–100% of estimated improvement cost), labor/materials bond, warranty bond
- [ ] Obtain city engineer/surveyor certification of final map
- [ ] Record final map with county recorder — this legally creates the new parcels
- [ ] For condos: record CC&Rs and condominium plan; obtain DRE public report before marketing
- [ ] Pay or secure all impact fees: school fees (Level 1 minimum $5.17/SF), park fees (Quimby 3–5 ac per 1,000 res), traffic fees, inclusionary in-lieu fees, etc.
- [ ] Release improvement bonds only after city acceptance of public improvements
- [ ] Maintain warranty/maintenance bond for 1 year after improvement acceptance

**Condominium-Specific (If Applicable):**

- [ ] File airspace subdivision as condominium map with three-dimensional legal description of each unit
- [ ] Prepare and record CC&Rs before units can be sold (identify common areas, assessment obligations, HOA structure)
- [ ] Form HOA and provide homeowner disclosure packages (required by Davis-Stirling Act)
- [ ] Commission reserve study within 12 months of HOA formation
- [ ] Prepare DRE Subdivision Public Report (Preliminary, Conditional, then Final) — 2–4 month timeline
- [ ] Ensure Davis-Stirling compliance: annual budget, insurance, SB 326 balcony inspections
- [ ] Disclosure: market units with understanding that HOA fees reduce buyer appeal by $30K–$50K/unit vs. fee-simple

:::
