# Chapter 14: Civil Engineering, Stormwater & Environmental Site Assessment
## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

Civil engineering, stormwater management, and environmental assessment are three interconnected disciplines that directly impact site design, construction cost, and regulatory compliance—yet are frequently underestimated in pro forma budgeting. This chapter decodes Bay Area stormwater (C.3) requirements that consume 5–10% of buildable site area, plus the environmental screening protocols that catch contamination liabilities early. Grading plans establish cut/fill balance, drainage adequacy, and retaining wall requirements ($50–$150/SF per wall face). More critically, Provision C.3 of the Bay Area Municipal Regional Permit mandates stormwater treatment for projects creating 10,000+ SF of impervious surface—bioretention areas that must sit 10 feet minimum from building foundations, often eating 8–15% of site area on typical projects. Hotels with large parking footprints and restaurant grease systems face additional requirements (grease interceptors, oil/water separators, parking area containment). Phase I ESAs ($3K–$6K) identify Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs) requiring Phase II investigation ($15K–$100K); adjacent property contamination (within 1,000 ft) must be evaluated for vapor intrusion risk. Menlo Park case study: C.3 stormwater consumed 12% of site area, forcing redesign of building footprint and loss of 1 unit + 8 parking spaces. Palo Alto case: SWPPP violations resulted in $45K fine + 2-week stop-work order + mandatory third-party monitoring. San Carlos Hotel Indigo: $150K environmental assessment suite (Phase I, Phase II, vapor intrusion, hazmat surveys) uncovered nearby tetrachloroethylene plume requiring precautionary vapor mitigation. **Core principle**: design stormwater management and environmental constraints before finalizing building footprint, not after.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **C.3 stormwater is the silent site-plan killer**: Budget 5–10% of site area for bioretention or other treatment measures. Bioretention requires 10-foot setbacks from building foundations, meaning usable building envelope shrinks by that setback distance per side. Menlo Park case: 2-acre site lost 1 unit to stormwater compliance—equivalent to $600K revenue reduction. Design stormwater early; don't treat it as post-design obligation.

- **Stormwater treatment options create different site planning impacts**: Bioretention is most common (planted, filters runoff); pervious paving reduces impervious calculation (parking, driveways); green roofs reduce impervious total while adding structural load. Evaluate each option's site footprint impact early in massing studies—choice of treatment directly affects buildable area and revenue.

- **Hotels require specialized stormwater controls beyond standard C.3**: Grease interceptors for food service (required by law); oil/water separators for parking areas; fire access lane maintenance; loading dock containment; and emergency generator fuel secondary containment. Budget these as part of landscape/site utility design, not afterthought. Environmental health departments inspect these regularly.

- **SWPPP compliance is actively enforced by Regional Water Quality Control Board**: Phase I ESAs ($3K–$6K) are mandatory on all sites. Projects disturbing 1+ acre require SWPPP under NPDES General Construction Permit—not optional. Palo Alto case: sediment-laden runoff to creek = $45K fine + stop-work + mandatory third-party monitoring = $120K total cost. Budget professional SWPPP preparation and qualified SWPPP practitioners (QSPs); prevention ($15K–$30K) is fraction of violation cost.

- **Phase I ESA findings categorize contamination risk**: REC (Recognized Environmental Condition) requires Phase II investigation; CREC (Controlled REC) with institutional controls may proceed; HREC (Historical REC) generally requires documentation only; De Minimis conditions need only note in file. San Carlos Hotel Indigo: nearby tetrachloroethylene plume triggered Phase II and vapor intrusion mitigation (precautionary but required by EIR).

- **Adjacent property contamination within 1,000 ft must be evaluated**: Check DTSC EnviroStor and SWRCB GeoTracker for sites within 1,000 ft (not just on your parcel). Groundwater plumes migrate; vapor intrusion can occur even if contamination is off-site. Budget Phase II and vapor intrusion analysis if upgradient contamination identified.

- **Grading cost overruns are endemic in Bay Area development**: Civil engineers often underestimate earthwork costs by 25–40%. Rock layers at unexpected depths, utility conflicts, and environmental constraints surface during construction. San Jose subdivision case: geotech estimate $85K, actual $125K due to rock layer at 4 feet. Budget grading at 1.4x civil engineer estimate; require test pits (not just borings) to 8 feet minimum.

- **Environmental assessment costs are NOT just Phase I—plan for full suite**: Phase I ($3K–$6K) + Phase II if triggered ($15K–$100K) + vapor intrusion ($5K–$50K) + asbestos/lead surveys if demolition ($10K–$50K). San Carlos Hotel Indigo case: $150K total environmental assessment before EIR costs. Include environmental contingency (5–10% of soft costs) for surprises.

:::

::: checklist

**Grading & Drainage:**

- [ ] Review grading plan for earthwork balance: cut/fill quantities, import/export needs and costs ($15–$40/CY)
- [ ] Verify positive drainage away from all structures; identify low spots that may pond water
- [ ] Assess retaining wall requirements and costs ($50–$150/SF wall face); note wall heights and locations
- [ ] Check ADA compliance for paths of travel (max 5% running slope, 2% cross slope)
- [ ] For modular/ICF projects: assess subgrade conditions and bearing capacity (from geotech report)

**Stormwater (C.3) Requirements:**

- [ ] Determine if project triggers C.3 (10,000+ SF impervious surface created/replaced)
- [ ] If C.3 triggered: budget 5–10% of site area for stormwater treatment (bioretention, permeable paving, green roof, detention)
- [ ] Design treatment measures compatible with site layout; factor 10-foot foundation setbacks for bioretention into building footprint
- [ ] Evaluate pervious paving strategy to reduce impervious surface calculation and recover buildable area
- [ ] Calculate stormwater management plan (ongoing maintenance obligations, property owner responsibilities)
- [ ] For hotels: specify grease interceptors (food service), oil/water separators (parking), loading dock containment, emergency generator secondary containment

**SWPPP & NPDES Compliance:**

- [ ] Determine if project disturbs 1+ acre (triggers SWPPP requirement)
- [ ] If SWPPP required: budget professional SWPPP preparation ($3K–$8K) and Qualified SWPPP Practitioner (QSP) management ($15K–$30K)
- [ ] Plan for SWPPP filing through State Water Board SMARTS system
- [ ] Designate Legally Responsible Person (LRP) for SWPPP compliance
- [ ] Develop erosion and sediment control BMPs appropriate to site risk level (Level 1, 2, or 3)
- [ ] For near-creek/sensitive-water sites: budget enhanced BMPs and continuous monitoring (more expensive but enforcement risk is high)

**Phase I ESA & Environmental Screening:**

- [ ] Complete Phase I ESA before closing ($3K–$6K); mandatory due diligence
- [ ] Review all Phase I findings: categorize as REC, CREC, HREC, or De Minimis
- [ ] Check DTSC EnviroStor database for contamination sites (on-site and within 1,000 ft)
- [ ] Check SWRCB GeoTracker database for groundwater contamination (may be upgradient, affecting vapor intrusion)
- [ ] For any REC identified: budget Phase II investigation ($15K–$100K depending on scope)
- [ ] For RECs with potential vapor intrusion: budget soil vapor testing and mitigation if positive ($5K–$50K)

**Phase II ESA (if Phase I triggers):**

- [ ] If Phase I identified RECs, engage Phase II consultant: soil/groundwater sampling, laboratory analysis, fate/transport modeling
- [ ] Budget Phase II at $15K–$100K depending on site complexity and number of test pits/monitoring wells
- [ ] Timeline: 4–8 weeks for field work and reporting
- [ ] Compare Phase II results against regulatory screening levels (EPA/DTSC standards)
- [ ] If contamination confirmed: develop remediation plan (cost $50K–$500K+); may require additional Phase III assessment

**Site-Specific Considerations:**

- [ ] For South Bay sites near creeks (Palo Alto, San Jose): evaluate Endangered Species Act compliance if in protected habitat
- [ ] For Bay-adjacent sites (Menlo Park): liquefaction risk requires coordination with geotech (Chapter 13) + stormwater (elevated design)
- [ ] For sites with basements: coordinate drainage design with geotech water table analysis; plan dewatering if water table shallow
- [ ] For modular projects with heavy point loads: verify grading plan accommodates foundation requirements from Chapter 12

**Cost Budgeting & Timeline:**

- [ ] C.3 stormwater compliance: budget $50K–$200K total (design + construction) plus ongoing maintenance obligations
- [ ] SWPPP preparation + monitoring: $15K–$30K for project duration
- [ ] Phase I ESA: $3K–$6K (mandatory before closing)
- [ ] Phase II ESA (if triggered): $15K–$100K; adds 4–8 weeks to timeline
- [ ] Grading: budget civil engineer estimate × 1.4 for actual cost; require test pits before finalizing estimate
- [ ] Environmental contingency: 5–10% of soft costs for surprises

:::
