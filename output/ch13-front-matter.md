# Chapter 13: Title, Survey & Geotechnical Engineering
## Executive Summary, Key Takeaways, and Action Checklist

---

::: execsummary

Title, survey, and geotechnical investigations form the deal-evaluation trifecta that separates competent developers from those blindsided by hidden liabilities. This chapter teaches you to read and interpret each discipline critically, catching site constraints before capital is committed. Title reports reveal encumbrances and CC&Rs that may fundamentally constrain development potential; surveys physically map those constraints onto your site plan; geotechnical reports quantify subsurface conditions and foundation costs. The Bay Area presents specific challenges: alluvial soils in Santa Clara Valley with liquefaction hazards requiring $8K–$15K/unit for ground improvement and enhanced foundations; high water tables near creeks triggering $200K–$500K in dewatering and waterproofing; expansive clays in South Bay requiring post-tensioned slabs and $6K–$15K/unit foundation premiums. Real case studies—Menlo Park's $1.4M liquefaction cost shock, Palo Alto's 14-month easement negotiation, San Jose's expansive clay surprises—demonstrate that skipping due diligence costs exponentially more than the $3K–$6K Phase I ESA. ALTA surveys with appropriate Table A items catch easement conflicts that title reports miss. Geotechnical investigations revealing liquefaction risk, high water tables, or expansive soils should directly drive design decisions (elevated podiums, ground improvement, alternative foundations), not be discovered during plan check. **Core principle**: treat due diligence findings as design inputs, not obstacles to navigate around.

:::

---

::: keytakeaways

- **Read title reports and surveys together, never separately**: Title reports describe easements by document reference; ALTA surveys show where they actually sit on the ground. Palo Alto case study: 10-ft PG&E easement in title became 15-ft diagonal easement on survey, cutting buildable envelope 18%. Always cross-reference with Table A Items 6, 11, 17 before closing.

- **Bay Area liquefaction risk is non-negotiable in Menlo Park and San Jose**: CGS Seismic Hazard Zone maps identify mapped liquefaction zones in Santa Clara Valley alluvial soils. Budget $8K–$15K/unit for ground improvement (stone columns, jet grouting) and enhanced foundations (mat slabs, lateral spreading design). Menlo Park case: $1.4M additional cost (~$12K/unit) discovered post-closing due to skipped GIS screening.

- **Geotechnical bearing capacity directly controls foundation economics**: Allowable bearing pressure of 1,500+ psf allows spread footings; less than 1,500 psf or highly variable conditions trigger deep foundations (piles/piers), adding $3K–$8K per unit. Liquefaction factor of safety (FOS) less than 1.2 requires mitigation. Settle these issues in geotech phase, not at foundation design.

- **High water table demands waterproofing and dewatering planning**: Santa Clara Valley near creeks and wetlands may have seasonal high water tables within 10 feet of grade. Budget $3K–$8K/unit for waterproofing; $100K–$300K for temporary dewatering systems if basements required. ICF basement projects especially vulnerable—plan dewatering permits 6 months in advance.

- **Expansive clays require post-tensioned slabs and overexcavation**: South Bay (Willow Glen, etc.) frequently has CH-classification expansive clays (PI > 40). Budget post-tensioned slab foundations ($4K–$6K/unit premium) plus 18-inch overexcavation with imported non-expansive backfill (adds $5K–$10K/unit site work). San Jose case: $6K/unit foundation cost + 25% grading cost increase from expansive soil.

- **Phase I ESA is mandatory before closing; Phase II cost is controllable after REC identification**: Phase I costs $3K–$6K and is non-negotiable—a $5K Phase I can save $5M remediation liability. If Phase I identifies RECs, budget Phase II at $15K–$100K for soil/groundwater sampling and testing. Check DTSC EnviroStor and SWRCB GeoTracker for adjacent contamination within 1,000 ft (not just on-site).

- **CREC (Controlled REC) or HREC (Historical REC) with engineering controls may still allow development**: Former gas station with institutional controls (deed notice, engineering assessment) may proceed; requires documenting that controls remain effective and factoring ongoing monitoring into operating budget. Distinguish from new REC requiring Phase II investigation and possible remediation.

- **Geotechnical findings drive site plan design decisions**: Liquefaction + flood zone in Menlo Park resulted in elevated podium design—a direct design consequence of geotechnical + survey findings. Don't treat geotech as a constraint to overcome; treat it as a design parameter that shapes your building footprint, foundation type, and cost structure.

:::

---

::: checklist

**Pre-Acquisition Due Diligence:**

- [ ] Run GIS site analysis (§15.5–15.6 protocol): Check for liquefaction zones, flood zones, fault zones, contamination
- [ ] If in Bay Area liquefaction zone or near creek: budget $8K–$15K/unit for ground improvement + enhanced foundations
- [ ] Check CGS Seismic Hazard Zone maps (Alquist-Priolo, liquefaction, landslide) before making offer
- [ ] Review DTSC EnviroStor and SWRCB GeoTracker for contamination within 1,000 ft radius
- [ ] For South Bay sites: research soil types (alluvial, clay, fill) and anticipate potential expansive soil conditions

**Title Analysis:**

- [ ] Request and review preliminary title report; categorize every Schedule B exception
- [ ] Flag CC&Rs that restrict density, height, use type, or commercial operations
- [ ] Identify all easements (utility, access, drainage, conservation); note their location on property
- [ ] For hotels: verify no restrictions on commercial use, 24/7 operations, or alcohol service
- [ ] Confirm all liens are disclosed and plan to clear or subordinate before close (request payoff amounts)

**Survey Ordering & Verification:**

- [ ] Order ALTA/NSPS survey with Table A Items: 1 (monuments), 3 (flood zone), 4 (area), 6 (setback lines), 8 (substantial features), 11 (utilities), 13 (adjoining owners), 16 (access), 17 (off-site easements), 19 (topo)
- [ ] Require surveyor to plot all title exceptions onto the survey map
- [ ] Cross-reference survey with assessor records: verify parcel boundaries match and lot size matches title
- [ ] Verify setback lines and buildable envelope against zoning requirements
- [ ] Confirm utility easement locations and clearances to planned building footprint

**Geotechnical Investigation:**

- [ ] Engage geotechnical engineer before finalizing pro forma assumptions
- [ ] Require soil profile analysis to 8 ft minimum depth (boring or test pits); 12 ft if deep foundations likely
- [ ] For Bay Area sites: specifically request liquefaction evaluation with factor of safety calculations
- [ ] Assess groundwater depth and seasonal variation; request monitoring well data if available
- [ ] For sites with basements or underbuilding: request waterproofing and dewatering recommendations
- [ ] Request bearing capacity and foundation type recommendations (spread footings vs. deep foundations)
- [ ] For modular projects: confirm bearing capacity supports concentrated loads at module bearing points

**Phase I ESA and Environmental Screening:**

- [ ] Complete Phase I ESA before closing (cost: $3K–$6K); non-negotiable
- [ ] Review Phase I findings for RECs, CRECs, and HRECs; understand remediation timeline for each
- [ ] If REC identified: budget Phase II ESA at $15K–$100K depending on scope
- [ ] Check for vapor intrusion risk (especially near VOC contamination); budget mitigation ($5K–$50K) if flagged
- [ ] For former commercial/industrial sites: budget asbestos and lead paint surveys before demolition

**Cost Impact Assessment:**

- [ ] Liquefaction or high water table identified: add $8K–$15K/unit to pro forma hard costs
- [ ] Expansive clay: add $5K–$10K/unit for enhanced foundations + grading impact
- [ ] REC with Phase II needed: add $15K–$100K soft costs + potential remediation (verify with engineer)
- [ ] Easement conflicts: model redline costs or negotiation timeline (could delay approval 6–18 months)
- [ ] Flood zone or seismic constraints: incorporate into site plan and verify design accommodates

**Title Clearance & Closing Coordination:**

- [ ] Ensure all liens are cleared or subordinated before closing
- [ ] Obtain title insurance commitment; verify exceptions are acceptable
- [ ] For easements that may impact development: consider easement relocation or recordation agreement
- [ ] Confirm access is legally recorded and not subject to termination by property owner
- [ ] Record all relevant survey information and geotechnical limitations in project file

:::
